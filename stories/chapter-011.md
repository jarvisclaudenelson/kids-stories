# Chapter 11: The Chord

Rafe slept for fourteen hours.

Not unusual, Sable said. After months of habitat sleep — too many people, recycled air, the constant low-grade tension of waiting for the Shadow Hand — a real bed in a real room could knock you out like a light switch.

He was set up in the cabin next to theirs. When he finally emerged, he stood in the doorway of the Nelson cabin wearing borrowed clothes slightly too big for him and holding his fragment very loosely, like a habit.

"Is there food?" he said.

"Always," Ezra said.

---

The first real meal together happened at the long table in the main hall. Dr. Baas's survey team had been absorbed into the colony with reasonable efficiency — Monscollis was experienced in absorbing people, having done it once with the Mayflower survivors. Half the team was already volunteering for colony work. Two of them had found the kitchen and wouldn't leave.

Rafe sat between Ezra and Sable. Julian sat across from him with the training notebook, which he was clearly trying not to open.

He lasted about four minutes.

"Can I ask you something?" Julian said.

Rafe glanced at the notebook. "You're going to anyway."

"Did your fragment do anything in the first month? Like signal-wise, contact-wise? Did you have — did you feel other fragments before you found us?"

Rafe thought. "No signals. But it was warm all the time. Even on Bryn Dural, which is cold. And once, maybe two months in, it pulsed differently — one long beat. Didn't repeat."

Julian was writing. "Timestamp?"

"I didn't have a notebook."

Julian looked briefly pained.

"I do now," Rafe said. "I'll start one."

That helped.

---

Declan had decided Rafe was interesting and there was nothing anyone could do about it.

He showed up at Rafe's elbow during the meal with a piece of toast and held it up.

"Toast," Declan said, with the gravity of someone making an offer.

Rafe looked at the toast. Looked at Declan. Took it.

"Thanks," he said.

Declan nodded, satisfied. He patted Rafe's knee twice — the universal Declan signal for *you're okay, I've checked* — and wandered off to assess the rest of the table.

Sable watched this. "He does that to everyone."

"Does it always work?"

"Every time."

Rafe ate the toast. He looked a little like someone who had been very alone for a long time and had just received something very small and very good.

---

Training that afternoon.

The clearing north of the colony. Same three fallen trees covered in pale blue-green moss, same alien pines, same sense of quiet. Ezra noticed it differently now — a place he'd practiced alone, then with Sable watching, then with Julian keeping notes, and now with Rafe sitting on one of the fallen trees watching the way you watch something you're about to try.

Sable had Ezra run through the extension sequence from the beginning. Not for Rafe's benefit, she said — though it was for Rafe's benefit — but because you should run the whole sequence periodically to make sure nothing's drifted.

"What's drifted?" Julian asked.

"Sometimes you develop compensations without noticing. You get a result but the form underneath is wrong. Looks fine until you push past a certain point and then everything falls apart." She looked at Ezra. "From your center."

Ezra planted his feet. He thought of Declan patting Rafe's knee twice. He extended.

Eight feet, steady. Nine feet, steady. Ten feet, and the tingling was just starting.

He held it for forty-five seconds, let it in, brought it back.

"Good," Sable said. Not lavishly. Just *good.*

Rafe's eyes tracked the whole thing. "What does it feel like? The extending."

"Like taking up space you already own," Ezra said. That was Sable's phrase, but it was the right one.

---

Rafe's power was different.

Not elasticity — something else. He'd been holding the fragment for seven months without anyone to train with, so most of what he knew was accidental, or defensive, or happened when he wasn't paying attention.

"Show me what you've done," Sable said. "Not what you think you should do. What you've actually done."

Rafe was quiet for a moment. Then he held out his hand, palm up, fragment stone on his palm. The amber-orange warmed — and then the stone lifted, maybe three inches, and rotated slowly, and came back down.

"That's what I figured out," he said. "Small things. I can move them. Not fast, not heavy. And only if I'm really paying attention."

Julian, who had been writing in his notebook, stopped writing.

"That's telekinesis," Julian said.

"I didn't know what to call it."

"It's telekinesis. Ezra's elasticity is internal — his body. Yours is external — objects."

Rafe looked at the amber stone sitting still in his palm. "Can that be useful?"

"Can it be—" Julian made a sound. "Yes. Yes, it can be useful."

Sable looked at the fragment, then at Rafe's face. "How far?"

"I don't know. I never tried far."

"Good," she said. "That's where we start."

---

The training notebook now had two sections.

Julian didn't announce this. Ezra just noticed it one evening — Ezra's section had the divider tab *Ez* in the corner, and now there was a new section behind it labeled *R* in careful block letters.

He didn't say anything about it. But later he heard Julian explaining to Rafe, very seriously, that the observation window for first-extension attempts was typically six to twelve tries before a plateau and it was important to document baseline before adjusting form.

Rafe listened to all of this.

"Do you actually know that?" Rafe said. "Or are you guessing?"

Julian paused. "Based on Ezra's training log, yes. Which is a sample size of one. But still."

"So you're guessing."

"I'm extrapolating."

Rafe looked at the notebook. "What's the difference?"

"Extrapolating has math behind it."

Rafe nodded slowly, like he was choosing to accept this.

"Okay," he said.

Julian uncapped his pen and moved to the next page, clearly satisfied. Ezra watched from across the room and said nothing, but his star gave a quiet, warm pulse, the way it sometimes did when something was right.

---

That evening, Captain Okonkwo came to the cabin.

She brought two things: a mug of coffee (hers), and news.

"Fletcher's been found," she said, without preamble. She sat down at the table. The kids arranged themselves in the way they had started doing automatically — Sable against the wall, Julian where he could see the door, Ezra in the middle. Rafe took the last chair. "He was at the secondary relay station on the south ridge. Alone. He'd been there four days."

"Alive?" Marcus asked.

"Alive. Not hurt. But—" She paused, and her particular kind of pause meant she was choosing words carefully. "He wanted to come in. He signaled the colony radio himself. He's been talking for three hours."

The room was quiet.

"What's he saying?" Sable said.

"That he was recruited by the Shadow Hand nine years ago. That he spent most of those nine years in various colony postings gathering information. That six months ago they told him the plan — the full plan — and he got scared." Okonkwo looked at her mug. "He says they're not trying to take the Covenant fragments to use them. He says they're trying to break them."

Ezra's star went cold and still in his chest. Not the cold-dark of a threat nearby. Something quieter. Wrong.

"Break them," Sable said. She wasn't asking.

"He didn't understand the mechanism. But the word he used was *shatter.* Said the Shadow Hand's theory is that if the Covenant is fragmented, it can't be used. And if it can't be used, no one can use it. The current holders can't fix anything, can't do the thing the fragments were made to do. It stops. Permanently."

The amber fragment on Rafe's cord pulsed once. Hard.

"Why?" Julian said.

"Fear," Okonkwo said simply. "They're afraid of what it could do. Whatever it could do that they don't control."

Ezra thought about the fragment in his chest. The warmth. The pulse when Declan patted someone's knee. The sound that had come out of holding all three fragments together at once, the chord that had filled the air in a way he hadn't had words for. He thought about the survey team on Bryn Dural, alive because a rockslide cut one particular antenna at one particular hour.

"That's not—" He stopped. Started again. "If they shatter the fragments, what happens to the people holding them?"

Okonkwo was very still. "He doesn't know. He said he didn't ask. That's part of why he got scared."

Rafe was looking at his fragment. His expression was that of someone who had spent seven months alone with something precious and was now considering what that meant from a different angle.

Julian's pen had not moved for the last two minutes.

---

After Okonkwo left, they sat with it.

Ollie came in from wherever he'd been and settled near Ezra's feet. He did not alert — just settled. This was not a danger-now problem. This was a bigger problem. He seemed to understand the difference.

"So we have a deadline," Julian said. He said it matter-of-factly, which was how he handled things he found frightening.

"We've had one since Chapter 3," Sable said.

"We didn't know how specific it was."

"It's more specific now," Rafe said. "That's not the same as worse."

Julian looked at him.

"Knowing what they want to do to the fragments is information," Rafe said. "Information helps. That's better than not knowing."

Julian considered this. Wrote something. Nodded, once.

Ezra looked at Sable. "What does the star chart say about fragments six and seven?"

Sable pulled the rolled chart from her pack. She spread it on the table. The yellow marks were where they'd always been — three remaining signatures, two of which had led them here, one still unknown.

"This one," she said, pointing to the third yellow mark. "The signal was stronger when I got close to Monscollis. It's not at a star system I recognize. But it's closer than Bryn Dural. Maybe four light-years."

"And six and seven?"

"I don't know if those two count as signals yet. They may be dormant. Or they may have no holder — just the fragment, in rock or ground, waiting."

"Like yours was at Vela Station," Rafe said.

"Like mine was."

Declan came padding in from the bedroom in his pajamas, carrying his carved Ollie figure. He looked at everyone around the table.

"Too loud," he said.

The room was completely silent.

He looked around at the silence. Seemed to decide the situation had been handled.

"Okay," he said, and padded back to bed.

Sable looked at the empty doorway. Then at the table. Then she made a sound that was, unmistakably, a laugh — short, surprised, genuine.

It broke something loose. Julian made a sound too. Even Rafe's shoulders came down two inches.

---

The star in Ezra's chest was steady.

Not happy, exactly. Not afraid. Something between: *paying attention.*

There were seven fragments, and they had three. The Shadow Hand had two and wanted to shatter them all. Somewhere in the dark between star systems, two more signals were out there — maybe dormant, maybe someone already walking around not knowing what they were carrying.

And somewhere, probably, someone who was very tired and cold, like Rafe had been. Waiting for a signal. Waiting to know they weren't alone.

He looked at his own hand in the dark.

The star pulsed. Warm. Steady. Present.

Tomorrow Sable would get out the chart again. Julian would run the numbers. Rafe would practice moving small things across the clearing and probably eventually large things, and Julian would document all of it. Okonkwo would keep asking Fletcher questions. Marcus would figure out where Voss and Crane were.

And Declan would give toast to the right people at the right time, which was also, Ezra thought, a kind of power.

He pressed his hand to his chest, felt the star say *I'm here,* and went to sleep.
