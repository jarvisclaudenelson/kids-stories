# Chapter 15: The Scattered Signal

The morning started with a disagreement about maps.

Julian had been up since before anyone else — or at least, that's what Ezra assumed from the three papers taped to the kitchen table and the fact that his notebook had crumbs in it already at six-thirty in the morning.

"The signal has to be directional," Julian said, not looking up when Ezra walked in. "Everything Sable said about the fragments is directional. They have a pull. They pulse *toward* other fragments. So if we triangulate from Monscollis and from Sable's Vela Station coordinates and from Rafe's Bryn Dural coordinates—"

"Morning to you too," Ezra said.

"—then we can at least eliminate a significant portion of the sky." Julian finally looked up. "Do you have the Vela Station coordinates? The actual coordinates, not the colloquial name."

"I just woke up."

"Sable has them."

"Sable is also asleep."

Julian considered this. "What time does she get up?"

"I don't know. Normal time."

Julian wrote something down. He seemed to interpret this as useful data.

Declan appeared in the doorway in his pajamas, hair going in four directions, holding a stuffed something that might once have been a rabbit.

"Brefkist," he said. Not a question.

"Yes," Ezra said. "Breakfast."

"Me too," Declan said, and walked to his chair and sat down and waited. This was his entire contribution to the breakfast process. He felt it was sufficient.

Ezra made toast.

---

Sable came in twenty minutes later, and to Julian's visible relief, she did have the Vela Station coordinates — or at least, a relative bearing from Monscollis, which Julian apparently found adequate.

She sat across from his papers, looked at them for a moment, and pointed at one of his angles.

"This assumes the fragments scatter from a single origin point," she said. "They might not."

"The pattern reader thinks they do," Julian said. "That's what Fletcher said — someone who understands the fragment scattering *pattern*. A pattern implies structure. Structure implies origin."

"Or it implies intention," Sable said.

Julian stopped writing. "What's the difference?"

"Origin means it happened once and spread outward. Intention means something decided where each one would land."

Julian stared at the paper. Then he wrote *origin vs. intention?* and circled it twice.

Rafe came in from the direction of the south bunkhouse with his fragment out, turning it over in his palm. He'd taken to carrying it outside his shirt in the mornings — something about the light. He'd explained this to Julian, and Julian had written it down as if it were a key tactical insight.

"The signal's stronger today," Rafe said, sitting down. He held the stone up. "Not pointing anywhere specific. Just — louder. Like it knows we're talking about it."

Everyone looked at Ezra's chest.

Ezra pressed his hand to it. The star pulsed once, warm and steady, and he had the distinct feeling it was paying attention.

"Maybe it does," he said.

---

Petra arrived last, with mud on her boots from what turned out to be an early-morning walk around the perimeter. She sat down, looked at Julian's papers, and said, "What are we triangulating?"

"Fragment Seven," Julian said.

"What do we have so far?"

"Three data points. Sable's Vela Station coordinates, Rafe's Bryn Dural coordinates, and Monscollis." He spread his hands. "The problem is the signal's scattered. It's like trying to find a radio station when you don't know what frequency it's broadcasting on."

"Can you change the frequency?" Petra asked.

Silence.

Sable looked at her. "Say that again."

"I mean—" Petra reached for her stone and turned it in her fingers, the violet-white light catching and shifting. "When I mapped Gerald, I mapped it in sections. I didn't try to see the whole thing at once. I'd pick a starting point and describe outward until I got to the edge, and then I'd move to the next starting point. You can map a whole planet that way if you're patient." She set the stone down. "What if Ezra's been trying to find the whole signal at once? What if he tried to find just — one part of it?"

Julian was already writing.

Sable was quiet for a moment. Then she turned to Ezra.

"Have you been reaching for the whole thing?" she asked.

He thought about it. He had been. Every time he tried for the seventh signal, he'd sent out his pulse and waited for something complete — a solid sense of presence, the way Rafe had felt on the Ariadne or the way Petra's signal had been sharp and watchful. He'd been listening for a full signal.

"Yes," he said.

"Try a piece," Sable said.

---

They went to the crystal spire after breakfast.

Okonkwo met them there — she'd been expecting this, which meant Marcus had told her, which meant Marcus had been watching Julian's maps and had said nothing about any of it while apparently knowing exactly where this was heading. Rafe noted this aloud. Marcus shrugged in the specific way that meant *I've been in the colony scout service for twelve years and I know how mornings work.*

The spire was at its best in the morning, when the light hit it from a low angle and the facets caught it in different colors — amber and blue and, today, something closer to violet that Petra stood still watching for a long moment.

"That's new," Ezra said.

"No," Sable said. "That's Petra." She gestured at the pendant. "The spire's been reacting to fragments since before you got here. Now there are four of us."

Petra looked at her stone, then at the violet sheen in the spire. Something crossed her face that was quiet and private.

"Okay," she said, after a moment.

Ollie had already taken his position at the base of the spire, sitting pressed against the pale stone, ears forward. Not alert — attentive. He knew what they were doing.

Ezra put his hand on the spire's surface and felt the familiar resonance — his heartbeat matching the slow pulse of the crystal.

"Piece at a time," Sable reminded him.

He closed his eyes.

---

The first piece he found was a direction.

Not a location — just a direction. A bearing, faint but consistent. It didn't feel like north or south. It felt like — away. Toward something that was also toward nothing else on Sable's chart. A gap in the yellow marks.

He kept his anchor on small things. Declan's four-direction hair this morning. The crumbs in Julian's notebook. The way Rafe's fragment had glowed when he said *louder* earlier.

He extended from his chest, slow and patient, and he didn't listen for a full signal.

He listened for a piece.

And there it was.

Just one thing. Just one: warmth. Not fear, not exhaustion, not the careful watchfulness of Petra's signal or the ragged tiredness of Rafe's. Just warmth. Faint and scattered, but — warm. Like something that was trying to be okay and mostly succeeding, but would be better if it weren't alone.

He came back slowly.

"Warm," he said, when he could talk again. "The signal. The piece I could find — it's warm. Not scared. Not lonely the way Rafe was, not tired the way the signal felt before we went to Gerald. Just — warm. Trying to be okay."

Sable looked at the star chart.

"That's a very specific emotional register," she said.

"Maybe they're not that far from someone they love," Rafe said.

Everyone looked at him.

He shrugged. "When I was at Bryn Dural, the survey crew was there for the first seven months. I wasn't alone then. The fragment felt different before the rockslide than it did after." He turned the amber stone over. "When I was alone I felt the cold all the time. If the seventh one is warm — maybe they still have their people."

---

"So they might not know they're in danger," Julian said.

"Or they know and they're not afraid," Petra said. "I wasn't afraid on Gerald until the agent showed up."

"You were alone for seven months and you weren't afraid?"

"I was sometimes." She looked at her boots. "But mostly I was — interested. I'd found a piece of the universe that didn't make sense yet. I wanted to figure it out." A small pause. "I named a planet Gerald. I wasn't in crisis mode."

Declan had been sitting with Ollie at the base of the spire, watching all of this with the focused attention of a two-year-old who understands approximately forty percent of any given situation but is tracking the emotional register of all of it.

"Gerald," Declan said, firmly, as if confirming his attendance in this conversation.

"Yes, buddy," Petra said.

Declan appeared satisfied. He went back to patting Ollie.

"The bearing is consistent," Ezra said. He looked at Sable. "When I held it, it didn't shift. It didn't scatter. It was faint, but it was pointing the same direction."

"That might be Petra's method," Sable said. "Smaller ask, cleaner answer." She pulled out the star chart and looked at the last remaining yellow mark. "If the bearing is consistent, and if the warm register is right—" She traced a line from Monscollis out along the bearing Ezra had indicated. "There are two possible systems along this path. One is charted and surveyed. Nothing of note. One is"— she paused — "a transit hub."

"What kind of transit hub?" Marcus asked.

"Commercial stopover. Ships come through for resupply and jump prep. There's a station — small, independent, not colony-affiliated." She looked up. "People in transit. People who aren't from anywhere in particular."

"Someone who moves around," Julian said. "That's why the signal scatters. If the holder is on a ship or in a transit station, the fragment's frequency shifts with their movement."

Rafe held up his stone. "It's closer than Bryn Dural was."

"How do you know?" Sable asked.

"Because I can feel it right now." He held it up. The amber stone had taken on the faintest pulse — barely visible, but there. "Faint. But it's not — it's not across the galaxy. It's reachable."

---

They found Okonkwo in her office an hour later.

She was at her desk with two sets of papers, working through colony freight records with the patience of someone who has searched freight records before and knows how long it takes and is prepared to be fine about it.

She looked up when they came in — all five of them, plus Ollie, plus Marcus, who had become so habitually attached to these situations that she didn't bother noting his presence separately.

"You have something," she said.

"A bearing," Julian said. "And two possible systems. One is empty. One is a transit hub." He put his notebook on her desk. "If the fragment holder is there, we can reach them. Maybe in a day."

Okonkwo looked at the notebook. She looked at the bearing. She looked at Julian's careful handwriting, the underlined words, the two starred systems.

She looked at Ezra.

"What did the signal feel like?"

"Warm," he said. "Not in distress. But not quite okay either. Like someone who is fine but would be better if—" He stopped.

"If they weren't alone with it," Sable finished.

Okonkwo was quiet for a moment. Outside, through her small window, the crystal spire caught the midmorning light.

"Is Wren available?" Marcus asked.

Okonkwo picked up her radio.

---

The Ariadne was still in dock, which turned out to be the result of Wren doing what Wren always did after a mission — spending an unreasonable number of hours running every system diagnostic twice on the grounds that an unreasonable number of hours was fewer hours than being stranded at a transit hub.

She listened to the situation in twelve words or fewer per response, which was about her average. When Marcus laid out the two possible systems, she looked at the trajectory, did something that might have been math in her head, and said, "The transit hub is an eight-hour jump. Weather's good."

"Weather," Rafe said.

"Space weather," Petra said.

"That's a thing?" Rafe asked.

"Yes," said Wren, Sable, and Petra at the same time.

Rafe wrote this down in the margin of Julian's notebook, which Julian immediately noticed and did not comment on. This was, for Julian, remarkable restraint.

---

Mom was in the cabin with Declan when Ezra came to say he was going.

He hadn't figured out yet how to do this part — the part where he told her. He was always surprised, a little, by how she handled it. Not with alarm. Not with the long silence that meant she was deciding something. Just with the steady, quiet look she had that meant *I know*, and *I'm afraid*, and *I trust you*, all at once.

"The transit hub," he said. "We think the seventh holder might be there."

"Wren?"

"Wren."

"Marcus?"

"Marcus."

"Sable?"

"Yes."

She nodded. That was her checklist, he realized. Three names. The list of people who made it less scary.

"Back in two days?" she asked.

"If it's the right system."

"And if it's the wrong one?"

"Then we figure out the other one." He sat down next to her. "Okonkwo thinks the window is real. Voss and Crane are gone. If we're going to find Seven before the Shadow Hand figures out where the signal is—"

"I know." She wasn't arguing with him. She hadn't been arguing with him for a while now. That was something he'd noticed — when had that changed? When had he become someone she didn't argue with about danger, only asked three names? He wasn't sure. He thought it might have been the Vault. Or Bryn Dural. Or maybe it had happened gradually, chapter by chapter, the way everything did.

"Tell me what it felt like," she said. "The signal."

"Warm," he said. "Just — warm."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she pulled him into a one-arm hug that was brief and specific, not the long-and-worried kind but the we're-okay kind.

"Okay," she said.

---

Declan performed his departure ritual with full ceremony.

The two-knee-pat. A long look at Ezra's face. A careful assessment of whether Ezra was wearing adequate footwear (result: inconclusive, Declan did not appear to own strong feelings about sneakers specifically).

Then he reached into his pocket.

He did not have toast. He had thought about this, apparently, and arrived at a substitute. He held out a small round rock — smooth, grey, unremarkable. One of about forty he kept in a collection under his bed.

"For lone-ee," he said.

Ezra took the rock. It was warm from Declan's pocket.

"You're sure?" Ezra said. "This is a good one."

Declan looked at the rock. He looked at Ezra. He looked at the direction of the spire.

"Lone-ee need it more," he said.

Ezra closed his fingers around it.

"I'll make sure they get it," he said.

Declan nodded — satisfied, mission-complete — and walked back inside. He appeared to be completely unbothered, the way only two-year-olds and very wise people are completely unbothered by the things they cannot control. He trusted Ezra with the rock. That was the whole thing.

Ollie was already at the dock.

---

The Ariadne jumped at noon.

The transit hub's designation was a number and a letter, which no one ever used. It was called Meridian Point by the ships that stopped there — which meant whoever had named it was either optimistic or had a good sense of humor, because it was three prefab modules bolted together in an unimpressive orbit around an unimpressive star, and the coffee was bad.

Wren knew this because she had been there before.

"Is the coffee actually bad?" Rafe asked.

"Famously," Wren said.

"My uncle used to say transit hub coffee is what happens when nobody knows how long they'll be there, so nobody cares how it tastes," Petra said.

"Your uncle sounds experienced," Sable said.

"He's been to forty-one systems." Petra had her fragment out, turning it in the low blue transit-jump light. "He always said the best sign of a good place to work is whether the food is bad or just boring. Bad means nobody's home. Boring means somebody's trying."

"What's Meridian Point?" Julian asked.

Petra considered. "Bad," she said.

"Good," Julian said, and wrote this down.

Ezra sat across from all of them and watched the way the jump light caught Rafe's amber and Sable's silver-blue and Petra's shifting violet-white, and he put his hand to his chest and waited for the signal.

Halfway through the jump, it came.

Not scattered this time. Not a flicker. A clear, steady, warm pulse — like a heartbeat that knew another heartbeat was coming.

"There," he said, quietly.

Ollie's tail swept twice against the floor.

Nobody said anything else for the rest of the jump.

The seventh fragment was awake. It was waiting. And tomorrow, unless everything went sideways, they'd find out who was carrying it.

Ezra turned Declan's rock over in his pocket and watched the stars compress toward the transit hub ahead.

*We're coming*, he thought. *Hold on.*
