# Chapter 15: The Scattered Signal

The morning started with a disagreement about maps.

Julian had been up before anyone else — Ezra knew this from the three papers taped to the kitchen table and the crumbs already in Julian's notebook at six-thirty in the morning.

"The signal has to have a direction," Julian said, not looking up when Ezra walked in. "Everything Sable said about fragments is directional. They have a pull. They pulse *toward* other fragments. So if we triangulate from Monscollis and from Sable's Vela Station location and from Rafe's Bryn Dural location—"

"Morning to you too," Ezra said.

"—then we can cut out a big part of the sky." Julian finally looked up. "Do you have the Vela Station location numbers? The actual ones, not the name."

"I just woke up."

"Sable has them."

"Sable is also asleep."

Julian thought about this. "What time does she get up?"

"Normal time."

Julian wrote something down. He seemed to think that was useful information.

Declan appeared in the doorway in his pajamas. Hair going in four directions. Holding a stuffed something that might have been a rabbit once.

"Brefkist," he said. Not a question.

"Yes," Ezra said. "Breakfast."

"Me too," Declan said, and walked to his chair and sat down and waited. This was his entire plan for breakfast. He felt it was enough.

Ezra made toast.

---

Sable came in twenty minutes later. Julian visibly relaxed. She did have the Vela Station location — or at least, a direction from Monscollis, which Julian said was good enough.

She sat across from his papers, looked at them, and pointed at one of his angles.

"This assumes the fragments scattered from a single starting point," she said. "They might not have."

"The pattern reader thinks they did," Julian said. "That's what Fletcher said — someone who understands the scattering *pattern*. A pattern means structure. Structure means it came from somewhere."

"Or it means someone chose where each one would go," Sable said.

Julian stopped writing. "What's the difference?"

"One happened by accident. One was decided."

Julian stared at the paper. Then he wrote *accident vs. decision?* and circled it twice.

Rafe came in from the south building with his fragment in his hand, turning it over. He'd started carrying it outside his shirt in the mornings — something about the light. He'd explained this to Julian. Julian had written it down as if it were a key tactical fact.

"The signal's stronger today," Rafe said, sitting down. He held up the stone. "Not pointing anywhere. Just — louder. Like it knows we're talking about it."

Everyone looked at Ezra's chest.

Ezra pressed his hand to it. The star pulsed once, warm and steady, and he had the clear feeling it was paying attention.

"Maybe it does," he said.

---

Petra arrived last, with mud on her boots from an early walk around the edge of the colony. She sat down, looked at Julian's papers, and said, "What are we trying to find?"

"Fragment Seven," Julian said.

"What do we have so far?"

"Three points. Sable's Vela Station location, Rafe's Bryn Dural location, and Monscollis." He spread his hands. "The problem is the signal's scattered. It's like trying to find a radio station when you don't know what frequency it's on."

"Can you change the frequency?" Petra asked.

Silence.

Sable looked at her. "Say that again."

"I mean—" Petra picked up her stone and turned it in her fingers, the purple-white light shifting. "When I mapped Gerald, I did it in sections. I didn't try to see the whole planet at once. I'd pick a starting point and describe outward until I hit the edge, then move to the next starting point. You can map a whole planet that way if you're patient." She set the stone down. "What if Ezra's been trying to find the whole signal at once? What if he tried to find just one piece of it?"

Julian was already writing.

Sable was quiet. Then she turned to Ezra.

"Have you been reaching for the whole thing?" she asked.

He thought about it. He had. Every time he tried for the seventh signal, he'd sent out his pulse and waited for something complete — a full, solid sense of someone there, like Rafe had felt on the Ariadne, or like Petra's signal had been sharp and watchful. He'd been listening for everything at once.

"Yes," he said.

"Try a piece," Sable said.

---

They went to the crystal spire after breakfast.

Okonkwo met them there — she'd been expecting it, which meant Marcus had told her, which meant Marcus had been watching Julian's papers and hadn't said a word while apparently knowing exactly where all of this was heading. Rafe mentioned this out loud. Marcus shrugged in the way that meant *I've been doing this for twelve years.*

The spire was best in the morning. The light came from a low angle and caught in the facets — amber, blue, and today something closer to purple that Petra stood watching for a quiet moment.

"That's new," Ezra said.

"No," Sable said. "That's Petra." She gestured at the pendant. "The spire's been reacting to fragments since before you got here. Now there are four of us."

Petra looked at her stone, then at the purple color in the spire. Something crossed her face that was private.

"Okay," she said, after a moment.

Ollie had already taken his spot at the base of the spire, sitting pressed against the pale stone, ears forward. Not on alert — just paying attention. He knew what they were doing.

Ezra put his hand on the spire's surface and felt the familiar feeling — his heartbeat matching the slow pulse of the crystal.

"A piece at a time," Sable reminded him.

He closed his eyes.

---

The first piece he found was a direction.

Not a location — just a direction. A bearing, faint but steady. Not north or south. More like *away*. Toward something that wasn't near any of the other yellow marks. A gap.

He kept his anchor on small things. Declan's four-direction hair this morning. The crumbs in Julian's notebook. The way Rafe's fragment had glowed when he said *louder.*

He reached from his chest, slow and patient, and he didn't try to find the whole signal.

He listened for one piece.

And there it was.

Just one thing: warmth. Not fear, not tiredness, not Petra's careful watchfulness or Rafe's ragged coldness. Just warmth. Faint and scattered, but — warm. Like something that was doing okay and trying to stay that way, but would be better if it weren't alone.

He came back slowly.

"Warm," he said, when he could talk. "The signal — the piece I could find — it's warm. Not scared. Not lonely the way Rafe was. Not tired the way it felt before we went to Gerald. Just — warm. Trying to be okay."

Sable looked at the star chart.

"That's a very specific feeling," she said.

"Maybe they're not far from someone they love," Rafe said.

Everyone looked at him.

He shrugged. "When I was at Bryn Dural, the survey crew was there for the first seven months. I wasn't alone then. The fragment felt different before the rockslide." He turned the amber stone over. "When I was alone I felt cold all the time. If the seventh one is warm — maybe they still have their people."

---

"So they might not know they're in danger," Julian said.

"Or they know and they're not afraid," Petra said. "I wasn't afraid on Gerald until the agent showed up."

"You were alone for seven months and you weren't afraid?"

"Sometimes." She looked at her boots. "Mostly I was — interested. I'd found a piece of the universe that didn't make sense yet. I wanted to figure it out." A small pause. "I named a planet Gerald. I wasn't panicking."

Declan had been sitting with Ollie at the base of the spire, watching everyone with the attention of a two-year-old who understands about forty percent of what's being said but is tracking all of the feelings.

"Gerald," Declan said firmly, like he was signing in for attendance.

"Yes, buddy," Petra said.

Declan looked satisfied. He went back to patting Ollie.

"The direction is steady," Ezra said. He looked at Sable. "When I held it, it didn't shift. It pointed the same way the whole time."

"That might be Petra's method," Sable said. "Smaller ask, cleaner answer." She pulled out the star chart and traced a line from Monscollis along the direction Ezra had described. "There are two possible systems along this path. One is mapped and surveyed. Nothing there. The other is—" She paused. "A transit hub."

"What kind?" Marcus asked.

"Commercial stopover. Ships stop for supplies and jump prep. Small, not part of any colony." She looked up. "People in transit. People who aren't from any one place."

"Someone who moves around," Julian said. "That's why the signal scatters. If the holder is on a ship or at a transit station, the signal shifts with their movement."

Rafe held up his stone. "It's closer than Bryn Dural was."

"How do you know?" Sable asked.

"Because I can feel it right now." He held it up. The amber stone had taken on the faintest pulse — barely there, but present. "Faint. But reachable."

---

They found Okonkwo in her office an hour later.

She was at her desk working through colony freight records. She looked up when they came in — all five of them, plus Ollie, plus Marcus, who had become so attached to these moments that she didn't bother noting him separately.

"You have something," she said.

"A direction," Julian said. "And two possible systems. One empty, one a transit hub." He put his notebook on her desk. "If the holder is there, we can reach them in about a day."

Okonkwo looked at the notebook. She looked at Julian's careful handwriting and the two starred systems.

She looked at Ezra. "What did the signal feel like?"

"Warm," he said. "Not in trouble. But not quite okay either. Like someone who is fine but would be better if—" He stopped.

"If they weren't alone with it," Sable finished.

Okonkwo was quiet. Through her small window, the crystal spire caught the midmorning light.

"Is Wren available?" Marcus asked.

Okonkwo picked up her radio.

---

The Ariadne was still in dock. This was because Wren, after every mission, ran every single system check twice. She said an unreasonable amount of checking was better than being stuck at a transit hub.

She heard the situation using twelve words or fewer per response, which was her average. When Marcus laid out the two systems, she looked at the path, did something that was probably math in her head, and said: "The transit hub is eight hours. Weather's good."

"Weather," Rafe said.

"Space weather," Petra said.

"That's a thing?" Rafe asked.

"Yes," said Wren, Sable, and Petra at the same time.

Rafe wrote this in the margin of Julian's notebook, which Julian noticed right away and chose not to comment on. This was, for Julian, remarkable self-control.

---

Mom was in the cabin with Declan when Ezra came to say he was going.

He hadn't figured out how to do this part yet. He was always surprised by how she handled it. Not with worry. Not with long silences. Just with the steady, quiet look she had that meant *I know*, and *I'm afraid*, and *I trust you*, all at the same time.

"The transit hub," he said. "We think the seventh holder might be there."

"Wren?"

"Wren."

"Marcus?"

"Marcus."

"Sable?"

"Yes."

She nodded. That was her checklist, he realized. Three names. The three that made it less scary.

"Back in two days?" she asked.

"If it's the right system."

"And if it's not?"

"Then we try the other one." He sat next to her. "Okonkwo thinks the window is real. Voss and Crane are gone. If we're going to find Seven before the Shadow Hand figures out where the signal is—"

"I know." She wasn't arguing. She hadn't been arguing with him for a while now. When had that changed? He wasn't sure. Maybe the Vault. Maybe Bryn Dural. Maybe it had happened slowly, the way most things did.

"Tell me what it felt like," she said. "The signal."

"Warm," he said. "Just — warm."

She pulled him into a quick, steady hug. Not the long worried kind. The we're-okay kind.

"Okay," she said.

---

Declan did his departure routine with full ceremony.

The two-knee-pat. A long look at Ezra's face. A careful check of Ezra's footwear (result: unclear, Declan did not seem to have strong feelings about sneakers specifically, but he wanted the record to show he had looked).

Then he reached into his pocket.

No toast this time. He had thought about this, apparently. He held out a small round rock — smooth, grey, completely ordinary. One of about forty he kept in a collection under his bed.

"For lone-ee," he said.

Ezra took the rock. It was warm from Declan's pocket.

"You're sure?" Ezra said. "This is a good one."

Declan looked at the rock. Looked at Ezra. Looked in the direction of the spire.

"Lone-ee need it more," he said.

Ezra closed his fingers around it.

"I'll make sure they get it," he said.

Declan nodded — satisfied, done — and walked back inside. He was completely calm about it. The way only two-year-olds and very wise people are calm about things they can't control. He had trusted Ezra with the rock. That was the whole thing.

Ollie was already at the dock.

---

The Ariadne jumped at noon.

The transit hub's official name was a number and a letter that nobody used. Ships called it Meridian Point — which meant whoever named it was either hopeful or had a sense of humor, because it was three prefab parts bolted together in orbit around a plain star, and the coffee was famously bad.

Wren had been there before.

"Is the coffee actually that bad?" Rafe asked.

"Famously," Wren said.

"My uncle always said transit hub coffee is what happens when nobody knows how long they'll be staying, so nobody cares how it tastes," Petra said.

"Your uncle sounds like someone who's been around," Sable said.

"Forty-one systems." Petra had her fragment out, turning it in the low blue jump-light. "He always said the best sign of a good place to work is whether the food is bad or just boring. Bad means nobody's home. Boring means somebody's trying."

"What's Meridian Point?" Julian asked.

Petra thought. "Bad," she said.

"Good," Julian said, and wrote this down.

Ezra sat across from all of them and watched the jump light catch Rafe's amber and Sable's silver-blue and Petra's shifting purple-white. He put his hand to his chest and waited for the signal.

Halfway through the jump, it came.

Not scattered this time. Not a flicker. A clear, steady, warm pulse — like a heartbeat that knew another heartbeat was coming.

"There," he said quietly.

Ollie's tail swept twice across the floor.

Nobody said anything else for the rest of the jump.

The seventh fragment was awake. It was waiting. And tomorrow, unless everything went wrong, they'd find out who was carrying it.

Ezra turned Declan's rock over in his pocket and watched the stars compress ahead of them.

*We're coming*, he thought. *Hold on.*
