# Chapter 6: The Second Fragment

The unknown watcher showed up again the next morning.

Not in the forest this time. In the colony's main hall, sitting at a corner table with a bowl of porridge and a cup of something hot, reading a folded piece of paper like someone who had nowhere to be and all day to get there.

Ezra saw them first.

He and Julian had come with Mom to pick up breakfast rations—a thing they did every other morning, a five-minute errand that usually involved Julian trying to talk Mom into extra bread and failing. But when Ezra walked through the main hall's big wooden doors, the star in his chest pulsed once. Sharp. *There.*

He caught Julian's sleeve.

Julian followed his gaze. Across the crowded hall, through the noise of colonists eating and talking and scraping chairs, the figure sat quietly. Dark clothes. Young—not a kid, but not an adult either. Maybe fifteen or sixteen. A girl, now that Ezra could see her face. Long dark hair pulled back. Tan skin, dark eyes. A thin scar along the line of her jaw. She was reading her paper. She looked completely, deliberately ordinary.

She also looked up at exactly that moment and met Ezra's eyes.

She didn't look surprised.

"Mom," Ezra said quietly.

"Hmm?" Mom was signing for their breakfast parcel.

"That person over there. I think they're the watcher from the forest."

Mom turned—too quickly, in the way that meant she was trying not to seem alarmed and wasn't quite managing it. She looked at the girl. The girl looked back at them, calm as anything.

Then she folded her paper, stood, and walked toward them.

Julian moved in front of Ezra, which Ezra thought was sweet and also a little bit silly, since Julian was seven.

The girl stopped a few feet away. She glanced at Julian and the corner of her mouth did something—not quite a smile, but close. Like she'd seen a lot of things and a small brave seven-year-old was, in context, a pleasant one.

"I'm not Shadow Hand," she said. Her voice was low and direct, like someone who didn't waste words. "My name is Sable. I've been here twelve days. I needed to make sure about you before I said anything." Her dark eyes moved to Ezra. "You have the first fragment."

"What's the second?" Julian said.

The girl—Sable—opened her collar, just slightly, and pulled out a thin cord. At the end of it hung a small stone that glowed, very faintly, with a cool silver-blue light. Different from the warm gold of Ezra's star. Steadier. Like starlight through ice.

"Me," she said.

---

They found Dad and Marcus at the cabin and made introductions. Marcus had his soldier face on the whole time, which was the face he made when he was deciding how much to trust something. Sable seemed to understand this without being told, and she answered his questions before he asked them, which made him trust her slightly more and also slightly less.

She was from a colony called Vela Station, fourteen light-years away. She'd been traveling alone for three months on a commercial freighter, following what she described as "the pull"—a compass feeling in her chest that had started six months ago when her fragment had activated.

"What activated it?" Dad asked.

Sable was quiet for a moment. "I did something hard," she said. "Something that cost me. The power noticed." She didn't seem to want to say more than that, and Dad didn't push her.

Her fragment had been embedded in a piece of rock near Vela Station's drill site—deep, deeper than the Monscollis Vault, so far down that nobody had gone there on purpose. Sable had gone down alone on a hunch. The rock had opened for her the way a hand opens when it decides to let something go.

"What can you do?" Julian asked.

Sable looked at him. The not-quite-smile again. "Lots of things. But that's not why I'm here."

"Then why are you here?" Mom asked.

"Because there are seven fragments," Sable said. "And someone has already collected two of the others."

---

She spread a rough hand-drawn map on the cabin table—not a colony map this time, but a star chart. Planets, routes, jump points, with marks in three colors. Blue for fragments found or suspected. Red for Shadow Hand activity. Yellow for something else that she didn't label.

"I've been tracking the Shadow Hand for eight months," she said. "They have collectors. People who go to sites where the Covenant energy is high and wait for someone to open a fragment source. Then they take it." Her finger traced two red marks, both far from Monscollis. "They have two fragments already. Not activated—they can't use them, because the power doesn't respond to them. But they're holding them."

"Like a key they can't put in a lock," Julian said.

"Exactly. They're hoping to find a wielder to force."

Ezra was looking at the map. The yellow marks. There were three of them. "What are those?"

Sable hesitated. "I don't know yet. Other presences. Power signatures I picked up in transit—not Shadow Hand, not open fragments. Something... waiting."

The star in Ezra's chest pulsed, as if it recognized a word it hadn't heard before.

"Three more fragment-holders?" Julian said.

"Maybe. Or three more sources nobody's found. Or something else entirely." She looked at Ezra again. "The Covenant is older than anything we know how to measure. I've learned to stay humble about guessing what it's doing."

Tyler had been sitting in the corner being very quiet—his deep-water worry thing—but now he looked up. "So what do we do?"

Sable pulled back her star chart and folded it carefully. "The Shadow Hand needs seven fragments to do what they're trying to do. They have two. You have one." She nodded at Ezra. "So do I. That's four unaccounted for." She paused. "The thing they want to do—I don't fully understand it yet. But it's something irreversible. Something that changes the Covenant itself." She looked at Dad and Marcus. "I think you need to know that. Before you decide whether to work with me."

The cabin was very quiet. Ollie, who had been lying under the table, crept out and put his head in Ezra's lap.

Dad looked at Marcus.

Marcus looked at Dad.

"What kind of help are you offering?" Dad asked.

"Information. My fragment complements Ezra's—they're different. Where his is warm and connected, mine is..." She touched the stone at her collar. "Precise. Clear. Good for seeing patterns. I've been tracking this for months alone. I'd rather not do it alone anymore." A pause. "And he needs to train. Properly. There are things he needs to know about the Covenant that I can tell him."

She looked at Ezra. And for the first time, her voice did something unexpected—it softened, just a little. Not in a sad way. In the way of someone who remembered being new at something hard.

"You've had your fragment for less than a week," she said. "And you're already thinking about how to use it to protect people." She tilted her head. "That's not common. That matters."

Ezra felt his face get warm. "I don't know what I'm doing," he admitted.

"No," Sable said. "But you know *why* you're doing it. That's the harder half."

---

They didn't decide everything that day. Mom and Dad asked Sable about a hundred questions. Marcus checked three things she said against what he already knew and found them accurate, which raised her from "possible" to "probable" in his mental ledger. Maya came over in the afternoon and immediately had strong opinions about Sable, which started out as suspicion and evolved, over two hours of talking, into something more like guarded respect.

Julian invited Sable to train with them the next morning.

Sable looked at him. "Where?"

"The northern clearing. With the moss logs." Julian paused. "I run the drills."

Sable looked at Dad.

Dad put his hands up in a *don't look at me* gesture.

"Fine," Sable said. "I'll come." She picked up her star chart, rolled it, and tucked it into the inside of her jacket. "I'll also bring some drills of my own."

Julian opened his mouth.

"Mine will be more useful," she said.

Julian closed his mouth.

Ezra, quietly, laughed. Ollie's tail thumped twice against his leg.

Outside, the crystal spire pulsed its slow, ancient heartbeat. Somewhere far away—fourteen light-years, twelve days, one hard thing done alone—the Covenant had put two fragments in the same colony. That seemed, to Ezra, like it probably wasn't an accident.

*Four,* the star in his chest said, in the way it never said words but sometimes still communicated clearly. *There are three more.*

*We'll find them.*

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest and thought: *Yeah. We will.*
