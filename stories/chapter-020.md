# Chapter 20: The Covenant Whole

The trip home should have taken two days.

It took one, because the fragments wouldn't stop glowing.

Not in a gentle, pleasant way. In a *please stop lighting up the sleeping quarters at 2 AM* way. Rafe had tried wrapping the yellow fragment in his jacket. It glowed through the jacket. Petra had found a small metal box. The box hummed. Julian had suggested putting them in a separate compartment, which was a sensible idea until the ship's navigation console started responding to their combined pulse and they nearly rerouted themselves to the wrong star system.

"They want to be together," Ezra said, holding all seven fragments in a cloth bag in his lap. The bag glowed like a lantern. "That's what they've been waiting for."

"That's great," Petra said flatly. "I slept four hours."

"I slept great," Declan announced. He had slept through everything.

"I know," said Petra.

---

Monscollis Colony looked the same when they landed — which was somehow both comforting and strange. The same red-rock plains. The same squat gray buildings with their patchy solar panels. The same smell of warm dust and colony fuel and whatever Kira from Building 3 was always cooking that smelled like garlic and engine oil.

Mom and Dad were waiting at the landing pad.

Mom had her arms crossed in the way that meant she was not going to cry in public. She absolutely cried in public.

"All seven?" she asked.

"All seven," Julian said. He held up the cloth bag, which was emitting a warm, steady glow through the fabric. 

"Fwagments!" Declan announced, lifting both arms for Dad to pick him up.

Dad grabbed him, hugged him hard, and then looked at Ezra over Declan's shoulder. "You all right?"

Ezra nodded. His chest still hummed — not loudly, just steady and low, the way the colony generators sounded at night. He'd stopped noticing it, which was either a very good sign or a sign that he'd adjusted to something deeply unusual. Both, probably.

"What happens now?" Mom asked.

Nobody answered right away.

That was the question.

---

Sable called a meeting in the research room that afternoon.

It was a real meeting, not the kind where Julian called it and immediately took over. Sable stood at the board with the star chart and the seven crossed-off marks. All seven fragments were arranged on the table in front of them — each one softly glowing, each one a different color, the whole spread of them together making the room feel warmer and less like a research room.

"We need to decide what to do with them," Sable said. "All seven fragments. Whole, for the first time."

"What *can* we do with them?" Rafe asked.

"That's what we need to figure out." Sable pulled out a slim, battered notebook — her own, covered in handwriting so small it looked like it was trying to hide from readers. "The original Covenant texts don't describe what happens when the seven are assembled. They just describe the shape of it. A complete chord. A key."

"A key to what?" Petra asked.

"I don't know."

Julian had already written *A KEY TO WHAT?* in his notebook, underlined twice.

"The dead star pulsed," Noa said. She had her headphones pushed back on her head, the way she wore them when she was actually paying attention. "In the Belt. When we got the seventh. It reacted."

"Everything reacted," Ezra said quietly. "Even here." He pressed a hand to his chest. "I felt it from the ship. Like — something finished. Something that had been open for a very long time just... closed."

"A circuit," Noa said.

Everyone looked at her.

She shrugged. "That's what it sounds like. I can hear them better since we got the seventh one. It's like a signal that was broken up into seven pieces, and now it's broadcasting whole." She paused. "I don't know what the broadcast *says*. I just know it's going somewhere."

Declan raised his hand.

They had learned, over months of missions, to take Declan's hand-raises seriously. He was two. He was also the person who had communicated with an inter-dimensional entity using perfect pitch, so.

"Yes, Decker," Sable said.

Declan pointed at the fragments on the table. "Song says: come here."

"Come here?" Sable repeated. "Like a beacon?"

Declan nodded, very certain. "Come here. Come see."

He picked up Julian's crayon (it was on the table, Julian always had crayons) and drew a messy circle on the nearest piece of paper. Then seven dots around the outside of the circle.

He looked up. "Home," he said.

He pointed at the middle.

Then he pointed at the fragments.

"Key," he said.

The room was quiet.

"The Covenant," Sable said, very carefully, "might be a key to something on Monscollis."

"Something underground?" Julian asked.

"Or something that arrives," Ezra said, and the gold fragment pulsed once, warm and certain, against his ribs.

---

It took them three days to find it.

Sable led the search, using her pattern-sensing to trace the faint signal that all seven fragments were now broadcasting together. It moved like a thread through the colony — invisible to everyone else, but to Sable it was clear as a line drawn in chalk.

It led them out past the western edge of the colony, through the red-rock plain, and down a rocky slope to a section of ground that didn't look like anything at all.

No carvings. No doors. No dramatic ancient architecture.

Just rock.

Declan walked to the middle of it and sat down.

"Here," he said.

Julian measured the area with a tape measure he had brought for this exact situation. Petra mapped the site. Rafe moved three large boulders out of the way with telekinesis because it was faster than everyone climbing around them. Ollie sniffed the entire perimeter, fur standing up in the way that meant ancient power, not danger.

"He's not scared," Ezra noted.

"No," Sable agreed. She was crouching at the edge of the site, both hands flat on the rock, fragment blazing. "He's not. Neither is my fragment." She looked up. "This isn't a threat. I've felt threats. This is... an invitation."

Ezra opened the cloth bag. The fragments lit up together, seven colors at once — gold, silver-blue, amber, violet-white, yellow-green, rust-red, blazing yellow. The combined glow hit the rock, and something in the rock answered.

Lines appeared — the same spiral carvings from the Shale Drift cave. Ancient and slow, like they had been waiting under the surface all along.

"Oh wow," Ezra whispered.

"Yeah," said Rafe, who did not usually say things like that.

Even Petra looked like she'd forgotten to be unimpressed.

The spirals spread outward from the center in slow, deliberate rings. They covered the rock the way frost covers a window in winter — quiet, patient, inevitable.

Then they stopped.

A door-shaped outline. Right where Declan was sitting.

Declan looked down at it. He looked up at all of them. He got to his feet with the specific careful ceremony of a two-year-old who knew everyone was watching.

He placed one small hand flat against the outlined rock.

The fragments blazed.

The door opened.

---

Not with a boom. Not with dramatic thunder or ancient fanfare. It just — slid aside, silent, opening onto a set of stairs cut from dark stone that spiraled down into soft light.

Not threatening light. Warm light. The same amber-gold as the combined fragments.

Julian stuck his head in first, because of course he did.

"Stairs," he reported. "Going down. There's light at the bottom." He pulled his head back. "I'm going."

"We're going together," Sable said firmly.

"I was *going* to say that."

"You were going to go first and say 'it's probably fine' over your shoulder."

Julian did not deny this.

They descended together — Sable first, then Julian, Rafe, Petra, Noa. Ezra went ahead of Declan, one hand back so Declan could hold it. Ollie trotted down the middle of the group, ears up, tail wagging slowly. Unbothered. Very useful for group morale.

The stairs were longer than expected. Not uncomfortably long. But long enough to make everyone quiet.

The light got warmer as they descended.

And then the stairs ended.

---

The chamber was round, carved from the same black stone as the Shale Drift, but not rough. Smooth. Ancient smooth, like it had been worn down over a very long time by... something. The walls were covered in spirals — some glowing faint gold, some dormant, waiting.

In the center of the floor: a single raised dais, roughly seven-sided. Seven shallow carved depressions in the stone, each one about the size of a fragment.

Nobody said anything for a moment.

Then Julian said, "That's a cradle."

"An altar," Sable said.

"A charging dock," Noa said. When everyone looked at her, she added, "That's what it sounds like. The signal is pointing at that." She gestured at the depressions. "Each fragment has a spot."

"We put them in there," Rafe said. "That's what it's asking."

"And then what?" Petra said.

Nobody knew.

Declan, not waiting for the philosophical debate to conclude, walked to the dais and held out one hand toward Ezra. "Give me?"

Ezra hesitated. He looked at Sable.

Sable studied the room. Her fragment glowed steady and sure. She nodded, once.

Ezra unclipped his gold fragment from its cord and placed it in Declan's hand. It was warm and steady, the same as always.

Declan walked to the dais. He looked at the seven depressions. He placed the gold one in the correct spot with the careful precision of a toddler who had done this in his dreams.

Then he turned and walked back to Ezra, hand out.

"Nex' one."

---

One by one, each Guardian brought their fragment forward and let Declan place it.

Not because he had to. Because it felt right. Because he was the one who had heard the song from the beginning, who had felt it grow, who had understood *finished* before any of them.

The rust-red one. The silver-blue. The amber. The violet-white. The yellow-green. The blazing yellow.

Each one clicked into place with a sound like a single struck note, clean and clear.

When the seventh settled into its spot, all seven sang together.

Not a pulse. Not a wave. A sustained, full, open chord — seven notes woven into something that the room seemed to breathe with. The spirals on the walls lit up, all of them, one after another, spreading upward through the stone ceiling and out — Ezra felt it — up through the rock and out across the plain, the way sunrise spreads.

The fragments rose.

Not high. Just a few inches, hovering gently above their depressions in the dais, spinning very slowly in a loose, synchronized ring.

The room was full of warm light.

Declan stood in the middle of it, bathed in seven colors, and put his hands in the air the way he did when he wanted Dad to pick him up.

"Done!" he announced happily.

Ezra grabbed him and hugged him so tight that Declan squeaked. Then Julian hugged Ezra, which he would later claim was an accident caused by enthusiasm. Rafe patted Julian on the back. Petra put her arm around Noa, who allowed it, and even leaned in a little. Sable stood at the edge of the dais with her hand over her mouth, looking at the seven fragments in their ring of light, and did not say anything for a long time.

Ollie sat in the middle of it all and wagged his tail.

The fragments hummed.

The chamber breathed.

Outside, the colony went about its day, not knowing that something old and whole had just remembered it was alive.
