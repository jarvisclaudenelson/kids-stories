# Chapter 9: The Signal

The morning after the crystal spire, Ezra slept until nearly midday.

He didn't mean to. He lay down on his bunk after a late breakfast and the next thing he knew Julian was sitting on the foot of the bed eating an apple and the light through the cabin window had moved all the way across the floor.

"You've been asleep for four hours," Julian said, by way of welcome.

Ezra's hand went to his chest. The star was there, warm and quiet. He let out a long breath.

"Did anything happen?"

"Sable and Marcus talked with Captain Okonkwo for a long time. I wasn't allowed in." Julian took another bite of his apple. "I stood near the window for about eight minutes before Dad told me to go play."

"Did you?"

Julian gave him a look that said *what do you think* and also said *no obviously not* and also said *I went and worked on my notebook instead*.

"Declan drew you a picture," Julian added, nodding toward the small table by the door. A crayon drawing was propped against the wall: a lumpy purple shape, a blue circle, and something that might have been a dog.

"Is that me?"

"He said it was you and the spire and Ollie." Julian finished his apple. "The purple is you."

"Why am I purple?"

Julian considered this. "I think he just likes purple."

---

The drawing was still on the table when Sable came in around noon. She looked at it without comment for a moment, then at Ezra, then set a bowl of leftover grain porridge on the table beside it.

"You need to eat," she said. "We're going to the relay station this afternoon."

Ezra sat up. "Captain Okonkwo agreed?"

"She proposed it." Sable sat cross-legged on the floor, back to the wall, which was her preferred seating in the cabin. Ezra had started to think of the floor-against-the-wall spot as Sable's chair. "She wants to try reaching the Bryn Dural team through the secondary relay while you amplify the signal through your fragment."

"I don't know how to do that."

"Neither do I." She said it plainly, not worried. "But you don't need to know how. You just need to be near the relay when you reach."

Julian had his notebook out before she finished the sentence.

"I have four questions," he said.

"You always have four questions," Sable said.

"Four minimum," Julian corrected, and she almost smiled, which was her version of laughing.

---

Declan was the one who made everything complicated.

Mom had planned to stay at the cabin with him while everyone else went to the relay station. That was the plan. It was a good plan. It lasted until approximately two minutes before departure, when Declan emerged from the back bedroom wearing his small jacket and his boots and holding a carved wooden figure of Ollie that someone in the colony had made him.

He walked to the door and stood there.

"Decker," Mom said. "We're staying here today."

Declan looked at the door. Then at Ezra. Then at the door again, in case the door changed his mind.

"I do it," he said. This was his phrase for going somewhere. It meant both *I am capable* and *let's go* and also, when used in a certain tone, *you are making a mistake and I am informing you of it calmly*.

"Decky," Dad said, crouching down to his level. "We need you and Mom to stay safe here today. Okay?"

Declan looked at Dad with the patience of a person who has heard this before.

"I do it," he repeated.

"Bud—"

"I do it." Then, with great deliberateness: "My I do it."

This was new. Ezra and Julian exchanged a look. Declan had apparently decided that wherever Ezra was going was, in some fundamental sense, also his.

Marcus coughed something that might have been a laugh from across the room.

Mom knelt beside Declan and talked quietly to him for a few minutes. In the end they negotiated: Declan and Mom would walk to the colony main hall, which was very slightly in the direction of the relay station, and would wait there with warm drinks. This was not what Declan wanted but he accepted it with the dignity of a two-year-old diplomat who knew when to take a partial win.

As they left, he pointed at Ezra and said "I do it" one more time. It sounded like a promise.

---

The relay station was a squat metal building on the colony's north ridge, its tall antenna tower rising into the pale Monscollis sky like a finger pointing at something specific. It looked nothing like the crystal spire — all function, no mystery — but when Ezra got close enough, his star noticed it anyway.

Not a reaction. More like a passing awareness. *This is a place where signals travel.*

Captain Okonkwo was already there with two colony engineers: a broad-faced woman named Pella who smelled faintly of solder, and a younger man named Sevi who looked at Ezra like he wasn't sure whether to be professional or nervous and kept landing somewhere in between.

"The secondary relay operates on a compressed burst protocol," Okonkwo explained, with the briskness of someone who assumes you'll keep up. "Normally used for long-distance colony communications when standard channels are degraded. I've been sending standard bursts to Bryn Dural every forty-eight hours for the past three weeks. No response."

"What kind of response were you expecting?" Sable asked.

"Any kind." Okonkwo looked at Ezra. "What I want to try is something different. You stand inside the relay room while we send. If the signal has any resonance with the Covenant—if there's a fragment-holder at Bryn Dural who's been trying to reach *us* — then the amplification may work in both directions."

Ezra nodded slowly. "And if there isn't a fragment-holder there?"

"Then the signal goes, hears nothing, and we know the silence is something other than Covenant interference." She said it like that was an acceptable outcome. Maybe it was. Knowing what something wasn't could be almost as useful as knowing what it was.

"He shouldn't touch the relay hardware directly," Sable said.

Okonkwo looked at her.

"The power is inside him. Uncontrolled contact with electrical equipment could— we don't know what could happen. I'd rather be cautious."

"Agreed," said Pella, the engineer, firmly, which made Ezra feel slightly better and slightly more nervous at the same time.

---

Inside the relay room, everything smelled like metal and recycled air. Equipment banks lined three walls. Pella sat at the main console. Sevi stood at the door looking like he'd been told to stay there, which he had.

Julian had convinced Marcus to let him come inside on the grounds that someone needed to document everything and he was the most qualified person for this and also he was already inside before anyone could say no.

Ezra stood in the center of the room.

He'd been thinking, on the walk over, about what he was actually supposed to do. Sable had said: *just reach.* Captain Okonkwo had said: *be present.* His own instinct said something in between—not passive, not forced, somewhere in the space between waiting and calling.

He thought about the night at the crystal spire. The signal had come in like one beat. Not afraid. Just alone.

He put his hand on his chest.

"Ready?" Pella said.

He nodded.

She sent the burst.

---

The relay hummed. Equipment flicked through its sequence. And Ezra reached.

Not out—that was the reaching mistake, the shoulder-reaching, the grasping. He extended from his center, the way Sable had taught him. Not *toward* something. More like: *I'm here. I'm staying. If you're there, this is where I am.*

For ten seconds nothing happened except the equipment doing its normal equipment things.

Then Ollie, who was sitting beside Marcus in the doorway, went absolutely still.

Not the alert-still, the danger-still. Something quieter. Ollie's head tilted left. His ears moved. His tail made two slow sweeps and stopped.

Julian saw it first. "Ez—"

And then the star spoke.

Not in words. It never did words. But the sensation that came back through it was—clear. Distinct. Nothing like the faint impression from the night before. Someone had heard the burst and was answering, not in radio, not in standard communications, but in the only channel they had left.

The signal was ragged. It had been waiting a long time. It came in fragments—not Covenant fragments, just pieces—impressions more than images. Cold. Enclosed. Something heavy overhead. Not afraid, but—worn. The particular tiredness of someone who has been holding on for a long time and is starting to wonder if they should.

And underneath all of it, one clear note. Warm, specific. The Covenant signature.

"There's someone there," Ezra said. His voice came out quieter than he expected. "They're—it's been a long time. They're really tired."

The room was quiet.

"Are they alive?" Captain Okonkwo said.

Ezra pressed his hand flat to his chest. Tried to listen harder. The signal was fraying at the edges, like a voice over a bad connection that keeps cutting out. He thought of Declan saying *my I do it*. He thought of Mom's voice. Small, specific, ordinary things.

The star steadied.

"Yes," he said. "But they need us to hurry."

---

Afterward, Okonkwo took notes for a long time and spoke quietly with Sable in the corner of the room. Julian filled four pages in his notebook. Pella ran a secondary diagnostic on every instrument in the room, not because anything had malfunctioned but because she was an engineer and this was how engineers processed surprising events.

Sevi made everyone tea, which was possibly the most useful thing anyone had done.

Ezra sat on a stool against the wall and drank his and let the star settle. The contact had been short—maybe ninety seconds—but it had cost something. Not physically. More like he'd leaned very hard in a particular direction for a long time and now he needed to stand up straight again.

Ollie came and put his head on Ezra's knee.

"Thanks, bud," Ezra said. He'd been saying that a lot lately. It still felt true every time.

Julian came over and sat beside him. For a minute he didn't say anything, which for Julian was unusual enough to be notable.

"You okay?" he finally said.

"Yeah." Ezra stared at his tea. "They were really alone, Jun. Whoever it is."

Julian was quiet. Then: "We know they're there now."

"They've been there for twenty-two days."

"Yeah." He didn't try to make that better. Julian was good at not making things better when they weren't better yet. "But now there's a line between here and there. And if there's a line, we can follow it."

---

Sable came and sat on Ezra's other side. The three of them were quiet for a while — Julian with his notebook open, Ezra with his tea, Sable rolling her pendant between her fingers. Okonkwo was still in conference with Marcus and Pella across the room.

"Seven fragments," Ezra said. "The Covenant put them in seven different places. Separate. On purpose."

"That's what it looks like," Sable said.

"Why would you do that? If you were building something—if you were making something meant to help people—why scatter the pieces?"

Julian looked up. "To make sure no one person could have all of it."

Sable turned the pendant over. "Or to make sure the people who found them had to find each other."

That sat in the room for a moment.

"Whoever is at Bryn Dural," Ezra said. "They've been alone with theirs. Since before the silence started. Maybe longer."

"Probably longer," Sable said. "I was alone with mine for four months before I followed the pull to you." She paused. "It's different when you're alone. The fragment is the same, but you don't have anyone to ask what it means. You just carry it and try not to break anything."

She said the last part matter-of-factly, but Ezra heard something in it that hadn't been there before. Not quite a confession. More like a door that had been left slightly open.

Julian had gone still in the way he went still when he was thinking about something that required more than four questions.

"Then we go get them," he said.

Okonkwo's voice came from across the room. "Not *we*, not yet." She'd been listening. She said it without turning around. "But yes."

---

That evening, Declan wanted a full debrief.

He didn't call it a debrief. He called it "I do it? I do it?" which was *tell me about the going*, and he asked it about fourteen times over dinner until Dad finally sat him on his knee and gave him a simplified version. Someone far away. Signal like a voice. Ezra heard it. We're going to help.

Declan thought about this for a while.

"Lone-ee?" he said.

"Yeah, buddy," Ezra said. "Lonely."

"Hug?" Declan said, and looked at Ezra with his brown eyes in exactly the way that had, Ezra now understood, been helping him move mountains all along.

"Not yet," Ezra said. "We have to get there first."

"I do it," Declan said, which meant: exactly, so let's go.

Ezra looked at Julian across the dinner table. Julian raised one eyebrow. Sable, sitting at the corner of the table, looked at her bowl like she was pretending not to have heard any of this, but the corner of her mouth had moved in a direction that was not down.

Dad cleared the plates. Ollie checked the floor for anything that had fallen, which was his version of helping with cleanup.

Outside, the crystal spire pulsed once, slow and steady, the way a heartbeat does when it's calm.

And somewhere six light-years away, through the dark and the cold and the silence, a fragment waited.
