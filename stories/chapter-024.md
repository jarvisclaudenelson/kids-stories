# Chapter 24: First Contact

The group slept in the Cradle, or tried to.

Petra had three hours against the wall. Rafe managed two on his folded jacket. Noa kept her headphones on and woke up every twenty minutes to check the signal, which had gone quiet and steady — the held breath between a question asked and an answer given. Julian did not sleep at all. He sat with his notebook open in his lap and wrote nothing in it.

Declan was still awake when the colony clocks turned to 5 AM. He had not moved from the dais since midnight. The seven fragments blazed above him in their slow ring, steady as firelight.

"Decker," Ezra said, sitting up. "Did you sleep?"

Declan shook his head.

"Were you here all night?"

A nod.

The gold fragment was warm and buzzing against Ezra's ribs. Not patient anymore. Not even alert. Something past alert, a state he didn't have a word for yet — a held readiness, like a breath before a jump.

"Okay," Ezra said. He sat down beside Declan on the cold stone floor.

Above them, the fragments turned, and the chamber hummed.

---

Outside, the sky was wrong.

Sable saw it at 5:17 AM when she stepped up to the rock shelf to check the plain. The northwest horizon — where Noa had tracked the echo, where the "eyes" had been burning brighter every night — was lit in a color that wasn't sunrise and wasn't anything else in the colony's known sky. Steady and massive and the specific combined color of seven fragments blazing at once.

She stood on the shelf and looked at it for a long moment.

Behind her, the colony was waking up. The generator's hum shifted. A door opened somewhere. Someone's voice, a question, then silence.

She went back down the stairs.

"They're here," she said.

She didn't have to say anything else.

---

The colony noticed at 6 AM.

Not all at once. These things happened in waves: one person out for morning water, stopped. Then they called to someone. Then two people stood in the lane between the residence blocks staring northwest. Then six, then thirty, going quiet the way crowds go quiet when everyone realizes simultaneously that the thing they're watching is real.

A shape. Slow, descending, enormous in implication if not in its actual visible size. Catching the early light in a way that metal doesn't.

Julian came up from the Cradle at 6:15 and stood on the shelf. He looked at the colony, then at the shape above the horizon, then at his notebook.

He had written one line during the night.

*We don't have a plan for this.*

"Julian." Rafe was beside him. "What do we do?"

Julian looked at sixty people watching the sky. Mom and Dad were in that crowd somewhere. People who had lived alongside this for two years without knowing any of it — no fragments, no Cradle, no Covenant, no Purge.

He closed the notebook.

"We get between them and the colony," he said. "The Builders see us first."

---

They spread across the flat red plain west of the settlement. Open ground, nothing behind them, nothing hidden.

Noa pressed both palms to the dirt, headphones on, the yellow-green glow running from her fragment through her fingertips into the earth. She'd stopped explaining what she was hearing and started translating it directly into directions — *left, hold, here* — and the group had stopped asking questions and moved.

The seven fragments blazed under the plain. All of them felt it from above.

"The sequence," Noa said. "Step one is active." She opened her eyes. "It's a question. I can't read the exact words. But the shape of it is clear."

"What shape?" Sable asked.

"Who are you."

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest.

The gold fragment answered.

Not him — the fragment, on his behalf, before his brain finished the thought. A declaration. *Here. This one. This world.*

Around him, one by one: Sable's hand went to her collarbone, Rafe's fingers curled, Petra looked down at her jacket pocket with an expression that mixed surprise and irritation in equal parts, Noa's glow brightened until it lit the dust around her knees.

At the center of the group, Declan spread his arms wide.

All seven colors rose above the plain.

---

It landed three hundred meters out.

No impact, no drama. A settling — the way a heavy bird takes a fence post. The craft was not large, and that was the first surprise; colony transports were bigger. Dark, smooth, shaped like nothing in any catalog Noa had studied. No visible seams, no landing struts. It sat on the red dust and did nothing.

Petra counted under her breath.

On forty-eight, the hatch opened.

One figure. Tall — taller than a person, but not by much. Roughly human in its basic architecture: two arms, two legs, something like a head. But the texture was wrong. Layered. Like sediment, or like a life spent close to very old things, and some of that oldness had settled on the surface. It moved like something that had been standing up for a very long time and no longer needed to think about it.

It looked at the group.

Each of them, one at a time, the way you read a sentence. Sable met its eyes without moving. Julian stood with his jaw tight. Rafe had his hands loose at his sides. Petra ate a snack bar, because she had decided — privately, the previous evening — that if aliens were coming she was going to face them fed.

When the Builder reached Ezra, it paused.

The gold fragment went bright.

It raised one hand and pressed it flat to the air between them — not touching him, not reaching. Acknowledging. A nod that said *Fragment One. I know you.*

The gold said back: *I know you too.*

---

Then the Builder reached into its own chest.

Not magically, not surgically. With the ease of someone reaching into a jacket — a practiced gesture so ordinary it took a moment to register as strange. It produced a small flat object. Rectangular.

Orange.

It held it out toward Declan.

Julian said, "That's the rectangle."

Nobody answered him, because everyone was staring at the object. It was the rectangle. The exact shape of what Declan had drawn on the whiteboard three days ago in orange marker, placed next to *Say hello* and *Be ready* without explanation, and which Julian had not erased.

Declan walked forward.

He stopped in front of the Builder and looked up at its face and looked at the object and reached out and took it. It was warm — Ezra could tell from the expression Declan made when he touched it. Not surprised warm. *Expected* warm. The warmth of something that had been waiting to reach the right hands.

Declan turned and looked back at Julian.

He held the object up.

"For the plan," he said.

Julian looked at the orange rectangle. He looked at his notebook. He looked at the whiteboard in his memory, the drawing in orange marker that had sat unexplained for three days while everyone tried to figure out what a two-year-old had known to draw.

"Yeah," Julian said. "Okay."

Rafe leaned close to Petra. "Did a two-year-old just—"

"Yes," Petra said. She ate the rest of her snack bar.

---

The Builder had not moved from its spot on the red dust.

It was patient in the specific way very old things are patient: no urgency, no impatience, no expectation of speed. Content to let what happened next take as long as it needed.

Behind the group, the colony had moved closer. Sable didn't turn around, but she felt sixty people on the edge of the plain — frightened and quiet and not yet running. That was something.

The orange object in Declan's hands was glowing now. Warm, steady. When he pressed it to his chest alongside the place where Ezra kept the gold fragment, the two colors overlapped and something in the air resonated.

"There are seven steps," Noa said. Her eyes were closed. She was reading through her headphones and through the ground both, glow running up her wrists. "Step one was identification. The fragments answered for us." She paused. "We passed."

"Passed what?" Julian asked.

She opened her eyes.

"The test," she said.

The silver-haired woman's voice, from the night before: *not all worlds who completed it are still intact.*

The Builder inclined its head — not at Noa, not at the group. At Declan. A specific acknowledgment, specific as a name.

Declan looked up at its layered, strange, unhurried face. He was two years old, and he was standing in front of the oldest thing any of them had ever seen, and he was not afraid.

"More?" he asked.

The Builder spoke. Not in words — in resonance. A sound that carried the shape of all seven fragments compressed to a single syllable, a sound Declan had been hearing in some form since before he could walk.

Ezra felt it press against his ribs.

The gold fragment said: *yes. So much more.*
