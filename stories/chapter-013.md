# Chapter 13: HD 34729

The Ariadne came out of jump at 3:12 in the morning, ship-time. The planet was right where Sable's chart said it would be.

It had no name. Just a number — HD 34729-c. Third planet around a yellow-orange star that looked a lot like the one Monscollis orbited, but older. The planet below was blue-grey with green at the poles. The clouds were thin enough to see through in places. A survey ship had flown past it twice, decided there was nothing worth building on, and left.

Someone had stayed.

Well — someone had ended up here. That was different.

Wren brought them in without saying much, which was how Wren did everything. She had a way of making landing on a nameless planet feel like pulling into a parking spot she'd used before. Ezra watched her hands on the controls and thought she and Sable had more in common than either of them would ever say out loud.

"Signal?" Rafe asked.

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest. The star was bright and moving, the way it got in the last few minutes before something big. Not danger. Not alarm. Something more like *almost.*

"Close," he said. "And pointing at us."

---

The fragment had been reaching out for three hours. Now that the Ariadne was in the air above the planet, Ezra could feel the signal the way you feel a voice when you've finally gotten to the room it's coming from.

Sharp. Present. Careful — but barely. The careful was loosening.

Whoever was at the surface had been holding themselves together for a long time.

Wren found a flat stretch of grey rock and set the Ariadne down clean. The landing legs sank half an inch into soft ground under the rock. She checked it, frowned a little, and said: "Stable."

Marcus had the door open before she finished.

Outside air came in — cool, heavier than Monscollis, smelling like rain that happened three days ago. The sky was low and the color of old silver. One sun, not two. Two of the planet's moons were at the horizon, pale and close together.

Ollie jumped out first. His nose went straight down — sweeping, careful — then he looked up in one direction and went still.

"That way," Julian said.

"Yes," Ezra said. The star agreed.

---

They found her on a ridge about four hundred meters from where they landed.

She was sitting on a flat rock with her knees up and her arms around them, watching the Ariadne settle. She had red hair, short on the sides. Pale skin gone a little tan from being outside. Practical clothes, a bit worn. Like she'd been stretching the same gear past when it was supposed to be done.

She was maybe eleven. Maybe twelve.

Around her neck on a simple cord was a stone that glowed a color Ezra didn't have a word for. Not quite purple. Not quite white. Something in between that shifted when you looked at it from different angles — like the inside of a shell.

She looked at Ezra, Sable, and Rafe — at the light coming from their chests in answer to her stone — and her face did something complicated.

She had been waiting for this. She had been afraid it would never come.

"Hi," Ezra said.

She said: "You're shorter than I expected."

---

Her name was Petra.

She'd been on HD 34729 for eleven months. Seven months before the fragment, four months after. She'd come with her uncle's survey team, and things had gone badly because one person on the team turned out to be working for the Shadow Hand. Nobody had figured it out until he tried to take the fragment right out of her hands and she ran.

"I didn't even know what it was," she said. She was walking back toward the Ariadne with them. Ollie was at her knee like they'd known each other for years. "I found it in a drill core sample and it was just — warm. Everything else on this planet is cold. So I put it in my pocket."

"And the Shadow Hand guy?" Marcus asked.

"He chased me for two days. Then he stopped." She looked at her hands. "I think I scared him."

"What did you do?" Rafe asked.

"I didn't mean to do it. I was cornered in one of the survey buildings and he was blocking the door and I was very, very angry—" She paused. "The whole wall lit up."

Julian, who had been quietly walking and not asking questions — which was very unusual for Julian — said: "What color?"

Petra looked at him sideways. "Purple-white. Why?"

"Just tracking." He made a note.

She watched him do this. "Who are you?"

"Julian. Ezra's brother."

"The tracker."

Julian looked up fast. "How did you know that?"

"I've been feeling you all through the fragments for three months," she said simply. "There's a very specific feeling when someone is tracking things. It has angles."

Julian stared at her for a long moment. Then he wrote something else down.

---

Inside the Ariadne, three glows — gold, amber, silver-blue — met a fourth.

Petra kept the fragment on its cord. When she sat across from Ezra, Rafe, and Sable and stopped keeping it tucked under her collar, the purple-white light found the others.

The chord changed.

Ezra had felt the two-note chord — amber and gold. He'd felt the three-note chord form at the crystal spire when Rafe's fragment joined his. What happened now he didn't have a word for.

Four notes. Different colors, different feelings — warm gold, warm amber, cool silver-blue, and now this: something clear and strange. It felt like the feeling you get when you see something impossible and realize it's actually real.

It wasn't quite music. More like what music is made of before it becomes sound.

Sable had her hand around her pendant. Her eyes were closed. "I can feel it this time," she said quietly. "Not just the change. The whole chord."

Rafe's amber fragment had gone very bright. He looked at it, surprised.

"Is this normal?" he asked.

"Nothing about this is normal," Sable said. "That's not a useful question anymore."

"What's a useful question?"

She opened her eyes. "What does yours feel like when it's near hers."

Rafe thought about it. "Like it's standing up straighter."

Petra looked at him. "Mine too." She had the look of someone who has been alone with something strange for a long time and is only now finding out there was a name for it.

---

It took about an hour and a half to get the whole story.

Petra had grown up on survey ships. Her uncle was a geologist who worked for three different colony companies. By age eleven she'd had more addresses than most adults have by fifty. She knew how to fix a hull seal, how to navigate by instrument and by eye, and how to figure out a jump window in her head.

She had been twelve when they landed on HD 34729. Thirteen when everything went wrong.

She'd been here alone for four months. The Shadow Hand agent had run after the wall incident. The rest of the survey crew had evacuated in the emergency ship — she hadn't been at the landing site when they left, and by the time she got there, they were gone.

She didn't sound bitter about it. She sounded like someone who had made peace with something that still hurt.

"I found the fragment in month seven," she said. "Before that I was just surviving. After—" She shrugged. "It helped. Hard to explain."

"You don't have to explain it," Ezra said.

"No, I want to." She turned the stone in her fingers. "Before the fragment, the planet felt empty. After, I could feel... something. Like background music you can almost hear. Not lonely exactly. Just—" She looked out the window at the grey-green planet. "Like something knew I was here. And didn't mind."

Nobody said anything after that. It was the kind of thing that shouldn't be filled in.

Declan would have asked if she wanted toast. Ezra thought about that and had to look at the floor for a second.

---

The funny thing was the name.

They'd been on the planet about forty minutes when Petra mentioned, sort of by accident, that she'd started calling it something.

"Called what?" Rafe asked.

"The planet." She looked a little embarrassed. "I know it's just a number. But I was here for eleven months and I needed to call it something."

"What did you call it?" Julian asked.

"Gerald."

Silence.

"Gerald," Sable said.

"I needed a name that felt dependable," Petra said. "Gerald felt dependable."

Julian wrote it down with the look of someone who wants to ask a follow-up question but is choosing very carefully. "Why not something like... Luminos. Or Vela. Or—"

"Those sound like they're trying too hard," Petra said firmly. "Gerald doesn't try too hard."

Ollie walked over and put his head in her lap with a sigh.

"See?" she said. "Ollie gets it."

Rafe looked at Julian. Julian looked at his notebook. He had written *Gerald* and underlined it.

"Fine," Julian said. "Gerald."

Marcus, who had not said a word during any of this, made a very quiet sound that was definitely a laugh that he immediately turned into a cough.

---

They had to leave fast — the window was less than an hour.

Petra packed in eight minutes. That was faster than anyone else on the Ariadne had ever packed, including Wren, which was saying something.

She had one bag. It wasn't big. When Ezra asked what was in it, she said: "Survey tools, three books, the fragment, and a piece of rock from Gerald that I took on day one."

"Sentimental," Rafe said.

"I was here for eleven months," she said. "I'm allowed one rock."

"You're allowed many rocks," Sable said. "You were clearly decisive about it."

Petra looked at her. "Is she always like this?"

"No," Ezra said. "Usually she's quieter."

Sable gave him a look that would have scared most people. He smiled at her.

Something in her face went soft for exactly one second before she put it away.

---

The Ariadne lifted off Gerald at 4:52 in the morning. Wren said nothing about the planet's name, which Ezra was pretty sure meant she'd heard it through the hull and quietly agreed.

The ground dropped away. The survey buildings, the flat rock where Petra had been sitting when they arrived, the drill site where the fragment had been waiting — all of it got small.

Petra watched from the window with her hand around the stone.

"First time?" Rafe asked.

"Leaving Gerald?" she said. "Yes."

"Does it feel how you thought it would?"

She thought about it seriously. "I expected to feel relieved," she said. "Mostly I do. But there's also—" She put her palm flat against the window. "I know it's just a rock. I know Gerald is just a number and the planet doesn't actually know I was there. But it kept me alive for four months, and now I'm leaving."

She watched until the planet was too small to see the buildings. Then she stepped back.

"Okay," she said, more to herself than anyone. "Okay."

Ezra reached into his chest pocket. He'd almost forgotten — it had been sitting over the star for three hours and the star had been busy.

He took out the piece of toast.

It was a lot more crumpled than when Declan gave it to him. At some point during all the walking and crouching and general adventure, it had become something that technically still looked like toast but was really trying its best.

He looked at it. He looked at Petra.

"This is from my brother," he said. "He's two. He gives things to people he thinks need something real and simple."

Petra looked at the toast.

"He wanted me to give it to you," Ezra said. "He doesn't know your name. But he knew you'd been alone."

Petra took the toast. She held it in both hands for a moment.

"Lone-ee," she said. She must have caught the word from somewhere, the way new people always picked up a word or two when they joined something.

"Yeah," Ezra said. "He said that."

She ate the toast. It was terrible — old and crumbled and had been next to a space fragment for six hours — and she ate every bit of it, and didn't make a face, and didn't say anything.

Sable looked out the window. Julian looked at his notebook. Ollie put his head on Petra's knee.

---

Four hours back to Monscollis.

Ezra sat with the four-note chord alive in the ship around him and tried to think about what they knew.

Four fragments. Four holders. Gold, amber, silver-blue, purple-white. Each one different. Each one chosen by the Covenant for something — Ezra's empathy, Rafe's focus, Sable's pattern-sense. And Petra, who had screamed at a wall and lit it up, who had named an unnamed planet Gerald, who had been alone for months with something impossible and figured out how to carry it.

He looked at her across the cargo bay. She was asleep with Ollie half on top of her, which Ollie had decided was completely fine. One hand curled around the stone.

He thought about the two yellow marks still left on Sable's chart.

And he thought about the Shadow Hand, who had a pattern reader, who had two fragments sitting dark and silent and useless.

Four good. Two taken. One unknown.

He pressed his hand to his chest. The star pulsed quiet and steady.

*Still here*, it said. *Still us.*

He closed his eyes and slept.

---

They landed at Monscollis at 8:36 in the morning.

The sun was up. The crystal spire caught the light and sent long pale lines across the clearing. Marcus had radioed ahead. By the time the Ariadne touched down, Okonkwo was there. Mom was there. And Declan was there — held in Dad's arms because he would not have stayed anywhere else.

When the door opened, Declan pointed at the new person in the group.

"New?" he said.

"New," Ezra said. "Her name is Petra."

Declan studied Petra with the careful look he gave all incoming people. He looked at her face. He looked at the pendant glowing quietly in the morning light.

He pointed at it. "Pretty."

"Thank you," Petra said.

"Gerald?" he asked.

Ezra blinked. He had not mentioned Gerald. Nobody had mentioned Gerald anywhere near Declan.

Petra looked at Ezra. Ezra looked at Declan.

"How do you know about Gerald?" Ezra asked.

Declan looked at him like that was a very basic question.

"Decker know," he said, and wiggled to be put down.

Dad set him down. He walked to Petra, gave her two pats on the knee, and walked back toward the main hall.

"Eggs," he announced to nobody in particular, and disappeared.

Petra stared at the space where he'd been.

"He's two?" she said.

"Two," Ezra said.

"Right." She looked at the crystal spire, at the glowing pendant, at the group around her — Sable and Rafe and Julian already walking toward the hall, Marcus and Okonkwo talking quietly, Ollie trotting after Declan. "Okay." She took a breath. "This is the team, then."

"This is the team."

She adjusted the strap of her bag on her shoulder.

"Good," she said. And walked in.
