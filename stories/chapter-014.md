# Chapter 14: What We Know Now

Petra had been on Monscollis for three hours when Okonkwo called them to the main hall.

The sun was fully up by then, throwing light across the colony in long warm sheets. Petra had been given a room in the south bunkhouse — the same row as Sable — and had spent approximately ten minutes in it before coming back out and sitting on the steps, watching the crystal spire catch the light.

Ezra found her there when he came back from breakfast.

"You've been staring at it for a while," he said.

"I can feel it from here," she said. "My fragment knew what it was before I figured out what I was looking at."

"It does that." He sat down next to her. The spire was maybe three hundred meters away, rising out of the center of the colony like something that had always been there and knew it. "When we first got here, after the crash, Declan walked straight to it. He was eighteen months old. He just walked to it and put his hand on it."

Petra looked at him. "What happened?"

"Nothing visible." Ezra thought about it. "But the spire pulsed. Just once. Like it recognized him."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "The Covenant picked you young."

"I think it picks whoever it picks," Ezra said. "I don't think it was supposed to be easy."

She made a sound that wasn't quite agreement, but was close enough.

---

The main hall at midmorning held a different quality than it did at meals — quieter, emptier, the light coming through the high windows at a useful angle. Okonkwo had arranged chairs in the loose circle she preferred, and Fletcher's debriefing notes were spread on the table.

Not Fletcher himself. He was in the colony's secondary medical building, which also served, currently, as the only holding facility. Okonkwo had not asked him back.

"We have a problem," she said, when everyone was seated.

She didn't elaborate immediately. This was her method — let the fact land before adding weight to it.

"Fletcher's questioning is complete," she continued. "He's consistent across twelve hours of interview. What he knows, we know. The issue is that what he knows has a shelf life."

Marcus leaned forward. "The pattern reader."

"The pattern reader." She tapped one of the pages. "Fletcher believes the Shadow Hand has had accurate fragment location data for at least two years. Possibly three. They knew about Monscollis before your ship arrived. They knew about Bryn Dural. They knew about Gerald."

Petra, beside Sable, went very still.

"How much ahead of us are they?" Dad asked.

"At Bryn Dural, they were weeks ahead. At Gerald, they were months ahead but couldn't get the fragment because Petra got it first." She looked at Sable. "That suggests the pattern reader is accurate but not unlimited. They can identify where a fragment will surface. They cannot identify who will pick it up."

"Because the Covenant chooses," Sable said.

"Because the Covenant chooses," Okonkwo confirmed. "Fletcher used the word 'frustrating' to describe the Shadow Hand's experience with this. I thought you'd want to know."

Julian, who had his notebook open, wrote something and nodded in quiet satisfaction.

---

There was one more thing, and Okonkwo said it plainly.

"Voss and Crane are still on Monscollis."

The room absorbed this.

"We've tracked them to the eastern work camp," she said. "They haven't moved in four days. At first we thought they were waiting for orders. Now we think they're waiting for something specific." She paused. "Fletcher believes the Shadow Hand will try to take a fragment holder rather than a fragment. The power won't respond to them — but it might respond if they have someone who loves the holder."

The room got colder.

"Leverage," Marcus said.

"Leverage," Okonkwo confirmed.

Declan was at the colony nursery for the morning. Ezra knew this because Mom had said it three times before they'd left the cabin, and also because Ezra had looked back twice on the walk over. He was fine. He was five hundred meters away and fine.

He pressed his hand to his chest anyway.

The star pulsed — steady, warm, not alarmed. Just there.

*I know*, Ezra thought. *I know.*

---

"What's Fragment Seven?" Julian asked.

He asked it the way he asked most things: directly, without preamble, with his pencil already on the page.

Sable had the star chart out on the table. She put her finger on the last remaining yellow mark — the sixth from Monscollis, by Ezra's count. The ones marked on the chart didn't correspond to order of discovery, just to position.

"This one." She tapped it. "I've never gotten a clean signal. HD 34729 — Gerald — was sharp and clear for three months before we went. This one has always been—" She searched for the word. "Scattered. Like a signal with interference. Sometimes strong, sometimes gone entirely."

"Could the holder be moving?" Rafe asked.

"That was my first thought." She traced the mark with one finger. "Moving holders are harder to track. The fragment syncs to its holder's heartbeat — which means if the holder is in transit, the frequency shifts."

"So the holder might be on a ship," Julian said.

"Or it could mean something else. Someone in a high-stress situation. Someone who hasn't fully accepted the fragment yet." She glanced at Ezra. "When did your signal start coming through clearly to other holders?"

Ezra thought about it. "I don't actually know. I didn't know other holders could feel me until you showed up."

"Point." Sable folded the chart. "The scattered signal could mean any of those things. The important part is—" She looked at Okonkwo. "The Shadow Hand's pattern reader would also be seeing interference. They might not know where this one is either."

For the first time in this conversation, Okonkwo looked something close to satisfied.

"Good," she said. "Then we have a problem in common."

---

Training had been moved.

The northern forest clearing was compromised — Voss and Crane knew it. Okonkwo had identified a new location, farther out, on the far side of the colony's water processing station. It was less picturesque than the clearing. The ground was hard and mostly flat and there was a smell from the filters that nobody acknowledged out loud.

"Better sight lines," Marcus said, which was a polite way of saying *harder to surveil without being seen*.

They started with Ezra, the way they always did, because his was the control now — the baseline. Ten feet from the center of his chest. He'd done it clean on the Ariadne, and he did it clean here, arm stretched out over the packed dirt, fingers splayed.

"Hold," Sable said.

He held. Forty seconds. The tingling started at forty-five — earlier than the last session.

"Okay." He pulled back.

"Nerves," Sable said. It wasn't a criticism, just a fact. "Your anchor's blurry today."

"I know." He had been thinking about Voss and Crane. He had been thinking about leverage.

"Small," she said. "Find the smallest thing."

He thought about Declan last night, eating dinner, getting a piece of noodle on his nose and being extremely offended about it. Not scared offended. Personally insulted. Like the noodle had done it on purpose.

The star steadied.

He stretched again. Forty-five feet. The tingling didn't start until he was past the nine-foot mark.

"Better," Sable said.

---

Petra went next.

She stood in the middle of the cleared area with her hands at her sides and her stone glowing its iridescent not-quite-violet under the pale Monscollis sky.

"I've never actually tried to make it happen," she said. "It just — went. When I was afraid."

"Fear works," Sable said. "It's not sustainable, but it works. We need to find the sustainable version." She looked at Petra. "When you lit the wall — describe what it felt like, exactly. Not what you saw. What you felt."

Petra thought. "Like my chest was too full. Like there was something that needed to get out and the only direction it could go was — out."

"Out, not inward. Not internal — external."

"Yes."

"Same as Rafe," Sable said. She glanced at Rafe. "Different material, but same direction. You're both external. Rafe moves things. You light them up." She turned back to Petra. "For Rafe, the anchor is attention — specific close focus on the object. What do you focus on when you're making art?"

Petra blinked. "I'm not — I don't really make art."

"Eleven months alone on a planet. You didn't draw anything? Write anything? Build anything?"

A pause. "I mapped it," Petra said. "Survey-style. All the ridges and rock formations. I made a map of Gerald."

"Okay." Sable nodded. "Try that. Map this clearing. Not physically. In your head. Start from where you're standing and describe it outward."

Petra closed her eyes. Nothing happened for twenty seconds.

Then the tips of her fingers lit up.

It was faint — barely visible in the daylight. But the iridescent violet-white was there, leaking out of her fingertips like something had finally found a small crack in a wall.

Petra's eyes opened. She looked at her hands.

"Oh," she said.

Julian wrote something down very fast.

---

Rafe, to everyone's surprise, was the one who made them laugh.

He'd been working on distance. He could move his amber fragment reliably now — lift it, rotate it, set it back down. Sable had brought a second object: a small flat stone from the path, unremarkable, not special.

"Something that doesn't already have a connection to you," she explained. "Your fragment is easy because you've been living with it. This is a test of whether the power transfers to objects you don't know."

Rafe focused on the stone. It lifted. Slowly, wobbling — not smooth like the fragment — but off the ground by two inches, and held.

"Good," Sable said.

He held it for eight seconds. Then it came down, and he let it come down.

"Again," Sable said.

He focused. The stone lifted. Higher this time. Three inches.

Then Declan appeared from behind Marcus — who had been watching him, Marcus was very clear about this afterward — walked into the middle of the training area, and picked up the stone.

"Mine," Declan said.

The stone, freed of both Rafe's focus and the ground, was lifted in Declan's fist.

Everyone stared.

Rafe stared.

"I—" Rafe started.

Declan examined the stone. He turned it over. He appeared to find it acceptable. Then he gave it back to Rafe with the straightforward generosity of a two-year-old who has decided something is interesting enough to share.

"Here," he said.

Rafe took it. He looked at Sable. Sable looked at Marcus. Marcus was looking at the sky with an expression of peaceful defeat.

"He walked past me," Marcus said. "He walked past me sideways. He's very fast."

"How long has he been here?" Rafe asked.

"Unknown," Marcus said. "He could have arrived any time in the last ten minutes."

"Did anyone notice a two-year-old walk into the training area?" Julian asked.

Silence.

"Incredible," Julian said, and wrote this down too.

Declan, apparently satisfied that the stone situation was resolved, pointed at Petra's glowing fingertips.

"Pretty," he said.

"Thank you," Petra said, for the second time in as many days.

---

Late afternoon, while Rafe and Petra were doing cool-down drills, Ezra found Sable standing near the outer fence, watching the colony.

She had her pendant out, turning it in her fingers. Not pulsing — just the default cool silver-blue it always held.

"The seventh one," Ezra said.

"Yes."

"You're thinking about it."

"I'm always thinking about it." She didn't look at him. "Five years ago, I was alone on Vela Station with a piece of rock and no idea what it meant. Now I have three other people who know what I mean when I try to describe it." She paused. "And the Shadow Hand has a plan that needs to go wrong. And the seventh fragment is scattered and I can't get a clean read on it."

"What does it feel like when you try?"

She thought. "Like trying to remember a dream. You know there's something there, but every time you look directly at it, it shifts."

"Could it be that the holder is a kid?" Ezra asked. "Like us. Like Rafe before he found us. Somebody who doesn't know what it is yet."

Sable was quiet for a moment.

"That's not a bad thought," she said. Finally.

He waited.

"In my first month with the fragment, before I started following the signal, I thought I was imagining it," she said. "The pulse. The warmth. I thought it was a physical thing — heat from a geothermal mineral deposit, maybe. I didn't accept it was real for—" She stopped. "A long time. And the whole time I was doing that, I was also a very inconsistent signal for anyone trying to find me."

Ezra looked out over the colony, at the long evening shadows and the crystal spire turning gold.

"So the seventh might just be scared," he said.

"The seventh might just be scared," she agreed.

There was something comforting about that. Not the fear itself — the recognizing of it. The fact that the Covenant found people who needed time. Who didn't understand yet. Who picked up a warm rock and spent months telling themselves it was geology.

"We'll find them," Ezra said.

Sable tucked the pendant back under her collar.

"Eventually," she said. Her version of *yes.*

---

Dinner was chaos, because four kids had trained for three hours and Declan had escaped into a training session and been given back and then fallen asleep on the couch in the main hall at 4pm and everyone had let him, and then he'd woken up at 5:45 extremely hungry and extremely interested in Petra and extremely concerned that she might not know the correct way to eat soup.

"Spoon," he told her, pointing at her utensil.

"I know," Petra said. "I have one."

"Spoon," Declan repeated, more firmly.

"Declan," Ezra said. "She knows about spoons."

Declan evaluated Petra's grip, her posture, the level of soup remaining in her bowl. He seemed to reach a cautious preliminary conclusion.

"Good," he said, and returned to his own bowl.

Petra looked at Ezra.

"He just needed to make sure," Ezra said.

"I gathered that." She took a careful, demonstrative spoonful. "Better?"

Declan looked up, checked her work, and nodded with the gravity of a quality inspector.

Rafe, across the table, was actively not laughing. His amber fragment gave a tiny pulse that Ezra was fairly sure was laughter.

"Can they do that?" Rafe whispered to Julian.

"I don't know," Julian whispered back. "I've been wondering."

---

After dinner, Okonkwo came to the cabin.

She didn't stay long. She had a thing she said standing in the doorway, with Marcus and Dad behind her and Mom in the room with the younger kids, and she said it the way she said things — no buildup, just the fact.

"We've moved Voss and Crane off Monscollis."

Ezra felt his shoulders drop about an inch.

"How?" Dad asked.

"Quietly," Okonkwo said. "Freight rotation. They're on a cargo runner that leaves the system tonight. They don't know we identified them until the ship is past the first jump point."

"And then?" Dad pressed.

"Then they know. And then they go report to whoever sends them somewhere else." She leaned against the doorframe. "This is not a permanent solution. But it changes the timing." She looked at Ezra and Julian and, through the doorway, at Rafe and Petra. "It gives you a window."

"For Fragment Seven," Julian said.

"For Fragment Seven," she agreed. "Before they regroup and send someone new, and before the pattern reader figures out where the scattered signal is coming from." She pushed off the doorframe. "I'd use it."

She left. The evening settled around them like it was trying to decide something.

Declan appeared in the hallway in his pajamas. He'd apparently been listening to all of this with no particular concern.

He pointed at Ezra.

"Boo?" he asked.

"Not yet," Ezra said. "Soon."

Declan processed this. He walked to Ezra, did the two-knee-pat, and looked up at him.

"You go," he said. "I stay. Tell lone-ee."

"I will," Ezra said. "I promise."

Declan seemed satisfied. He turned around, walked back to his room, and fell asleep with the specific efficiency of a two-year-old who has completed everything on his to-do list.

---

Later, when the cabin was quiet and the others had gone to their rooms, Ezra sat on the front step with Ollie's head in his lap and looked at the crystal spire.

The spire was dark now, the way it got at night — not glowing, just a shape against the stars. But every few minutes, in the very deep dark, it gave a single pulse of pale light. Ezra's star matched it, heartbeat for heartbeat.

He tried for the seventh signal.

He did it the way Sable had taught him — slow, patient, directional — not reaching but extending from the chest. Opening up rather than grabbing. He let his anchor settle on the ordinary things: Declan's pajamas, the sound of Rafe breathing through the door, Petra's map of Gerald, Julian's two-section notebook.

The star sent out its slow pulse.

For a moment there was nothing.

Then — faint, scattered, on-and-off like a signal bouncing off something — there was a flicker.

Not a voice. Not a feeling. Just a flicker.

But it was there.

It had the quality of someone who had just noticed a hand reaching toward them in the dark. Not pulling away. Not grabbing on. Just — registering it.

*I hear you*, Ezra thought, even though he didn't know if it worked that way.

The flicker came once more. Then gone.

Ollie shifted in his lap. He wasn't in alert posture. He was just there, warm and present and calm.

"Seven," Ezra said quietly. Not to anyone. To the dark and the stars and the thing they hadn't found yet.

He sat there a while longer before going inside.

Tomorrow they'd start.
