# Chapter 14: What We Know Now

Petra had been on Monscollis for three hours when Okonkwo called them to the main hall.

The sun was all the way up, throwing warm light across the colony. Petra had been given a room in the south building — same row as Sable — and had spent about ten minutes in it before coming back out to sit on the steps. She was watching the crystal spire catch the light.

Ezra found her there when he came back from breakfast.

"You've been staring at it for a while," he said.

"I can feel it from here," she said. "My fragment knew what it was before I figured out what I was looking at."

"It does that." He sat down next to her. The spire was about three hundred meters away, rising out of the center of the colony like it had always been there and knew it. "When we first got here, after the crash, Declan walked straight to it. He was eighteen months old. He just walked to it and put his hand on it."

Petra looked at him. "What happened?"

"Nothing you could see." Ezra thought about it. "But the spire pulsed. Just once. Like it knew him."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "The Covenant picked you young."

"I think it picks whoever it picks," Ezra said. "I don't think it was supposed to be easy."

She made a sound that wasn't quite agreeing but was close enough.

---

The main hall felt different in the morning between meals — quieter, more light through the high windows. Okonkwo had pulled chairs into a loose circle. Fletcher's notes were spread on the table.

Not Fletcher himself. He was in the colony's side building, which was also being used as the only holding room. Okonkwo had not asked him back.

"We have a problem," she said, once everyone was seated.

She didn't explain right away. That was her way — let the thing land before adding weight to it.

"Fletcher's questioning is done," she said. "He told the same story for twelve straight hours. What he knows, we know. The problem is that what he knows has an expiration date."

Marcus leaned forward. "The pattern reader."

"The pattern reader." She tapped one of the pages. "Fletcher thinks the Shadow Hand has had accurate fragment location data for at least two years. Maybe three. They knew about Monscollis before your ship arrived. They knew about Bryn Dural. They knew about Gerald."

Petra, sitting beside Sable, went very still.

"How far ahead of us are they?" Dad asked.

"At Bryn Dural, they were weeks ahead. At Gerald, they were months ahead but couldn't get the fragment because Petra got there first." She looked at Sable. "That means the pattern reader is accurate but not all-powerful. They can figure out where a fragment will appear. They can't figure out who will pick it up."

"Because the Covenant chooses," Sable said.

"Because the Covenant chooses," Okonkwo said. "Fletcher used the word 'frustrating' to describe the Shadow Hand's experience with this. I thought you'd want to know."

Julian, who had his notebook open, wrote something and nodded with quiet satisfaction.

---

There was one more thing. Okonkwo said it straight.

"Voss and Crane are still on Monscollis."

The room went cold.

"We tracked them to the eastern work camp," she said. "They haven't moved in four days. At first we thought they were waiting for orders. Now we think they're waiting for something specific." She paused. "Fletcher thinks the Shadow Hand will try to grab a fragment holder instead of a fragment. The power won't work for them — but it might work if they have someone the holder loves."

"Leverage," Marcus said.

"Leverage," Okonkwo said.

Declan was at the colony nursery for the morning. Ezra knew because Mom had said it three times before they left the cabin, and also because he'd looked back twice on the walk over. He was fine. He was five hundred meters away and fine.

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest anyway.

The star pulsed — steady, warm, not worried. Just there.

*I know*, Ezra thought. *I know.*

---

"What's Fragment Seven?" Julian asked.

He asked it the way he asked most things: straight, no warm-up, pencil already on the page.

Sable had the star chart on the table. She put her finger on the last remaining yellow mark. "This one." She tapped it. "I've never gotten a clean signal from it. HD 34729 — Gerald — was sharp and clear for three months before we went. This one has always been—" She thought for a word. "Scattered. Like a signal with something in the way. Sometimes strong, sometimes completely gone."

"Could the holder be moving?" Rafe asked.

"That was my first thought." She traced the mark with one finger. "Moving holders are harder to track. The fragment stays in sync with its holder's heartbeat — so if the holder is traveling, the signal shifts."

"So the holder might be on a ship," Julian said.

"Maybe. Or it could mean something else. Someone in a hard situation. Someone who hasn't fully accepted the fragment yet." She glanced at Ezra. "When did your signal start coming through clearly to other holders?"

Ezra thought about it. "I don't know. I didn't know other holders could feel me until you showed up."

"Right." Sable folded the chart. "The scattered signal could mean any of those things. The important part—" She looked at Okonkwo. "The Shadow Hand's pattern reader would also see the interference. They might not know where this one is either."

For the first time all meeting, Okonkwo looked almost satisfied.

"Good," she said. "Then we have a problem in common."

---

Training had moved.

The northern forest clearing wasn't safe anymore — Voss and Crane knew it. Okonkwo had found a new spot, past the water processing station. Less pretty. Ground was hard and flat. There was a smell from the filters that nobody mentioned out loud.

"Better sight lines," Marcus said. Which meant: *harder to sneak up on.*

They started with Ezra, the way they always did. His was the baseline now. Ten feet from the center of his chest. He'd done it clean on the Ariadne. He did it clean here — arm stretched out over the packed dirt, fingers wide.

"Hold," Sable said.

He held. Forty seconds. The tingling started at forty-five — earlier than last time.

"Okay." He pulled back.

"Your anchor is blurry today," Sable said. Not a criticism, just a fact.

"I know." He had been thinking about Voss and Crane. He had been thinking about leverage.

"Find the smallest thing," she said.

He thought about Declan at dinner last night. A piece of noodle had landed on Declan's nose and Declan had been extremely offended. Not scared-offended. Personally insulted. Like the noodle had done it on purpose.

The star steadied.

He stretched again. Forty-five feet. The tingling didn't start until after the nine-foot mark.

"Better," Sable said.

---

Petra went next.

She stood in the middle of the cleared area with her hands at her sides and the stone glowing its purple-white under the Monscollis sky.

"I've never actually tried to make it happen," she said. "It just — went. When I was afraid."

"Fear works," Sable said. "It's not something you can keep using. We need the steady version." She looked at Petra. "When you lit the wall — describe what it felt like. Not what you saw. What you felt."

Petra thought. "Like my chest was too full. Like something needed to get out and the only way was — outward."

"Same as Rafe," Sable said. She glanced at Rafe. "Different material, same direction. You're both external. Rafe moves things. You light them up." She looked back at Petra. "Rafe's anchor is close attention on an object. What do you focus on when you're making something?"

Petra blinked. "I don't really make things."

"Eleven months alone on a planet. You didn't draw anything? Build anything?"

A pause. "I mapped it," Petra said. "Survey-style. All the ridges and rock formations. I made a map of Gerald."

"Okay." Sable nodded. "Try that. Map this clearing. Not on paper. In your head. Start from where you're standing and describe it outward."

Petra closed her eyes. Nothing happened for twenty seconds.

Then the tips of her fingers lit up.

It was faint — barely there in the daylight. But the purple-white was there, leaking out like something had found a tiny crack in a wall.

Petra's eyes opened. She looked at her hands.

"Oh," she said.

Julian wrote something down very fast. Then he looked up and said, completely deadpan: "I'm adding a new column to the tracking chart."

"You have a tracking chart?" Petra asked.

"I have several."

Petra looked at Ezra. Ezra nodded slowly. She had so much to learn.

---

Rafe was the one who made them laugh.

He'd been working on moving objects. He could move his amber fragment reliably now — lift it, spin it, set it back down. Sable had brought a second thing to practice with: a small flat stone from the path. Nothing special.

"Your fragment is easy because you know it," she explained. "This is a test of whether you can move something you don't have a connection to."

Rafe focused on the stone. It lifted. Slowly, wobbling — not smooth like the fragment — but off the ground by two inches, and held there.

"Good," Sable said.

He held it for eight seconds. Then it came down, and he let it.

"Again," Sable said.

He focused. The stone lifted. Higher this time. Three inches.

Then Declan appeared from behind Marcus — Marcus was very clear about this later, he had been watching Declan — walked into the training area, and picked up the stone.

"Mine," Declan said.

The stone, freed from Rafe's focus and from the ground, was now in Declan's fist.

Everyone stared.

Rafe stared.

"I—" Rafe started.

Declan examined the stone carefully. Turned it over. Found it acceptable. Then gave it back to Rafe with the generous attitude of a two-year-old who has decided something is interesting enough to share.

"Here," he said.

Rafe took it. He looked at Sable. Sable looked at Marcus. Marcus was looking at the sky with the expression of someone who has accepted his life choices.

"He walked past me sideways," Marcus said. "He's very fast."

"How long has he been here?" Rafe asked.

"Unknown," Marcus said. "Could have been any time in the last ten minutes."

"Did anyone notice a two-year-old walk into the training area?" Julian asked.

Silence.

"Incredible," Julian said, and wrote it down.

Declan, happy that the stone situation was settled, pointed at Petra's glowing fingertips.

"Pretty," he said.

"Thank you," Petra said, for the second time in two days.

---

Late afternoon, while Rafe and Petra did cool-down drills, Ezra found Sable standing near the outer fence, watching the colony.

She had her pendant out, turning it in her fingers. Silver-blue, its default color.

"The seventh one," Ezra said.

"Yes."

"You're thinking about it."

"I'm always thinking about it." She didn't look at him. "Five years ago, I was alone on Vela Station with a piece of rock and no idea what it meant. Now I have three other people who know what I mean when I try to describe it." She paused. "And the Shadow Hand has a plan that needs to go wrong. And Fragment Seven is scattered and I can't get a clean read on it."

"What does it feel like when you try?"

She thought. "Like trying to remember a dream. You know something's there, but every time you look directly at it, it shifts."

"Could the holder be a kid?" Ezra asked. "Like us. Like Rafe before he found us. Somebody who doesn't know what it is yet."

Sable was quiet for a moment.

"That's not a bad thought," she said.

He waited.

"In my first month with the fragment, before I started following the signal, I thought I was imagining it," she said. "The pulse. The warmth. I told myself it was heat from a mineral deposit. I didn't accept it was real for—" She stopped. "A long time. And the whole time, I was a very messy signal for anyone trying to find me."

Ezra looked out at the colony. At the crystal spire turning gold in the evening light.

"So maybe Fragment Seven is just scared," he said.

"Maybe Fragment Seven is just scared," she agreed.

There was something comforting about that. Not the fear itself — the understanding of it. The Covenant chose people who needed time. Who didn't understand yet. Who picked up a warm rock and spent months telling themselves it was geology.

"We'll find them," Ezra said.

Sable tucked the pendant under her collar.

"Eventually," she said. Her version of *yes.*

---

Dinner was chaos.

Four kids had trained for three hours. Declan had escaped into the training session and been returned and then fallen asleep on the main hall couch at four o'clock and everyone had let him. He woke up at 5:45 very hungry and very interested in Petra and very worried that she might not know the right way to eat soup.

"Spoon," he told her, pointing at her utensil.

"I know," Petra said. "I have one."

"Spoon," Declan repeated, more firmly.

"Declan," Ezra said. "She knows about spoons."

Declan looked at Petra's grip, her posture, the soup left in her bowl. He made a careful decision.

"Good," he said, and went back to his own bowl.

Petra looked at Ezra.

"He just needed to check," Ezra said.

"I gathered that." She took a careful, very proper spoonful. "Better?"

Declan looked up, inspected her work, and nodded like someone whose job it was to know about spoons.

Rafe, across the table, was working very hard not to laugh. His amber fragment gave a tiny pulse that Ezra was pretty sure was a giggle.

"Can fragments laugh?" Rafe whispered to Julian.

"I have no idea," Julian whispered back. "I've been wondering."

---

After dinner, Okonkwo came to the cabin.

She didn't stay long. She stood in the doorway with Marcus and Dad behind her, and she said what she had to say the way she always did — no build-up, just the fact.

"We've moved Voss and Crane off Monscollis."

Ezra felt his shoulders drop an inch.

"How?" Dad asked.

"Quietly," Okonkwo said. "Freight rotation. They're on a cargo ship leaving the system tonight. They won't know we spotted them until the ship is past the first jump point."

"And then?" Dad pressed.

"Then they know. And then they go report to whoever sends them somewhere else." She leaned on the doorframe. "Not a permanent fix. But it changes the timing." She looked at Ezra and Julian and, through the door, at Rafe and Petra. "It gives you a window."

"For Fragment Seven," Julian said.

"For Fragment Seven," she agreed. "Before they regroup, and before the pattern reader figures out where the scattered signal is." She pushed off the doorframe. "I'd use it."

She left. The evening settled around them.

Declan appeared in the hallway in his pajamas. He had clearly been listening to all of this with no particular concern.

He pointed at Ezra.

"Boo?" he asked.

"Not yet," Ezra said. "Soon."

Declan thought about this. He walked to Ezra, did the two-knee-pat, and looked up.

"You go," he said. "I stay. Tell lone-ee."

"I will," Ezra said. "I promise."

Declan seemed satisfied. He turned around, walked back to his room, and fell asleep with the efficiency of a two-year-old who had finished everything on his to-do list.

---

Later, when the cabin was quiet and the others had gone to bed, Ezra sat on the front step with Ollie's head in his lap and looked at the crystal spire.

The spire was dark now — no glow, just a shape against the stars. But every few minutes, in the deep dark, it gave a single pale pulse. Ezra's star matched it, heartbeat for heartbeat.

He tried for the seventh signal.

He did it the way Sable had taught him — slow, patient, not grabbing but opening up. He let his anchor settle on ordinary things: Declan's pajamas, the sound of Rafe breathing through the door, Petra's map of Gerald, Julian's two-section notebook.

The star sent out its slow pulse.

For a moment there was nothing.

Then — faint, scattered, flickering on and off like a signal bouncing off something — there was a light.

Not a voice. Not a feeling. Just a flicker.

But it was there.

It felt like someone who had just noticed a hand reaching toward them in the dark. Not pulling away. Not grabbing on. Just — noticing it.

*I hear you*, Ezra thought, even though he didn't know if that worked.

The flicker came once more. Then it was gone.

Ollie shifted in his lap. He wasn't on alert. Just there, warm and calm.

"Seven," Ezra said quietly. To the dark and the stars and the thing they hadn't found yet.

He sat there a while before going inside.

Tomorrow they'd start.
