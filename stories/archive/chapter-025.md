# Chapter 25: Six Steps

Nobody moved for thirty seconds.

The Builder stood on the red dust, unhurried, its layered surface catching the early light in patches. It had the patience of something that had been doing this for a very long time and knew what came next. The group did not know what came next.

Noa had both palms pressed flat to the ground, the yellow-green glow running up her wrists. Her headphones were on. She was reading.

"Step two," she said. "It's there. I can feel the shape."

"What's the shape?" Sable said.

"I can't decode the words yet." She pressed harder. "It's not a question this time. More like an instruction."

Julian opened his notebook. "Okay. So step one was 'who are you.' We answered with the fragments." He wrote that down. "Step two is an instruction. Do we have any guesses?"

"We don't guess," Sable said.

"We could guess," Petra said.

"We don't guess about alien contact protocols."

"I have guesses," Petra said.

Sable looked at her.

"I'm just noting that guesses exist," Petra said, and ate a piece of her snack bar.

Declan had not moved from his spot three meters ahead of the group, in front of the Builder. He was still holding the orange rectangle with both hands, pressed lightly to his chest. His face had the look it got when he was listening to something far away.

---

The colony crowd had edged forward by another thirty meters.

Ezra noticed because one of the people who had moved was Dad. Not close — still sixty meters behind the group, in the front line of the gathered colonists. But he had moved from his first position to a new one, a slow drift forward, the way people do when they can't fully stop themselves.

Mom was beside him. She had stopped moving. Her arms were crossed.

Ezra knew that posture. It was the one she got when she was deciding whether to intervene in something and coming down on the side of *not yet*.

He turned back to the Builder.

The gold fragment was running hard against his ribs. Not distressed — working. Like the difference between a sprint and a run: both fast, but one had purpose and the other had panic, and this was purpose. He had learned that over two years with the fragment, and he trusted it.

The Builder's gaze was back on the group now. It had settled on Noa.

She looked up from the ground, her glow still lit.

"It's listening to me," she said.

"Yeah," Sable said. "What are you putting out?"

"Nothing on purpose." Noa glanced at her fingertips. The yellow-green ran in steady lines. "But the signal — the Covenant signal — it's going through me. It's been doing it since I activated in the Belt. I'm not sure I can turn it off."

The Builder took one step forward.

Just one, slow and deliberate, the way you step toward something you want to look at more closely.

Noa held still.

"Should I move?" she said.

"No," Sable said.

The Builder stopped. It pressed one hand flat against the air — the same gesture it had used for Ezra and Fragment One — and held it toward Noa.

The yellow-green glow leaped off her fingertips and ran upward, not through her, but through the air between them. For two seconds it hung there, a line of signal made visible.

Then it settled back.

The Builder inclined its head, the smallest degree.

"That was step two," Noa said. She sounded surprised. Her eyes were wide. "I think I just—" She looked at her hands. "I think I translated for it. I didn't do it on purpose. The signal just ran through and the Builder read it."

"What did you translate?" Rafe asked.

"The Covenant. All of it. Every fragment, every location, every Guardian." She pressed her lips together. "Like handing over documentation."

Julian was writing fast. "It needed to verify the record."

"I think so."

"We passed?"

Noa closed her eyes for two seconds, reading. "Step two complete."

---

Ezra nudged Declan's shoulder.

"Five more," he said.

Declan still had the orange rectangle to his chest. He looked up at his brother, then back at the Builder.

"Tell me what you're feeling," Ezra said.

Declan considered this with the seriousness of someone being asked a technical question. He tapped his chest once with the rectangle — not his fragment spot, but the place slightly to the right where the rectangle had settled against him.

Then he held his hand out flat, fingers up. Then he moved his hand forward, like pressing through something.

"Through," Ezra said.

Declan nodded.

"Something going through?"

Another nod.

"The signal?"

Declan thought about this. Then he tapped his own temple, his chest, the rectangle, and pointed at the Builder, in a chain.

"Oh," Ezra said. He turned to the group. "He's not just holding it. The rectangle is doing something. Decker can hear the Builder through it."

"Hear it how?" Sable said.

Declan held up two fingers.

"Two-way?" Ezra said.

A very slow, very serious nod.

The group looked at Declan. Then at the Builder. Then at the two-year-old who was apparently already in contact with the thing they had all been waiting for.

"How long?" Julian said.

Declan held up three fingers.

"Three what — days? Hours?"

Declan pointed at Julian's whiteboard. Then at the orange rectangle he'd drawn on it three days ago.

"Since you drew it," Julian said.

Declan nodded.

Petra set down her snack bar slowly. "A two-year-old has been in contact with an ancient alien for three days and nobody noticed."

"I noticed," Ezra said.

"Nobody said," Petra corrected.

"He didn't say," Ezra said. "He communicated. There's a difference."

Rafe looked at the Builder. "Does it know everything that's happened?"

Declan looked at the Builder. He waited one beat, then held up three fingers again.

"Three steps worth," Noa said. "Maybe."

---

Step three came at 8:14 AM.

Noa heard the shift in the signal before it happened — a change in the texture, something narrowing. She said "incoming" and the group stilled.

The Builder raised both arms out at its sides. Not wide, not dramatic. Maybe shoulder height. Held.

Seven lights answered.

Ezra's gold. Sable's silver-blue. Rafe's amber-orange, bright from his open hand. Petra's violet-white at her collar. Noa's yellow-green. The rust-red Fragment 6, which none of them wore exactly — it had come to Monscollis Colony on its own in Chapter 18 and settled loosely with the group, and it blazed now from the direction of the Cradle. And Fragment 7, the blazing yellow Rafe had pulled from the dead star, also down in the Cradle.

All seven. Even the ones underground.

The Builder's arms stayed out.

The seven lights climbed.

Ezra looked up. He had never seen all seven together in daylight, above ground, in the open air. In the Cradle they were warm and close. Out here under the Monscollis morning sky they went up sixty feet and lit the red dust and made long colored shadows behind every person on the plain.

The colonists behind them went silent.

"Step three," Noa said, very quietly, not to anyone in particular.

The Builder held the pose for a long count. Long enough that Julian's arm started to ache from holding his fragment hand steady and he switched hands without thinking about it.

Then the Builder lowered its arms.

The lights descended back to their bearers. Steady, unhurried, like something going back into a drawer.

"What was that?" Rafe said.

"Roll call," Sable said. "Every fragment, accounted for."

"In public."

"In public," she said. "On record. Above the colony, in daylight, with sixty witnesses." She looked back at the crowd. "The Covenant being complete is not a secret anymore."

---

From the rock shelf above the Cradle entrance, the silver-haired woman watched.

She had not moved since the group had walked past her and back inside the night before. She had stayed at the shelf through the night, through the 6 AM landing, through Step 1 and 2 and now 3. Her second operative was somewhere in the colony lanes — she had not checked since the landing. It hadn't seemed important to check.

She watched the seven lights rise and hang and descend.

She watched the Builder's arms lower.

She had been with the Covenant Purge for eleven years. She had seen the aftermath of two completed Covenants — not this one, not here, but elsewhere. She had read the records. The records were not good.

But the records also had not included a two-year-old.

That was new.

The Purge had studied every completed Covenant in the archive and every Guardian group they could document. They had never included anyone under the age of seven. They had never, in any record, included someone like Declan.

She pressed her back against the rock. The morning light was warming the shelf.

She was not sure the records applied here.

---

"Step four," Noa said.

It was 9:30 AM. She had come back from a bathroom break in the colony and they had brought water and Julian had eaten half a nutrition bar standing up, which he considered adequate.

The Builder had not moved.

"Another question?" Sable said.

"No." Noa pulled one headphone cup aside, listening with her actual ear and the fragment simultaneously. "It's different. It's—" She frowned. "It's waiting for us to ask."

Julian looked up from his notebook.

"Waiting for us to ask?"

"The signal structure reversed. The first three steps came from the Builder toward us. Step four is shaped like it's waiting for something coming from us." She put the headphone back. "We have to initiate."

"Ask what?" Rafe said.

"What we need to know," Sable said. She looked at the group. "If you could ask the thing that built the Covenant one question, what would it be?"

Silence.

Petra said, "Are we in danger."

Rafe said, "What are the other three steps."

Noa said, "What was the dead star's pulse."

Julian said, "Is the silver-haired woman right."

They all looked at Ezra.

He had known what he wanted to ask since the night before, or maybe since the Cradle chamber, or maybe since the first time Declan had said *Old Friend* with the specific certainty of someone naming something they already knew.

"Who is Declan," he said.

He hadn't meant it as the question — he'd meant it as his turn to answer the question. But the moment he said it, the gold fragment blazed white-hot in his chest. Not painful. Answering.

The Builder looked at him.

"That one," Noa said. Her eyes were wide. "That's the one it's been waiting for. The signal just — exploded. Step four is active." She spun to face Ezra. "Say it to the Builder. Out loud. Not to us."

Ezra looked at the Builder.

He looked at Declan, who was watching him, the orange rectangle held steady, his face unreadable and patient and two years old.

He turned back to the Builder.

"Who is Declan?" he said.

The Builder took three long steps forward.

It stopped in front of Declan.

It lowered itself to one knee in the red dust, which took a moment because it was very tall and it moved like something that had not knelt in a long time. It looked at the toddler at eye level.

Declan looked back.

The Builder pressed one hand to its own chest — the layered, textured chest, the oldest thing Ezra had ever seen — and inclined its head.

Declan pressed the orange rectangle to his.

A sound came out of the Builder. Not loud. Not frightening. A resonance — the same compressed syllable of all seven fragments, but longer this time, structured, like a name given carefully so it lands right.

Declan said, "Oh."

Like finding a sock you'd been looking for. Like: *that's where I left that.*

"What did it say?" Julian asked.

"He can't translate it," Rafe said.

"He knows," Ezra said. He was watching his brother. "He already knew. I think he just wanted to hear it said out loud by them."

"What does that mean?" Julian said.

Ezra pressed his hand to the gold fragment.

"I have no idea," he said. "But I think step four is done."

---

They ate lunch on the plain, because it seemed ridiculous to go inside and the Builder showed no sign of leaving.

Noa spread her notes on a flat rock. Six pages covered in diagrams and decoded fragments of the Builder signal.

Steps one through four: complete.

Steps five through seven: still dark. She couldn't read their shape yet. They were there — she could feel the structure — but they wouldn't resolve until each previous step was finished.

Rafe sat beside her and looked at the diagrams.

"Rafe," she said, not looking up. "Why did you ask about the dead star?"

He looked at Fragment 7, blazing yellow in his palm.

"Because that star pulsed once when we got the fragment," he said. "And then it went dark again. Something happened there and I don't know what."

"You think the Builder knows."

"It built the fragment. It put it there." He turned it over. "Yeah, I think it knows."

"You could ask," she said. "Step four is done. I think we can still ask things."

Rafe looked at the Builder, standing patiently thirty meters out, its layered face turned toward them the way you watch a group you are responsible for.

"Maybe after seven," he said.

"Maybe," she said.

She wrote *dead star — Rafe's question* at the bottom of page six and circled it.

---

Declan fell asleep on Ezra's jacket at 1 PM.

He had been awake for thirty-two hours — all of the previous day, all of the long Cradle night, all of the morning of first contact. The orange rectangle was still in his hand. He would not let go of it even in sleep, his fingers curled around the warm edge.

Ezra sat beside him, watching his little brother breathe in the red Monscollis afternoon.

The Builder had noticed. It had looked at Declan, then back at the group, with something that might have been patience or might have been recognition. It adjusted its stance — settled in, weight back, the posture of something willing to wait.

Sable sat down beside Ezra.

"Four steps," she said.

"Four steps," he agreed.

"The Purge is still on the shelf."

"I saw."

"She hasn't moved since last night." Sable looked at the rock shelf. "She's watching."

"I know." Ezra looked at Declan. "Do you think the Builder knows she's there?"

"It knows the silver-blue fragment went to her first," Sable said. "It knows every fragment's history from step two." She paused. "I don't know what it makes of that."

Ezra was quiet for a moment. Below them, in the Cradle, the six remaining fragments turned in their slow ring above the dais. Fragment One was up here, warm at his ribs, running steady.

"What do you think step five is?" he asked.

Sable looked at the sky.

"I don't know," she said. "But I notice the Builder keeps looking at all of us, not just Declan. It's not only here for the song-keeper." She glanced at him sideways. "It's here for all the things that gathered around him."

Ezra thought about that. About the years since Fragment 1 had shown up in his chest at age three, and every person and place and crisis since.

"That's us," he said.

"That's us," she said.

Below them, the Covenant Cradle glowed, the six fragments turned, and Declan slept in the red afternoon holding the thing he'd known was coming before any of them did.

Three steps left.
