# Chapter 27: The Mountain Door

The white flash faded like a dream you can't hold.

Ezra stood blinking in the returning daylight, the gold fragment steady against his ribs, the Monscollis dust still orange-red underfoot. Everything was the same. The Builder, rising slowly from its knee. Noa pressing her headphones tight. Petra counting something under her breath — ten, actually, her snack bar count, she'd later confirm.

The difference was inside.

The echo of shared memories still clung to him — every Guardian's moment of finding their fragment, compressed and vivid and then gone, like a song you catch the tail end of through an open window.

"Step six," Noa said. "The shape of it — it's a door. An actual door, I think."

The Builder gestured again toward the mountains. Purple, jagged, maybe four kilometers north across the plain.

Declan had both hands pressed around the orange rectangle, holding it to his chest. He was very still.

That was wrong.

Declan was never still while standing. He was always in motion — reaching, tilting, walking toward something whether or not anyone was ready. His stillness meant the rectangle was telling him something he needed to think about. He was thinking hard.

"Decker," Ezra said.

Declan looked up. His brown eyes were clear, not frightened exactly, but holding something careful.

He pointed at the mountains. Then held his hand flat, palm down, and moved it slowly — describing something large.

"Big?" Ezra said.

A nod.

"Something behind the door is big?"

Declan pressed his hand to Ezra's chest, where the gold fragment sat.

"Me?" Ezra said.

A slow, serious nod. Then Declan pointed at his own chest. Back at Ezra's. Then at the fragment specifically — that small motion meaning *that, exactly that.*

The gold fragment warmed.

---

Julian had the notebook open. He'd written: *Step 6 = doorway, mountains, 4km N.* Below that, a small arrow to a rectangle labeled DOOR and three question marks.

"I'm just saying," he said, "we should have a plan."

"You said that before step one and we had no plan," Rafe said.

"I had partial plans."

"The whiteboard had two entries and your brother drew a rectangle on it."

"The rectangle turned out to be extremely relevant."

Rafe considered this. "Fair."

Julian pointed his pen north. "Four kilometers of rough terrain, a two-year-old, and an ancient alien. I'm not saying I know what to do. I'm saying someone should write things down." He paused. "Also — has anyone seen Ollie?"

---

Ollie came from the colony at a flat run, ears streaming, scruffy gray-white fur catching the morning light. He had apparently decided thirty-two minutes was the correct amount of time to wait near the colonists before his Schnoodle brain said *enough* and sent him looking.

He ran past Petra — who grabbed a snack bar piece as a bribe but was too slow — past Sable, who stepped aside with the reflexes of someone who'd learned not to stand in his path, and straight to Declan.

He sat in front of the toddler and looked at him.

Declan scratched his ears. Ollie's tail moved once, deliberate, like a flag being raised.

Then Ollie looked at the mountains.

His tail slowed. Not fear — Ollie didn't do ordinary fear. His nostrils were working, sorting something complicated, running the full computation of whatever it is dogs do when the air tells them something they can't name.

He looked back at Declan.

Declan looked back at Ollie.

Then Declan started walking north.

---

The Builder fell in beside them, moving with the ease of something that considered distance a formality it was observing out of courtesy. Ollie trotted at Declan's left.

Ezra glanced back once and saw his father at the front line of the colonists, one hand raised against the sun. Not moving. Not calling out.

His mother had moved forward. Twenty meters behind the group now — not among them, but close. Following without interrupting.

He didn't look back again.

The rock changed as they neared the mountains. Red dust gave way to grey-black stone, flat and old, polished by something long gone. Cold seeped through his boots even in the morning heat. The air had a different texture — drier, thinner, with a mineral tang, the smell of rock that hadn't seen rain in a long time.

Three kilometers in, Noa stopped short.

"The signal changed," she said.

"Changed how?" Sable said.

Noa pulled one headphone cup off and pressed her bare ear to the flat black stone. The yellow-green glow ran down her arm and pooled at her palm, spreading in a thin sheet against the surface. "The spiral carvings. They're here too. Underground. Running all the way from the Cradle."

Sable went still, her pattern-sense extending. Ezra could always tell when she was reading — she got the expression of someone deciphering something just outside their line of sight.

"The carvings run *into* the mountain," Sable said. "Not along the surface. Deep."

"So the Cradle is one end," Noa said. She was back on her feet. "This is the other."

---

They found the door at the base of the third ridge.

It didn't look like a door at first. A section of cliff face that was slightly too smooth, too symmetrical, its surface covered in spiral carvings so dense they were almost solid. In the center, a depression: seven-sided, seven fragment-shaped hollows cut into ancient black stone.

"It's a lock," Julian said.

"Same shape as the Cradle dais," Ezra said.

The Builder stopped three meters back. It did not approach. It turned and looked at Declan.

Declan stood at the edge of the black stone, the orange rectangle held in both hands, looking at the hollow with that careful stillness.

Ezra crouched beside him. "What does the rectangle say?"

Declan tapped the hollow in the door. Tapped his own chest. Tapped the rectangle.

"You can open it," Ezra said. Not a question.

Nod.

"But?"

Declan looked at him. His brown eyes held that weight again — the thing that didn't belong to a two-year-old, sitting in a two-year-old's face.

He put his hand flat on his chest and moved it outward, toward the door. Something going somewhere.

"It costs something," Ezra said.

Declan pressed his lips together. His chin did the thing it did before he cried. He didn't cry. He held it.

A voice came from the rocks above the door.

"It doesn't cost him," the silver-haired woman said.

She stood on a narrow ledge, twenty feet up. She climbed down without hurrying and dropped the last four feet to the stone, landing easy. She held her hands open at her sides — not a threat, not a greeting. Something in between.

"It reminds him," she said. "There's a difference." She looked at the carvings on the door, not at the group. "We came here eleven years ago. Four of us. We found this door and couldn't open it. We didn't understand what it needed." She exhaled slowly. "The Purge thought a child at the center of this was a sign of corruption. Weakness. We thought the Covenant was being exploited." She finally looked at Declan. "I think we read the records wrong."

Declan looked at her for a long, still moment.

He did not shake his head.

He stepped forward and pressed the orange rectangle into the seven-sided hollow.

The carvings blazed gold-white. Light traced each spiral outward from the door, running up the cliff face and down into the stone underfoot, racing back toward the Cradle four kilometers behind them.

The door opened inward.

A breath of air came through — mineral, cold, deep, and underneath all of it something else, something warm that had no name but made the gold fragment ring like a struck bell.

Inside: dark.

Then, one by one, from somewhere deep in that dark, seven points of light appeared.

The same seven colors. Gold, silver-blue, amber-orange, violet-white, yellow-green, rust-red, blazing yellow.

Not theirs.

Ezra's breath stopped in his chest.

"Noa," Sable said, very quietly. "What's step seven?"

Noa was already reading, her face gone completely still.

"Step seven," she said, "is a meeting."
