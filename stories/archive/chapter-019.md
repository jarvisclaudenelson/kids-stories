# Chapter 19: The Final Piece

The rust-red fragment sat on the kitchen table.

Everyone kept looking at it.

It wasn't doing anything dramatic — just glowing a steady, deep crimson, like a campfire coal that had decided to be polite about it. But it was *there*, and it had been through the Shale Drift and an impossible trip across light-years, and somehow that made it feel like the most important object in the universe.

Declan was eating toast. He had already forgotten the entire adventure.

"More bwead," he announced.

Mom put another slice in the toaster without looking away from the fragment.

"We have six," Sable said. She had her map spread across half the table, the old star-chart with the hand-drawn circles. Six of the seven marks were now crossed off. One remained. A small yellow dot near the edge of the page, in a corner Sable had labeled "TBD."

"One more," Rafe said. He had said it four times in the last hour.

"One more," Julian confirmed, like he was checking a very important item off a very important list. He was absolutely checking it off a very important list.

---

The trouble with "one more" was that nobody knew where it was.

They set up in the colony's main research room after breakfast. Sable pinned the map to the wall. Petra pulled up the colony's navigation logs. Rafe stacked his notes next to Julian's notes, which was ambitious because Julian's notes filled two notebooks and a folded napkin.

"Fragment Seven has never appeared in any colonial record," Sable said, tapping the yellow dot. "The original Covenant scan only registered it as a weak signal. Outer edge of known space."

"That means beyond the Outer Ring," Petra said.

Julian wrote this down. "Farther than we've ever gone."

"Farther than most colony ships have gone," Sable corrected. "The star charts get thin out there. The navigation AIs call it the Unmapped Belt."

Noa, who had been sitting quietly with her headphones around her neck, looked up. Her expression did something complicated. "The Belt," she repeated.

Sable looked at her. "You've been there."

It wasn't a question. Sable's silver-blue fragment was glowing faintly. Pattern sensing.

Noa was quiet for a moment. "My family's cargo ship. We ran through the Belt once, about three years ago. Bad navigation jump. We were lost for six weeks." She paused. "There's something out there. I didn't know what it was. I thought it was just static. But the headphones picked it up."

The room was very still.

"A signal?" Julian asked.

Noa reached up and pressed one headphone to her ear. She tilted her head, listening to something nobody else could hear. Then she said, "It's still there."

---

The ship was loaded in two hours.

This was either very good preparation or extreme panic, depending on who you asked.

Julian had done a full inventory. Petra had mapped their route to the Unmapped Belt — six jumps, approximately two days of travel. Rafe had triple-checked the emergency kit because the last time they hadn't he'd spent three hours eating habitat ration bars that tasted like cardboard and regret.

Mom and Dad had a quiet conversation by the landing pad that ended with Dad nodding and Mom squeezing his arm.

"You come back with all seven," she said, looking at the kids. "Every single one of you."

"Obviously," Julian said.

"That means you too, Ollie." 

Ollie wagged his tail very seriously.

Declan waved at Mom from the top of the ramp like he was leaving for a short errand. "Bye-bye Mama! Get fwagment!"

"He is terrifyingly calm about everything," Petra said.

"He's two," Ezra said. "He doesn't know what 'terrifying' means."

"Neither does the universe, apparently, because it keeps sending terrifying things to a two-year-old."

Declan toddled past them onto the ship and climbed up into his booster seat.

"Go!" he announced.

They went.

---

The first two days of travel were mostly boring, which Ezra secretly appreciated.

He spent a lot of time near the window. Space looked the same out here as it did back home — dark, scattered with light, impossibly huge. But the closer they got to the Belt, the more he felt the fragment in his chest. A low, steady hum. Not uncomfortable. More like a reminder.

*Almost there.*

He held Fragment Six in his hand sometimes. The rust-red stone was warm and steady now, a deep pulse that matched the rhythm of his own breathing. The Rift Entity had given it to them clean, whole, ready. He hoped wherever the entity had gone, it was somewhere soft.

"Ezwa," Declan said, appearing at his elbow on Day Two.

"Hey, Decker."

"I feel it."

Ezra looked at him. "Feel what?"

Declan pressed one small hand flat against his own chest, right over his heart. "The song. It's getting louder."

Ezra felt the gold fragment pulse. Then the red one, warm in his palm. He pressed his own hand to his chest.

He felt it too. Like a chord building note by note, each fragment adding its voice.

"Six notes," Ezra said softly. "Waiting on the seventh."

---

The Unmapped Belt looked like someone had thrown a bag of rocks into a fog machine.

Rocky debris floated in slow, silent arcs. Drifting ice. Ancient asteroid fields with no names. The star charts showed empty space, but the reality was a cluttered, dim labyrinth that swallowed light.

"Navigation is degraded," Petra reported from the controls. "We're on manual from here."

Sable was at the sensor panel, her fragment glowing so bright it lit the side of her face blue. "Pattern sensing is..." She paused. "Strange out here. The patterns keep folding back on themselves. Like they're moving."

"The Belt shifts," Noa said. She had moved to the co-pilot seat, headphones pressed hard to both ears now. "The debris orbits a dead star at the center. The whole field rotates. You have to read it the right way or you just see chaos."

"Can you read it?" Sable asked.

Noa was quiet. Her eyes were closed. She was listening.

Then, slowly, she reached forward and began adjusting the navigation display. Not with calculations — more like she was sketching a path she could *hear*. Her yellow-green fragment, which had never done anything before, began to glow.

Julian pointed at it. "Noa."

She opened one eye. "I know."

"Your fragment—"

"I *know*, Julian."

The glow spread from the fragment up through her fingertips. The navigation display responded, lines shifting, routing through the debris field like threading a needle. A clear path appeared.

"There," Noa said, opening both eyes. She stared at her own glowing hands like they'd done something impolite without asking. "Huh."

"The Boost," Rafe said softly, remembering what they'd been told months ago, when Noa first joined them. "Your power. It finally showed up."

Noa looked at her hands for another moment. Then she pulled them back and crossed her arms. "It showed up when the universe needed it to. Very inconvenient. I'd prefer a manual."

Declan leaned over from his booster seat and patted her arm. "Good job, Noa."

Noa looked at the two-year-old. Some of the tension went out of her shoulders. "Thanks, Decker."

---

The dead star at the center of the Belt was not actually a star anymore.

It was a white dwarf — a collapsed remnant, small and dense and very, very old. It did not give off light the way living stars did. It sat at the heart of the Belt like a cold grey eye, surrounded by its ring of spinning debris.

Fragment Seven was in orbit around it.

Not on a planet. Not in a cave. Just... there. Floating in space.

It was bright yellow — not warm yellow-green like Noa's, but pure, blazing yellow, like the middle of a sunbeam. It spun slowly, trailing a faint arc of light behind it like a comet's tail.

"That's it," Sable breathed.

"It's not on anything," Rafe said. "It's just floating."

"It doesn't need a planet," Ezra said. His chest was blazing gold. All six fragments they carried were lighting up at once, colors blending into something warm and complicated. "It's the last one. It's been waiting for the others to come to it."

Julian checked his notebook. "We don't have a plan for floating space retrieval."

"I do," Rafe said quietly.

Everyone looked at him.

He hadn't said it with bravado. He said it the way Rafe always said things — measured, certain, a little quiet. He held up one hand. His amber fragment pulsed, and across the cabin, three empty water bottles gently rose off the counter.

Telekinesis. External object movement.

"That's a fragment," Petra said. "That's not a water bottle."

"I know." Rafe dropped the water bottles. He looked at the glowing yellow stone through the viewport. "I don't know if it'll work. But I can try."

"That," Julian said, writing it down with finality, "is a plan."

---

Rafe stood at the viewport.

The cabin was very quiet. Even Declan, somehow, was still.

Rafe breathed out. He thought about focus. He thought about how he'd moved rocks at Bryn Dural to find his own fragment in the dark. He thought about how the amber stone in his chest felt when he reached past himself.

He reached.

The amber fragment blazed. His hands went steady. Outside the viewport, the yellow fragment wobbled in its orbit. It slowed. It turned. Then, steadily, like it was walking across the room, it began to move.

It took three full minutes.

None of them breathed for the last thirty seconds.

The yellow fragment drifted through the cargo hatch they'd opened at Sable's command, across the interior air lock, and settled into Rafe's outstretched palm.

Blazing yellow. Warm as sunlight. Humming with a sound like the highest note in a chord that had been building for centuries.

Rafe exhaled.

"Got it," he said.

---

Declan started crying.

Not scared crying. Not hurt crying. The kind of crying that Ezra knew well — the kind that meant something was too big for two-year-old words.

"What is it, Decker?" Ezra crouched beside him, hand on his back.

Declan pressed both hands flat to his own chest. Tears were rolling down his cheeks, but he was smiling.

"The song," he said. "It's finished."

And Ezra felt it. The gold fragment. The rust-red one. All six they carried, plus the yellow one in Rafe's hand. Seven notes, all at once.

Not a battle. Not a signal. A *chord*.

It rolled through the cabin like a warm wave, soft and low, making the walls hum. Ollie's tail swept back and forth. Petra's fingertips glowed without her trying. Even Julian stopped writing.

Outside, in the Belt, the dead star pulsed once — a single beat of white light, like a heartbeat, and then it was still.

"Seven," Sable said. Her voice was rough at the edges. "All seven."

She held up her silver-blue fragment. One by one, each of them held up theirs. Seven stones. Seven colors. One complete, humming, blazing whole.

Declan wiped his face on Ezra's sleeve, blew a completely undignified bubble, and said, "Now we go home."

Rafe laughed — a real one, shoulders-shaking. Petra snorted. Noa smiled in that small, quiet way that meant she'd finally decided this was her crew too.

"Yeah, Decker," Ezra said. "Now we go home."
