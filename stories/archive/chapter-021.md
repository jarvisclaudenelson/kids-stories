# Chapter 21: What the Covenant Opens

The fragments had been humming for three days.

Not loud. Not dramatic. Just a constant, low, seven-note chord that you felt more than heard — like standing next to a large refrigerator, except the refrigerator was ancient and magical and located in a secret underground chamber beneath a red-rock plain.

They left one person on watch at all times. At night it was Sable. During the day, they took shifts.

Julian had put together a schedule. It was color-coded.

"You gave Declan a shift," Rafe said, looking at the chart.

"His shift is during his nap," Julian explained. "He's technically there if anything happens."

"He's asleep."

"He's present."

---

On the morning of the fourth day, Noa said, "I decoded something."

She was at the map table, headphones on, three different paper notepads open in front of her, covered in symbols that looked like a combination of math and music. She'd been awake since before sunrise, which no one knew until Petra came to the kitchen for water at 5 AM and found Noa eating cereal dry from the box with the focused expression of a person who had forgotten food was supposed to be hot sometimes.

Everyone gathered.

Noa spread her notes. The yellow-green fragment at her wrist glowed steady — it had been doing that constantly since the Cradle chamber was opened, like it recognized what the signal was for.

"The broadcast," she said. "The one all seven fragments are sending out together. I've been listening to it for days." She tapped her headphones. "It's not random. It's structured. It's got layers."

"Like a message?" Sable asked.

"Like an address." Noa pointed to a string of symbols on her top notepad. "I think this part is a location. Coordinates — not planet-based, but deep space. Something a long way out." She moved to the next notepad. "And this part—" She paused. "This part is a door."

Julian sat up straight. "Like the Cradle door."

"Like the Cradle door," Noa confirmed. "But bigger."

The room was quiet.

Ezra looked at his chest. The gold fragment hummed low and steady, the same as always. It didn't feel alarmed. It felt the way it felt when something important was about to happen — that low-level hum-plus-warmth combination that Ezra had learned, over two years, meant *pay attention, not run.*

"What kind of door?" Sable asked.

Noa hesitated.

That was notable. Noa did not usually hesitate.

"I think," she said carefully, "that the Covenant is a key that opens a door, and the door is also a question. Like — whoever made this wanted to know if we were ready." She tapped the third notepad. "And if we are, the answer comes from here."

She pointed at the fragments, glowing in their ring above the dais below them.

"The answer comes from the Covenant itself."

---

"We should go down there," Julian said.

"We just decided we needed to think about this," Petra said.

"I've thought about it."

"For thirty seconds."

"I'm a fast thinker."

Petra stared at him. "You once thought your way into a black-hole-adjacent asteroid field with no nav charts."

"That worked out."

"That worked out because Noa saved us."

"So Noa will come," Julian said, very reasonably.

Noa pointed at him. "Don't make me a verb."

---

They went down.

All of them, because at this point there was no such thing as a scout mission when it came to the Covenant. The fragments responded to all of them together. That was the deal.

The stairs still felt long. They still went quiet going down — everyone did, every time, because the air changed on the way. It got warmer and cleaner, and the kind of quiet down there wasn't empty quiet. It was full quiet. Like standing in snow.

The chamber looked exactly as they'd left it. Seven fragments in their slow ring above the dais, casting seven-colored light across the smooth black walls. The spirals glowing. The chord humming.

Declan walked in and immediately sat cross-legged in front of the dais like he was expecting a story.

"He does that every time," Rafe said.

"He knows something we don't," Ezra said. He sat down next to Declan, which made Declan scootch over to make room and then immediately lean into Ezra's side.

Sable walked slowly around the perimeter of the chamber. Her fragment blazed silver-blue, her pattern-sense working, reading the walls.

"There's new script," she said. "Here—" She stopped at a section of wall to the left of the entrance. "This wasn't glowing before. Not when we first came in." She pressed two fingers to it lightly. "It activated when the seventh fragment was placed."

The script was unlike anything on the walls above. The spirals up top were old — old the way the Shale Drift carvings were old. This was different. Sharper. More deliberate.

Noa stepped up beside Sable and studied it. Her headphones lit up yellow-green. She turned to the fragments on the dais, then back to the wall.

"It's the same pattern," she said quietly. "As the broadcast." She touched the wall, not with her fragment hand — with her regular hand, careful and slow. "Sable. Can you read it?"

"Not all of it." Sable's jaw was tight with focus. "It's like... hearing a conversation in a language you almost know. I'm getting the shape of things, not the exact words." She paused. "But there's one section. Right here." She moved her finger to the bottom portion of the script, where it curved and gathered into a single dense cluster. "I can read this part."

She took a breath.

"It says: *What remains when the song is whole?*"

The fragments flared — all seven, one bright pulse together, like an exclamation point.

"Oh," said Ezra. "That's a question."

"A question for us," Sable said.

"Or an answer," said Noa.

---

Julian paced.

He did this when he was thinking. He paced a tight circle around the outer edge of the room, hands behind his back, face set. Rafe watched him and did not say anything, because Rafe knew better than to interrupt The Pace.

Petra sat on the bottom stair and ate one of the snack bars she had started keeping in her jacket pocket since the Shale Drift mission. She'd eaten three of them over the past two days. She offered one to Noa. Noa took it.

"What remains when the song is whole," Julian repeated, mid-pace. "The song is whole when all seven fragments are together. We did that. The Covenant chord is complete. So what remains?"

"Us," Ezra said.

Julian stopped pacing.

Everyone looked at Ezra.

Ezra shrugged. "The fragments don't need us anymore. They're home. They're resting." He gestured at the dais. "We found them, we brought them here, we kept them safe. The mission's done." He paused. "So what remains isn't the fragments. It's us."

"It's a question about the Guardians," Sable said slowly. "Not about the Covenant."

"What do we do now," Noa said. Not like a question. Like a translation.

The chamber was quiet for a moment.

Then Declan, who had been silent so long that some of them had forgotten he was there, looked up from his cross-legged position on the floor.

"More," he said.

He pointed at the wall of new script — the whole wall, the long section they hadn't been able to read yet.

"More words," he said.

"You can read it?" Sable asked.

Declan looked at her like she'd asked if water was wet.

He stood up, walked to the wall, and put his small palm flat against the unread section.

The fragments pulsed.

And the wall started to glow — not just the new script. All of it. Every spiral, every carving, the whole chamber from floor to ceiling, warming up like embers.

Declan turned around with his palm still flat on the wall behind him.

"They're coming," he said.

---

"Who's coming?" Julian asked.

"Who built this?" Rafe asked.

"Who left the broadcast address?" Noa asked.

All three of those questions arrived at the same time, which was the loudest the chamber had been since they found it.

Declan looked at each of them in turn. He had the very specific expression he got when he knew something enormous that he did not have the vocabulary to explain — a look that was part frustration, part patience, part something older than two.

He pointed at the dais.

He pointed at himself.

He pointed at the wall.

"They made it for us," Ezra said quietly.

Declan nodded.

"And they want to know," Sable said, working it out like reading a chart, "if we're here. If we found it. If we finished the song."

Declan pointed at the fragments. "Song," he confirmed. Then he pointed upward, toward the sky above the stone ceiling. "Song goes up." He spread his fingers wide. "Goes far."

He mimed something flying away with both hands, very seriously, which on a normal day would have looked extremely funny but today nobody laughed.

"The broadcast reached them," Noa said. She pulled her notepad from her jacket. The coordinates she had decoded stared back at her. "And they're on their way back."

"The people who built the Covenant," Sable said.

"The people who made the fragments," Rafe said.

Petra, who had been quiet through all of this, swallowed the last of her snack bar, crumpled the wrapper carefully, and put it back in her pocket.

"How long?" she asked. "How long until they arrive?"

Declan held up both hands.

Ten fingers.

"Ten days," Sable said.

Declan nodded.

Then he walked back to his spot on the floor in front of the dais, sat down cross-legged again, and looked expectantly at the fragments like a kid settling in for a very good show.

"Right," said Petra. She stood up from the stair. "Then we have ten days to stop looking confused and figure out what we're actually going to say."

---

That night, Ezra lay in his bunk and pressed his hand to his chest.

The gold fragment hummed. The same as always — steady, warm, certain. Like it knew exactly what came next and felt completely fine about it.

Ezra did not feel completely fine about it.

He felt about thirty-seven different things at once, which was pretty normal for him, but tonight they were louder than usual. The Covenant was whole. The mission they'd been on since the beginning — find the fragments, keep them safe, bring them together — was done. Something new was starting. Something none of them had planned for.

He stared at the ceiling.

"What do you think they're like?" he asked.

Julian, in the bunk above him, was quiet for a moment.

"Probably smart," Julian said. "Smarter than us, probably. They built the whole Covenant."

"Does that scare you?"

Another pause. Longer this time.

"A little," Julian said. Which was a lot, coming from Julian.

Ezra nodded at the ceiling. "Me too."

From down the hall, they heard Declan's room go quiet — the small rustling sounds that meant he'd finally fallen asleep.

The colony generator hummed outside.

Somewhere below them, deep in the stone, the Covenant chord held steady.

Ten days.

Ezra closed his eyes.
