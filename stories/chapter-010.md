# Chapter 10: Bryn Dural

The decision to go took two more days and about six hundred conversations.

Ezra wasn't involved in most of them. The adults talked in Okonkwo's office, in the main hall, in low voices after the kids were supposed to be asleep. He lay in his bunk listening to the murmur and tried to read what it meant from tone alone. It sounded like logistics. Like people working through something they'd already decided.

Julian had made a chart. He brought it to breakfast — a full page of columns for travel time, ship class, relay coverage, and something he called his "shadow hand proximity index," explained with a confidence Ezra found both impressive and slightly worrying.

"The colony has a survey runner," Julian said. "Needs a crew of four. I already looked."

Dad put down his fork. "When did you—"

"During the quiet hours," Julian said. That meant 2 a.m.

The survey runner was called the *Ariadne*. Ezra went to see it the afternoon before they left. Sable followed, which he was starting to expect. It was docked at the colony landing pad — compact, angular, a ship that had kept going not because it was well-maintained but because it was too stubborn to quit.

"Are you scared?" Sable asked.

"A little."

"Good. It means you're taking it seriously." She rolled her pendant between her fingers. "I counted my steps down to my fragment at Vela Station. Made it to a hundred and twelve."

"How long was the tunnel?"

"Maybe sixty feet."

Ezra laughed. The first real laugh he'd had in two days.

---

They left at dawn on the third day.

Full crew: Ezra, Julian, Sable, Marcus, and a colony pilot named Wren — a compact woman with close-cropped grey hair who said twelve words in the first hour, which Sable appeared to respect enormously. Captain Okonkwo stayed behind to run the investigation. Mom and Dad stayed with Declan.

The goodbye was harder than Ezra expected. Declan didn't cry. He stood at the edge of the landing pad in his too-large boots, held out his carved wooden Ollie figure, and pushed it into Ezra's hands.

"You take," he said. "It goes wif you."

"Decky, it's yours—"

"You take it." Completely firm. Then: "You bring it back."

Ezra looked at the small carved dog. Someone in the colony had made it with real care — the curly ears, the scruffy body, the tail mid-wag.

"Deal," he said.

Mom hugged him for a long time. Dad told Julian to look out for his brother, and Julian, to his credit, just said "I know." Ollie trotted up the ramp like he'd always been going.

---

The first three days in space were uneventful in the way that space travel is always uneventful: cramped, strange-smelling, loud with engine hum, and repetitive in ways that would have been boring if Ezra weren't still slightly in disbelief that any of this was happening.

Julian worked at the cargo bay table for roughly the first day and a half without stopping. Sable slept efficiently, like she knew exactly how much rest she needed and took it without apology. Wren flew the ship and occasionally said things like "coasting phase in four hours" and "recommend securing loose items," all with exactly the same tone.

Ollie found a warm spot behind the engine housing, claimed it as his, and was there every time anyone looked.

On day two, Julian found a loose bolt rolling under the table. He spent two hours figuring out where it came from, replaced it, and showed Wren. He looked slightly smug about it for the rest of the day.

"You just fixed our ship," Ezra said.

"I tightened one bolt," Julian said. "But yes."

---

On day four, Ezra's star woke up.

Not in a threatening way. A shift — the way you know when you've crossed into a different kind of country, even if the road looks the same. He was lying in his bunk when the fragment oriented itself, a slow quarter-turn, like a compass finding something.

He got up and went to the cockpit. Wren was there, as always.

"You felt it," she said, without turning.

"How did you—"

"Your dog went still in the cargo bay twenty minutes ago." She tapped the coordinate display. "Bryn Dural system boundary. Another two days to the planet."

Ezra sat in the co-pilot seat. His star kept doing the quarter-turn — slow, searching, certain.

"It's different from the relay," he said. "That was like a voice cutting out. This is steady. Like it knows I'm close."

"Sit with it," Wren said. "You don't have to do anything yet."

So he sat. In the dark, watching the coordinates shift, while the star said *closer, closer* in the only language it had.

---

The planet was grey-green and overcast. Three moons — too small to be impressive and too numerous to ignore. The *Ariadne* descended through the cloud layer and Ezra pressed his face to the porthole trying to understand what he was looking at.

"Habitat is underground," Wren said. "Life support still running. Colony registration: fifteen people."

"Twenty-five days now," Sable said.

The habitat entrance was a reinforced door set into a cliff face, half-buried in grey sediment from a recent slide. The exterior antenna array was snapped clean off, lying in pieces on the ground.

"Rockslide," Marcus said. "Not Shadow Hand. A hill fell on their antenna."

Julian stared at the wreckage. "Twenty-five days."

Ollie was already at the door, nose working, tail moving — not alarm. Recognition. He looked back at Ezra in the way that meant *there's something here you need to know.*

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest and extended the star — not reaching, just present. *I'm here. If you're there, this is where I am.*

The response came in less than two seconds. Not the fraying signal from the relay. Something immediate. Still worn. But *there*, certain, and underneath it the warmth of someone who had been waiting and had not stopped hoping.

"They're okay," Ezra said. His voice cracked on the last word. He wiped his face, embarrassed, and noticed Julian looking somewhere deliberately else — his version of giving you a moment.

Sable handed him the access codes.

"Open the door," she said.

---

Inside was warmth, and light, and the smell of fifteen people who had been in a small space for a long time being very glad to see other people. All fifteen of them had opinions about this, and several were expressed at the same time and at volume.

The team leader was Dr. Ingrid Baas, tall and calm in the specific way of someone who had been calm on behalf of other people for twenty-five days and was very ready to hand that job to someone else. She shook Marcus's hand for a long time and didn't let go immediately.

"The slide took the antenna and the secondary transmitter in the same hour," she said. "We had nothing that could reach the relay." She looked at her team — exhausted, mostly fine, the particular look of people who had been careful with what they had. "Nobody's hurt. But I have twelve people who will not be happy about powdered vegetables for the rest of their natural lives."

"Tell them to complain to me directly," Marcus said.

"They will."

---

The fragment-holder was a boy named Rafe.

He was twelve, which Ezra had not expected. He sat at the back of the habitat and stood when Ezra came in — very still, watching. Red hair. Pale skin with dark freckles. Dark eyes doing the thing Ezra's did sometimes, seeing slightly ahead of what everyone else was seeing.

He was wearing the fragment on a cord: a small warm amber-orange stone that caught the habitat light and held it.

Ezra walked through the crowd. His star was doing something he hadn't felt before — not the synchronized pulse from meeting Sable, but something bigger. Three notes. A chord.

"You heard me," Rafe said.

"I heard you at the crystal spire first," Ezra said. "Then we got closer and it got clearer." He stopped in front of him. "How long have you had it?"

"Seven months. The drill team found the deposit. Everyone else walked away. I just—" He shrugged. "I couldn't."

Sable pulled out her pendant. Three fragments in proximity: Ezra's gold, Sable's silver-blue, Rafe's amber. Three different temperatures of light, all pulsing together — not in unison but in rhythm, the way instruments stay in the same key even when they play different notes.

Julian had his notebook out. "That's three. Plus the two the Shadow Hand has." He looked up. "More than half."

Rafe blinked. "The Shadow Hand has two?"

"We'll explain," Sable said. "But not here. Let's get everyone out first."

---

Getting fifteen survey team members into a ship designed for four took forty minutes and generated creative solutions, some of which were Julian's.

Ollie was extremely helpful. He circulated through the crowd sitting beside anyone who looked like they needed a moment, which was most of them. Dr. Baas crouched and let Ollie put his head in her hands and stayed there a few seconds longer than necessary.

At the hatch, Rafe stopped and looked back at the cliff face.

"Forty-seven steps," he said. "From the back of the habitat to the door. I counted them every morning. Never stopped, because stopping meant giving up." He paused. "I lost count of how many mornings."

He stepped onto the ship.

The flight back took eleven days. Louder. More cramped. Wren added two words to her daily vocabulary average.

On the third day, Rafe sat at the cargo bay table with the others and told them what he knew. Seven months alone with the fragment. No signal, no pull — just the warm stone and the knowing. The Shadow Hand came twice looking for it. He'd hidden it in the habitat. The rockslide came the day before their third visit.

"The slide protected you," Julian said.

"Maybe." Rafe shrugged. "Or maybe it was just weather."

He looked at Ezra. "Whatever's next — I want to help."

Ezra looked at the amber stone. At Rafe's face — someone who'd carried something enormous alone for seven months and was very ready to stop doing that.

"You're already helping," Ezra said. "Being here. That's it."

---

Monscollis at dawn. The crystal spire catching first light. Mom and Dad and Declan at the edge of the landing pad, Declan wound so tight with waiting he was practically vibrating.

When the ramp came down, Declan broke loose from Mom's hand and ran directly to Ollie, which was extremely on-brand. He buried his face in the scruffy neck for three full seconds. Then he stood up, turned, and held out his arms with the specific expectation of being picked up immediately.

Ezra picked him up. Declan patted his cheek twice — the universal Declan signal for *you're okay, I'm checking.*

"Back," Declan said.

"Yeah. We're back."

Ezra reached into his pocket and put the carved Ollie figure back into Declan's hands. Declan looked at it, then at the real Ollie standing right there.

"Both Ollies," he announced. The happiest sentence Ezra had heard in eleven days.

Sable stood at the base of the ramp watching this. Rafe stood beside her, looking at the colony for the first time — the wood-and-metal buildings, the spire, the pale morning light on the alien pines.

"Is it always like this?" he said.

"Like what?"

"Loud. A lot of people. The dog everywhere."

Sable looked at Ollie, who was simultaneously greeting Declan, wagging at Marcus, and investigating a nearby cart.

"Yes," she said. "It's always like this."

Rafe looked at the spire, the colony, the light.

"Good," he said.
