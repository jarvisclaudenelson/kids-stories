# Chapter 8: The Captain

Captain Okonkwo arrived at the Nelson cabin just after the second sun cleared the ridge.

She was shorter than Ezra expected. All the adults had been talking about her in the tone people use for someone imposing—"Captain Okonkwo is on her way," "Captain Okonkwo wants statements," said the way you'd say "the tide is coming"—so he'd built up a picture of someone tall with a commanding voice and a lot of hardware on her belt.

Instead she was compact and square-shouldered, with close-cropped silver-gray locs and a face that had spent decades deciding things without announcing them first. No uniform. Just the colony's dark green jacket, a small notebook in one hand, and the particular stillness of someone who had learned that stillness was louder than noise.

She shook Dad's hand at the door. Then she looked at all three boys with the specific, considered attention of someone who was actually seeing children and not just noting their presence.

"Julian," she said. "Ezra. And this must be Declan."

Declan, who was sitting on the floor eating crackers with Ollie beside him, looked up and said, "Cap-chin."

Captain Okonkwo looked at Marcus, who was standing by the wall. Something passed between them—not quite a smile, but something near it.

"He's not wrong," she said.

---

She talked to the adults first, out on the porch. Julian stood near the window for about four minutes before Mom gently redirected him to the kitchen table, which was where Ezra was sitting with Declan's crackers and Sable's star chart.

Sable had left the chart with them before Okonkwo arrived. "Look it over again," she'd said. "You know more than you think."

So Ezra looked.

Seven marks on the projected star field. Monscollis glowed gold—his. Vela Station glowed silver-blue—Sable's. Two marks in the outer arm of the map, deep red, still: Shadow Hand's taken fragments. And three yellow marks, scattered. Not clustered. Not near each other. Not near any known colony he recognized.

Julian sat across from him and pulled the chart closer without asking, which was Julian, and traced one of the yellow marks with one finger.

"These aren't random," Julian said.

"Sable said they might be other fragment holders. Or undiscovered sources."

"They're not random *distances* either." Julian had a pencil. He made tiny numbers in his notebook. "If you draw a line from Monscollis to Vela Station and extend it—"

"You're not going to get a straight line through seven points in a star field."

"I'm not trying to. I'm seeing if the spacing means anything." He erased something. "It might not. But if the Covenant fragments were *placed* somewhere, there might be a pattern to where."

Ezra looked at the yellow marks again. One of them pulsed, faint and quick, in his vision—or maybe he imagined it. He pressed his hand to his chest. The star was quiet. Just warm.

"Do you feel anything when you look at the chart?" Julian asked.

"Not really." He paused. "It's like... sometimes the star notices things before I do. But I can't make it do that on purpose."

Julian wrote that down too.

---

Captain Okonkwo came inside forty minutes later and sat at the kitchen table across from both of them. She set her notebook open but didn't look at it.

"I'm going to tell you what I know," she said, "and then I'm going to ask what you know. That's not how investigations usually go, but you're not usual witnesses."

Julian straightened almost imperceptibly.

"The two men in the treeline this morning are named Voss and Crane. I've had eyes on them for six days—they came in on the freight rotation as cargo handlers. Their manifests are clean. Too clean." She tapped the notebook. "In three years on Monscollis, I've logged every freight arrival. These two didn't unload a single crate."

"They came for us," Julian said.

"They came for the fragment. Or for whoever had it. Before Marcus's report, I didn't know a fragment existed." She looked at Ezra. "Now I do. And I need to understand what we're dealing with before I decide how to respond."

Ezra told her everything. The Vault, the Covenant, what the fragment felt like, the involuntary healing of Fletcher's scar, the watcher in the training clearing before Voss and Crane, Sable's star chart. He talked for a long time, and she let him. She asked three questions: *Where did Fletcher go after the Vault incident?* (He'd been reassigned to the south camp infirmary, Marcus said—and he'd stopped showing up two days ago.) *Did the fragment react to anyone else the way it reacted to Sable's?* (No, Ezra said, only to hers.) *What does it feel like when it senses danger?*

He thought about that one. "It's not like an alarm. It's more like—being in a room and realizing the temperature changed. You don't know when it changed. You just know it's different than it was."

Captain Okonkwo wrote that down.

---

Julian slid his notebook across the table and showed her his work on the star chart.

She looked at it carefully—longer than Ezra expected, which meant Julian had either done something smart or something complicated enough to deserve patience.

"The spacing," she said.

"If there's a deliberate pattern—"

"There is." She said it simply, no drama, the way you'd confirm the time. She pulled the star chart toward her and pointed to one of the yellow marks. "This one is in the Bryn Dural system. Six light-years out. No established colony. There's a survey team there—fifteen people, six months in. We received their last transmission twenty-two days ago."

The room got quiet.

"And since then?" Sable said from the doorway. She'd come back inside at some point without any of them noticing.

"Nothing." Captain Okonkwo looked at her evenly. "I've been treating it as a communications failure. The equipment in the outer systems is unreliable."

"And now?"

"Now I have a second hypothesis." She closed the notebook. "If the Shadow Hand is moving on undiscovered fragment sites—if they've been tracking this map longer than we have—"

"Fifteen people," Julian said.

"Yes."

Declan had wandered over from his crackers and was standing at Ezra's elbow. He didn't say anything. He just leaned into Ezra's arm with his whole toddler weight, the way he did when a room felt too big. Ezra put his hand on Declan's back.

"What do we do?" Ezra said.

"Right now? You stay safe, you keep training, and you don't go anywhere without Marcus or another adult." She stood, tucking the notebook away. "I file reports. I try to reach the Bryn Dural team through secondary relays. I find out where Fletcher went." She looked at each of them in turn. "What I need from you is time."

"The Shadow Hand doesn't give time," Sable said.

"No," Captain Okonkwo said. "That's why I'm not asking them."

---

After she left, the cabin felt different. Not smaller—more like everything that had been fuzzy had come into focus, and focus made things feel real in a way that fuzziness doesn't.

Mom set sandwiches on the table and nobody talked for a while.

Ezra ate half of his. He was thinking about the survey team on Bryn Dural. Fifteen people who'd gone quiet for twenty-two days. Not soldiers, not trained colonists—a survey team. Scientists, probably. Equipment techs. People who'd signed up to map an empty star system, not to get caught between the Shadow Hand and a seven-part ancient power source.

He wondered if the fragment was there, if it had already been taken, if someone had touched it the way he'd touched his and had it change them from the inside.

The star in his chest pulsed once. Not warm this time. Searching.

"I need to ask you something," he said to Sable.

She looked at him.

"The fragment—when you found yours. You said you went alone. Far underground, alone." He tried to say it without making it sound like an accusation. "Were you scared?"

She was quiet for a second. Then: "Yes."

"Of what?"

"That I'd pick it up and nothing would happen." She said it like she'd been waiting to say it to someone. "Like I'd made the whole thing up. Made three months of travel up. Gone all that way and it would just be a rock in the ground and I'd have to go home." She looked at the pendant, just visible inside her collar. "It wasn't nothing. But I've thought about that a lot since. What if it had been nothing. What I would have done."

"What would you have done?"

She shrugged. One small, honest motion. "Come here anyway, probably. Because the pull was real even if I couldn't explain it. The direction was real." She looked at him. "Why?"

"I'm trying to figure out if the people on Bryn Dural felt it," Ezra said. "The pull. If there's someone on that survey team who went somewhere they couldn't explain."

Julian had his notebook out. "You think the fragment found someone."

"Maybe." He pressed his hand to his chest. The star was still doing its searching-pulse, slow and patient. "Mine found me in a crash. Sable's pulled her across fourteen light-years. They don't just sit and wait."

---

The afternoon wore on. Declan napped. Julian filled three notebook pages with fragment-spacing calculations that may or may not have proved anything, and which he refused to throw out on the grounds that you didn't know what would matter until later.

Marcus checked in by radio: Fletcher had not reported to the south infirmary. Fletcher had not checked in at any colony checkpoint since two mornings ago. The investigation was now being treated as a missing person case, with two additional possibilities that Marcus didn't name but that everyone understood.

Sable sat on the floor with her back to the wall and her pendant in her hand and didn't talk much, which turned out to be her way of worrying. Ezra recognized it because he did the same thing.

He practiced the center-extension three times in the main room—small, careful, not more than five feet—and it felt different than it had in the clearing. Not worse. Just indoors. Like the power knew it was inside a cabin with low ceilings and was adjusting accordingly.

"Does it know where it is?" Julian asked, watching.

"I don't know. Maybe." He let it go. "It feels aware. Not like a tool. More like—" He thought about how to explain it. "Like when Ollie knows something is wrong before any of us do. It's not a decision. It's just what it is."

As if to confirm this, Ollie lifted his head from his spot by the door and looked at Ezra. His tail moved once.

"Thanks, bud," Ezra said.

Ollie put his head back down.

The fragment pulsed again—still that searching note, still patient. And then, so faint Ezra wasn't sure he'd felt it at all, something else answered. Not from inside him. From somewhere in the direction of the deep colony, toward the crystal spire.

He turned toward it.

"What?" Julian said.

"I'm not sure." He kept his hand on his chest. "But something just—heard it."

---

He told Sable.

She didn't dismiss it and she didn't immediately run to the crystal spire, which was the right response—just listened with her pendant out and her eyes slightly unfocused, the way she looked when she was trying to hear something subtle.

"Mine didn't catch it," she said. "But that doesn't mean you're wrong. Our fragments are tuned to different registers."

"What's near the crystal spire?"

"The spire itself. The Deep Dig entrance, two hundred meters east. The colony's equipment storage." She paused. "And the communication relay tower."

Julian's head came up fast. "The communication relay."

They looked at each other.

"Secondary relays," Ezra said.

"If someone wanted to piggyback a signal on the colony relay—if someone was trying to reach *out*—" Julian stopped himself. He looked at Sable. "Is that possible? Someone at Bryn Dural trying to use a Covenant channel instead of standard comms?"

Sable turned the pendant over in her fingers. "I don't know. I've never tried anything like that. But the fragment amplifies—some things. I've had it pick up signals in space that I couldn't explain. Electromagnetic isn't the right word. But—adjacent."

"I'm not allowed to go to the spire alone," Ezra said.

"No," Sable said. She stood. "But I'm not six years old." She looked at Marcus's radio on the table. Then at Dad, who had been sitting quietly in the far chair reading and had clearly been listening to all of this for the last five minutes.

Dad closed his book. He looked at Ezra, then at Sable, then at the radio.

"I'll call Marcus," he said.

Sable picked up her pack from beside the wall. "Tell him we need fifteen minutes."

---

They went at dusk—Sable, Dad, Marcus, Ezra, and Ollie. Julian stayed with Mom and Declan, which he accepted with more grace than Ezra expected, and which was almost certainly because someone needed to keep working on the fragment-spacing calculations and he knew it.

The crystal spire rose above the colony's east edge, pale and quiet in the last light. It had always pulsed—Ezra had been told that, a background heartbeat that the original colonists had learned to sleep through. But standing this close, he felt it differently than he heard it. The pulse matched his own heartbeat. Maybe it always had and he'd just noticed.

He stood near the base while Marcus and Dad did a perimeter walk. Sable stood beside him with her pendant out and her eyes on the spire.

He reached into his chest. Pulled the star up gently. Let it feel the air.

And there it was again—faint, directional, not quite a sound. More like a memory of one. Something very far away that had been trying to speak for a long time and had just now found a surface to speak against.

"Hear that?" he said.

"No," Sable said. "But I feel you feeling it." She closed her eyes. "Keep going."

He didn't try to reach back. He just stayed open. Stayed warm. Let the star pulse.

For thirty seconds nothing happened.

Then Ollie sat down and leaned hard against Ezra's leg—not alert-still, not warning-still, but the specific lean he did when he was keeping Ezra company. Like he knew this was hard work and he was just there.

And the signal came in clear. One beat. Somewhere in the dark between stars, a fragment was awake.

It wasn't afraid.

But it was alone.
