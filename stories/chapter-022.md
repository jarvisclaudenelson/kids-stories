# Chapter 22: Ten Days

Julian had a whiteboard.

He always had a whiteboard. He had been the kind of kid who asked for a whiteboard for his birthday when he was four, and he had never once stopped being that kind of kid. The current whiteboard was propped against the wall of the colony research room with *TEN DAYS — A PLAN* written at the top in green marker and underlined twice.

Below that was a table. The table had columns.

"I've organized it by urgency," Julian explained to the group, who had been eating breakfast and were now sitting in front of a whiteboard instead.

Rafe looked at the table. "You have a column called 'Things We Already Know.'"

"Yes."

"It's empty."

"That's how I know we need to fill it."

---

The first three days were mostly arguments.

Not bad ones. The kind where everyone was trying to think out loud at the same time, which was what happened when six smart people and one toddler all needed to process the same enormous piece of news and had no manual to guide them.

Sable spent every morning in the Cradle. She went alone at first, then Rafe started coming down with her after breakfast — he couldn't do much, but he could move boulders and hold lights and not talk unless he had something to say, which made him excellent company. Sable worked through the spiral carvings from one side of the chamber to the other, reading whatever her pattern-sense could catch.

"It's like reading something in a language where you know every third word," she said on Day 3, sitting back on her heels. "I can feel the structure. I can tell it's a language. But the specific meaning..." She shook her head.

"That one section," Rafe said, pointing. "You've been staring at it for an hour."

"It's the section Declan touched." Sable studied it. The glyphs there glowed slightly warmer than the rest — not because of the fragments, just... on their own. "I think it says something about the Guardians specifically. Not the Covenant. Not the fragments. *Us.*"

Rafe looked at it for a moment.

"That's either very good," he said, "or the other kind of good that's actually bad."

"Thank you, Rafe."

"Any time."

---

Noa decoded two more lines of the coordinate string on Day 4.

She announced this at lunch, which was becoming the default time for announcements, because everyone was present and had food, and food made hard news easier.

"The address the Covenant is broadcasting," she said, "points outward from known colony space. Not toward any mapped planet. Not toward anything in the Outer Ring charts." She paused. "I've triangulated it four different ways because I wanted to make sure I wasn't wrong."

"Were you wrong?" Petra asked.

"No." Noa looked mildly annoyed about this. "The coordinates point to a region we have no data on. Zero records, zero surveys. Colony expansion hasn't gone there. No one has gone there." She turned her headphone cord in her hands — the fidget she did when something was bothering her. "Which either means the Builders live somewhere completely undiscovered."

"Or?" Julian prompted.

"Or the broadcast is pointing to where they *were* when they built the Covenant." She looked at the table. "Which might be a very long time ago."

Nobody rushed to fill that silence.

"So they might have moved," Sable said finally.

"They might have a lot of things," Noa said.

---

Ezra noticed that the gold fragment felt different.

Not wrong. Just different. For two years — since the day it had first settled warm and certain against his ribs in the rock above Monscollis — it had hummed with something like urgency. Low, patient urgency, but always there. The feeling of an unfinished thing that wanted finishing.

That urgency was gone now.

In its place was something quieter. Something that felt, honestly, a little like the way the colony felt on Sunday afternoons when the generators ran slower and the dust settled and everything just *rested.*

Patience, he decided. The fragment felt patient.

He pressed his hand to his chest during Sable's next Cradle shift and tried to think about what that meant. The fragment had always known things before he did. If it wasn't worried, maybe that was useful information.

He was still working on whether he believed that.

---

Mom found Declan in the Cradle on Day 5.

She had been looking for him for twenty minutes. He had slipped out after his afternoon nap — the sneaky quiet exit that meant he knew exactly where he was going and did not want company. She followed the trail of his small footprints in the red dust out past the western plain and down the rocky slope to the door in the rock.

He was sitting cross-legged on the stone floor in front of the dais, hands in his lap, looking at the seven fragments in their hovering ring.

He was perfectly still, which was alarming, because Declan was never perfectly still.

Mom stood at the bottom of the stairs for a long moment. Then she walked over and sat down beside him on the floor, which took a moment because the floor was cold stone and she was an adult.

Declan glanced at her, then back at the fragments.

"Waiting," he said, answering a question she hadn't asked.

"For what, buddy?"

"Friends." He considered. "Big friends."

"The people who are coming?"

He nodded. He reached over and took her hand, the way he did when he wanted her to stay but didn't want to say so. She stayed.

They sat together in the warm seven-color light for a while.

Later, she told the group what had happened. She said it in her quiet voice — the one she used when she was being careful. "He wasn't scared," she said. "Not even a little."

Sable nodded. "He's never been scared of the fragments."

"Or of what's coming," Ezra added.

Julian wrote *Declan: not afraid* in his notebook. He had stopped pretending the notebook was a tactical document and started treating it like what it was, which was a record of things he didn't want to forget.

---

On Day 6, Julian admitted that the whiteboard plan was mostly theater.

He said it to Ezra, at night, which was the time he said things he wouldn't say in front of the group.

"I don't actually know what we're preparing for," he said. "I put things in columns because it makes me feel like I'm doing something. But I don't have a real plan."

"Nobody has a real plan," Ezra said.

"I'm supposed to."

Ezra looked at him from the lower bunk. "You're seven."

"I'm a strategist."

"Julian. You're seven and you're waiting for alien people to show up in four days. There is no plan for that."

Julian was quiet.

"Petra had a good point today," Ezra said. "About the Covenant Purge."

"I know." Julian stared at the ceiling. "If the Builders made the fragments, they had to know someone might try to destroy them. They built the Cradle anyway. They sent the signal anyway."

"So they're not surprised by any of this."

"Probably not."

"That makes me feel a little better," Ezra said.

Julian was quiet for a moment. "Me too," he said. "A little."

The colony generator hummed outside. Two of the smaller stars were visible through the window — the ones that were always grouped together, that Declan had started calling "the eyes."

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest.

Patient. Still patient.

He closed his eyes.

---

Sable was on watch on Day 7 when she felt it shift.

It was past midnight. Everyone else was asleep. She was in the research room with her fragment in her hand and her notebook open, doing what she did on night watch, which was read patterns the way other people listened to music — just to have something moving through her, just to stay present.

The Covenant chord had been steady for seven days. Constant. Like breathing.

At 2:14 AM, it got louder.

Not dramatically. Not in a way she could have explained to someone who couldn't feel patterns. Just — more present. More *close.* The way a sound gets when the source is moving toward you.

She was down the stairs and into the Cradle in under three minutes.

The fragments were brighter.

Definitely brighter. Warm and blazing instead of warm and steady. All seven of them in their ring, spinning a little faster, the combined light casting the spirals on the walls in sharp relief.

And Declan was sitting on the floor in front of the dais.

He was in his pajamas. He was wide awake.

He had been here for a while — Sable could tell by the settled look of him. She had woken the others before she came down, message through the wall panel. She could hear them on the stairs now, feet and questions.

"Decker," she said. "How long have you been down here?"

"Long time," he said simply.

The rest of them arrived — Ezra first, then Julian, then Rafe still pulling on a jacket, Petra with her hair sideways from sleep, Noa with her headphones already on and her eyes wide.

"The signal changed," Noa said immediately. She was reading something through her headphones, turning slowly. "It's still broadcasting — but there's an echo. Something coming back."

"Coming back?" Rafe said.

"Like a reply." She stopped turning. She faced the northwest arc of the chamber wall. "From that direction."

The fragments blazed.

Sable looked at Declan.

Declan had turned around to face all of them. He had the expression — the one that was part frustration, part patience, part something older than two.

He held up three fingers.

"Three days?" Julian said.

Declan nodded.

"But it was supposed to be ten," Rafe said.

Declan looked at Rafe. He lowered his three fingers. He thought about it.

"Hurrying," he said.

The chamber filled with the Covenant chord — all seven fragments, all at once, warm and bright and certain as anything Ezra had ever felt pressed against his ribs.

Three days.

The Builders were hurrying.
