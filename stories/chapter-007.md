# Chapter 7: Drills and DarkThreads

The northern clearing smelled like pine needles and morning dew.

Ezra arrived first, which was unusual. Julian was usually the one who got to places first, powered by whatever internal engine made him need to win at everything. But today Julian had been caught up talking to Maya about something—school stuff, probably, which Ezra had immediately tuned out of.

Sable arrived second, which was also unusual, because she seemed like someone who was never late to anything. She was wearing the same dark clothes as always, her silver-blue pendant hidden beneath her collar. She carried a small satchel that clinked when she set it down.

Third was Marcus, because Dad had asked him to come as backup, and Marcus was the kind of person who took backup seriously. He had his emergency radio and a look on his face that said he was already cataloging escape routes.

Julian showed up running, which meant he'd been sprinting the whole way.

"Sorry," he gasped. "Maya was explaining fractions."

"Terrible thing, fractions," Marcus said mildly.

Ezra had set up the clearing the way Sable had shown him last time—three moss-covered logs arranged in a triangle, giving him room to stretch in any direction without hitting trees. He'd practiced twice this week on his own, reaching farther each time, and he was quietly proud of the progress.

But when Sable looked at his setup, she shook her head.

"You're thinking about it wrong," she said.

"Thinking about what?"

"Distance." She stepped into the triangle and crouched, touching the ground with her fingertips. "The stretch isn't the point. The point is control. If you can reach ten feet but can't come back, you're just a kid with a problem."

Ezra thought about this. "I can come back. I just have to think about it."

"Then you're using the wrong muscle."

"The wrong... what?"

Sable stood. "Show me your worst stretch."

Ezra hesitated. Then he thought about Julian running, about being left behind, about all the times his legs weren't quite fast enough—and his right arm shot out like a rubber band, extending nine feet, his hand gripping empty air where a tree trunk would be if the clearing were smaller.

Sable watched. "Now bring it back."

He did. It took longer than it should have. His arm trembled on the return, and when it snapped back into its normal socket, his shoulder ached.

"That," Sable said. "That's the problem. You're using your arm. You're thinking of it like a bungee cord—pull, snap back. But that's not how it works." She paused. "The fragment is in your chest, right? Near your heart?"

Ezra nodded.

"Then start there. Feel where the stretch comes from. Not your arm—your chest. Your center. The power doesn't push through you. It *is* you."

Julian, who had been about to make a comment, stopped. He'd never heard Sable talk this much.

Ezra closed his eyes. He thought about his star—the warm golden point that lived inside him. He'd felt it pulse when the Shadow Hand was near. He'd felt it react to danger. But he'd never really *talked* to it.

He thought: *Okay. Show me.*

Nothing happened.

He thought harder: *I'm trying. I want to do this right.*

The star flickered. Like a candle in a breeze. And then—something. A small warmth that started in his chest and flowed down his arm, not like electricity but like water. Gentle. Patient.

He opened his eyes and extended his arm again.

This time, it was different. Slower. His arm stretched out smoothly, reaching eight feet, then nine, then ten—and when he stopped, it *stayed*. No trembling. No snap-back pressure. Just... there. An extension of himself.

"Now come back," Sable said.

He thought about the warmth in his chest, and his arm returned at the same slow, smooth pace. No ache. No jolt. Just ease.

Julian made a sound that was half impressed and half annoyed, which was his default sound for watching Ezra do something well.

"That's better," Sable said. "That's the real drill. Not reach far. Reach *controlled*."

---

They drilled for an hour. Ezra stretched different parts—his legs, his torso, eventually his whole body, which looked very strange but worked. Sable corrected his posture, his breathing, his mental focus. She had him think about the people he loved while he stretched, which was embarrassing in front of Marcus but worked better than thinking about winning.

Julian, meanwhile, had gotten bored of watching and started running laps around the clearing. Marcus was keeping an eye on him and the tree line simultaneously, which was a skill.

By the second hour, Ezra could hold a five-foot arm extension for almost a full minute without strain. He was starting to understand what Sable meant—the power wasn't something he used. It was something he *was*.

Then his star pulsed.

Not the sharp warning pulse it gave for Shadow Hand. Something different. Slower. Like a heartbeat that had found a rhythm.

Sable's hand went to her pendant. The silver-blue stone was glowing.

"You feel that?" she said.

Ezra nodded. The two stars were pulsing in sync now, two different colors beating as one.

"It's not just the fragments," Sable said quietly. "It's the connection between us. The Covenant recognizes its own." She looked at him. "This is what they want to break. The connection. That's why they need seven fragments—not just to have the power, but to *reshape* it. Turn it into something that serves them instead of..." She gestured at the clearing, the trees, the sky. "This."

"What happens if they do?" Julian had stopped running. "If they reshape it?"

Sable was quiet. "I don't know. But the Covenant has been here since before humans. Before the stars we're living on. If they break it—" She shook her head. "That's why we can't let them."

Ollie, who had been lying by a log, suddenly stood up.

His ears went forward. His nose wrinkled. And then, very quietly, he growled.

Marcus moved fast. He was between the kids and the tree line in half a second, his radio in his hand. "How many?" he murmured.

Ollie didn't answer, because he was a dog, but his body answered for him. He was looking left, toward the dense pines. Then right. Then left again.

"More than one," Marcus said. "Spread out."

Sable's hand was still on her pendant. The silver-blue light was bright now, almost blazing. "They're not close," she said. "But they're watching. I can feel them—a pattern. Like a web."

"Can you see what they are?" Julian asked.

She closed her eyes. The light under her fingers pulsed, and Ezra felt his star answer, a dual beacon.

"Two of them," Sable said. "At the edge of the treeline. Gray uniforms. Shadow Hand." She opened her eyes. "They're not attacking. They're just... watching. Taking notes."

"Marcus radioed Captain Okonkwo." Marcus had his radio up. "Report in, get backup here."

The radio crackled. A voice came through—Captain Okonkwo herself, crisp and calm.

"Already on my way. Two minutes. Hold position."

Marcus lowered the radio. "We hold. We don't engage."

Julian looked like he wanted to engage. Ezra looked at him and shook his head.

"Not yet," Ezra said. "Not until we know more."

Julian clenched his fists. Then he unclenched them. "Fine. Not yet."

They waited. The clearing felt smaller. The shadows between the trees felt deeper. And the two fragments, pulsing in sync, kept watch over everything.
