# Chapter 7: The Center

The morning started with fog.

Not thick fog—Monscollis fog was never quite thick—but the soft, milk-colored kind that settled between the trees in the early hours before the twin suns burned it off. Ezra had noticed it every morning since they'd crash-landed. He liked it. It made the planet feel quieter than it was.

He was already awake when Sable knocked on the cabin door at first light.

She had a pack over one shoulder and a tin cup of something hot in her hand, and she looked at him the way someone looks when they're not used to people being ready before them. Not quite impressed. Just noting it.

"You're up," she said.

"Couldn't sleep." He stepped outside. "Too much to think about."

"Good," Sable said, which was not the response most people would give to *I couldn't sleep*, but Ezra was starting to understand that Sable's idea of comfort ran straight through honesty without making many stops.

Julian came out a minute later, still pulling on his jacket. Behind him, Declan appeared at the door in his pajamas with Ollie pressed against his knees, looking outraged at the hour.

"Coldie," Declan announced, pointing at the fog.

"Stay inside, bud," Julian said.

"No."

"Deck."

"I stay inside," Declan said, which meant he was going to stay inside, just in a way that made it his idea. He turned around and padded back to Mom.

Ollie stayed at the door. His nose lifted. Then he slipped out and fell into step beside Ezra.

"He comes," Sable said.

Ezra scratched behind Ollie's ear. "He always comes."

The northern clearing was thirty minutes through the pine forest by the route Marcus had found—quiet, no obvious paths, hard to follow if you didn't know the turns. Ezra had memorized it after two days. Julian had memorized it after one day, which he had mentioned exactly once and which Sable had responded to by immediately moving on to the next topic.

The clearing itself was narrow but long. Three fallen trees lay across it end to end, their bark half-consumed by pale blue-green moss that glowed faintly in low light—not bright enough to be eerie, just enough to make them look like they'd been placed there by someone who cared about the arrangement. Between them, the ground was soft with low silver-gray grass that Ezra had decided was his favorite thing on Monscollis.

Sable set down her pack and turned to face him.

"From the beginning," she said. "Show me what you've got."

Ezra took a breath. He'd been practicing every day since Chapter 5—small things, careful things, trying to hold what he'd learned without overdoing it. He reached down into the feeling in his chest, the warm pulse of the star, and pulled it up through his arm the way he'd figured out: not pushing the stretch out from his hand, but letting it flow from the center outward, like water finding its level.

His right arm extended. Five feet. Six. He held it.

The stretch felt steadier than it had three days ago. Smoother. Like he'd found a groove in it.

"Keep going," Sable said.

Eight feet. The familiar tingling started at his elbow—pins and needles, like a limb waking up. He breathed through it.

"What's the sensation?" Sable asked.

"Tingling. Starts at the elbow when I go past six feet."

"What does the tingling mean?"

Ezra thought about it. "That I'm working hard?"

"It means you're pulling from the wrong place," Sable said. She walked around him, studying his arm the way someone studies a bent piece of metal. "You're reaching from your shoulder. Your shoulder is fighting the stretch. Don't reach—*extend*."

"What's the difference?"

She stopped in front of him. "Reaching means you want something that's far away. Extending means you're taking up space that's already yours." She tapped her own sternum. "Start from here."

--- PAGE BREAK ---

Ezra let the stretch snap back—carefully, not the sharp-rubber-band snap that had happened three times in week one and hurt like a sudden cramp. He rolled his shoulder. Then he tried again.

Chest first. Not his arm reaching for distance. More like... breathing out. Like the stretch wasn't happening *to* his arm but *through* his whole body, starting from the warm place between his ribs.

The tingling stayed quiet until nine feet.

"There," Sable said.

He held it. Nine feet. Not trembling. Not fighting.

"Now think of someone," Sable said.

He thought of Declan, still standing in the cabin doorway in his pajamas, pointing at the fog and announcing "Coldie" with total confidence.

The stretch reached ten feet without him deciding to move.

"Oh," Ezra said.

Julian, who had been keeping very quiet with his arms crossed against the cold, uncrossed his arms. "Ten feet?"

"Ten feet," Sable confirmed. She looked at Ezra. "You knew the emotional connection made it stronger. But you were thinking of big things—scary things, important things. Scary makes you tense. The power doesn't work well in tense." She paused. "Try something small. Something true."

Declan in pajamas. Declan calling everyone "coldie." Declan's stubborn two-year-old face.

The arm stayed steady at ten feet for forty-five seconds before Ezra let it go.

Julian made a sound that was not quite a word but was somewhere between *okay* and *fine*.

"Your turn," Sable said.

Julian looked at her. "I don't have a fragment."

"No. But you see things. Run the drill." She nodded at the clearing.

"You want me to run my own drills?"

"I want to see what you build." She sat on one of the moss logs with her tin cup. "Go."

Ezra, sitting in the silver grass to rest his arm, watched Julian process the fact that someone was actually listening to his training plan. Julian's face did something complicated—surprised, then trying not to look surprised—and then he squared his shoulders and started running the drill like he'd been waiting for someone to hand him the clipboard.

--- PAGE BREAK ---

They worked for an hour. Ezra practiced the center-extension nine more times. Sable stopped him when he was doing it wrong, let him go when he was doing it right. She didn't compliment in the way that made you feel seen and stopped working; she just moved on to the next thing, which meant you knew you'd done something right because she wasn't correcting you anymore.

Julian ran his drill—a pattern of movements through the clearing that, Ezra realized, was actually a smart way to practice covering ground fast. When he was done, Sable said two things: "Left turn, too wide" and "The pace changes work." Julian wrote both in the small notebook he'd started carrying.

At the end of the hour, Sable pulled the pendant from inside her collar and held it in her palm.

The silver-blue glow was steady in the morning light—not bright, but visible. Clear. Still.

Ezra's star pulsed.

He hadn't noticed it at first, but now he did—the two glows had fallen into a rhythm together. Not the same rhythm. More like two instruments playing different notes that somehow fit. The Covenant, speaking to itself.

"Does yours do that often?" he asked.

"When it's near another fragment, yes." She looked at the stone. "It doesn't always mean something. Sometimes it's just—recognition. Like a handshake." She looked at him. "But sometimes it's a warning."

"How do you know which?"

"I don't always." She closed her fingers around the stone. "That's the honest answer."

Ollie, who had been lying at Ezra's feet in the silver grass, lifted his head.

Then he went rigid.

--- PAGE BREAK ---

Ezra knew that posture now. He'd seen it in the forest with the first unknown watcher, and twice more since. Ollie's whole body went still in a particular way—not afraid-still, not alert-still, but *listening*-still, like something in him was receiving a signal that hadn't reached anyone else yet.

"There," Julian said quietly.

He'd seen it too.

Sable was already turning. Slowly. Not reaching for anything, not tensing up—just turning the way someone turns when they've been in this situation before and know the first rule is don't spook whatever's watching you.

At the treeline, seventy feet away and mostly in shadow, were two figures in gray uniforms.

Not the unknown watcher from before. These were different.

Shadow Hand.

They stood still, not advancing, not retreating. One of them was writing in something—a small pad or a device. The other was just watching.

"They're logging it," Sable said. Quiet. Even. "They've been doing this. Not here to take anything—yet. Just watching what you can do and taking notes."

"How long?" Ezra said.

"Long enough." She kept her back straight. "Don't stop. Don't look scared. Keep doing exactly what you were doing."

Julian looked at her. "You're serious."

"If we run, they learn we run. If we stop, they learn what stops us." She met his eyes. "We're not hiding anything from them anymore. So we show them something they can't use."

--- PAGE BREAK ---

Ezra pulled his arm through the center-extension again. Steady. Ten feet.

It was harder to stay in his chest with two strangers staring at him from the treeline. He kept wanting to pull back, curl in, protect the star. But he thought about Declan again—this time, the image of Declan putting his tiny hand in Ezra's and pulling him toward whatever Declan currently found interesting, totally confident that his big brother would come.

The stretch held.

At the edge of his vision, he saw one of the gray-uniformed figures write something down.

"Marcus is two minutes out," Julian said. He'd had his hand in his pocket on the colony radio for the last ninety seconds.

"Good," Sable said.

"You knew he was coming?"

"I told him to patrol this direction this morning." She didn't sound apologetic about not mentioning it earlier. "Contingency."

Julian looked at her for a second. Then he nodded—one short nod—which was Julian's version of a peace offering. *You know more than me about this part. Fine.*

Ollie stayed rigid until the snap of footsteps came from the eastern path. Then his tail moved, once, and he sat down. Marcus stepped into the clearing with the easy quiet of someone who'd spent years making himself not-alarming, which somehow made him more alarming, and looked at the treeline.

The two gray figures looked back.

Then they turned and walked away through the trees.

They didn't run. They didn't hurry. They just left, which was almost worse than if they'd done something dramatic. It was the leaving of people who had already gotten what they came for.

--- PAGE BREAK ---

Marcus stood with his hands at his sides and watched the treeline for a minute after they were gone. Then he turned to the group.

"Same two as yesterday," he said.

"You've seen them before?" Julian said.

"Captain Okonkwo and I have been tracking their positions since the incident at the Deep Dig. Two on the treeline. Two on the eastern ridge. Rotating." He looked at Sable. "Your report was accurate."

"I know," Sable said.

Marcus studied her for a moment. Then, faintly, the corner of his mouth moved—not quite a smile, but the older-soldier equivalent of Julian's one-nod. "Captain Okonkwo is en route to formalize the investigation. She wants a statement from everyone in this clearing."

"I can do that," Sable said.

Ezra let his arm come back—all the way back, smoothly, with the tingling barely registering. He sat down on the moss log where Sable had been sitting. He was tired, but a different kind of tired than in the first days. Not the dragged-through-mud exhaustion of overextending. More like the feeling after running hard on purpose.

Julian sat next to him. They didn't say anything for a moment.

"They're going to keep coming back," Julian said.

"Yeah."

"They're not scared of us."

Ezra thought about the watcher writing in his little pad. Logging a five-year-old's stretch training while standing forty feet inside the colony's tree line. "No. They're not scared of us yet."

Julian looked at him. "Yet?"

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest. The star pulsed—steady, warm. It didn't feel scared either.

"We've had the fragment for two weeks," he said. "They've been at this for years. We're behind." He paused. "But they're still coming to watch us. Which means we have something they don't."

"What?"

Ezra looked at Sable, who was talking to Marcus with her arms crossed, precise and clear, already building the next contingency. He looked at Julian, who had a notebook and a clipboard-shaped hole in his soul that had found something useful to do. He looked at Ollie, who had moved to guard the east path like someone had given him a shift.

"I don't know exactly," he said honestly. "But I think it's us."

--- PAGE BREAK ---

They walked back to the cabin through the thinning fog, single file, Ollie leading.

The pine trees were starting to show the gold-and-green shift that meant the twin suns were up and working. Somewhere in the colony, a bell rang—the morning ration call—and Ezra could smell something cooking from two hundred yards away.

He thought about what Sable had said. *Start from your center.* Not from your arm. Not from what's far away. From the place that's already warm.

He'd been treating the power like a tool he picked up and put down. Something that lived in his hands. But it didn't live in his hands. It lived in the same place the love did—the specific, ordinary, embarrassing kind of love that made you stay in the cabin doorway in pajamas and point at fog and call it "coldie" because your big brother was out there and you wanted to be near him.

That wasn't something they could log in a notebook.

That wasn't something they could collect.

At the cabin door, Declan was waiting—still in pajamas, now with a piece of toast, watching the path with total confidence that Ezra would come back. When he saw them, his face opened up like a window.

"EZ!" he announced. "You cold?"

"A little."

"I ate toast," Declan said, holding it up as evidence.

"Good job, bud."

Declan considered this, decided it was sufficient, and turned back inside. Ezra followed him through the door, out of the fog, into the warm noise of the cabin.

The star in his chest pulsed once, quiet and sure.

*Three more.*

*We'll find them.*
