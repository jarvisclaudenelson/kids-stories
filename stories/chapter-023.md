# Chapter 23: What Comes First

The new whiteboard said *THREE DAYS — REAL PLAN* at the top, underlined three times.

Below the title was a single line in blue marker.

*Say hello.*

"That's not a plan," Petra said.

"It's an entry," Julian said.

"It's two words."

"Plans build from entries." Julian uncapped the green marker and added a second line below the first: *Be ready.*

Rafe read it from across the room. "Ready for what?"

Julian looked at the board. He looked at Rafe. He looked back at the board.

"That's what the rest of the plan is for," he said.

Declan walked over and picked up the orange marker and drew what appeared to be a very serious rectangle next to the two entries. He capped the marker, put it back, and walked away.

"What did he add?" Noa asked.

"A rectangle," Julian said.

"What does that mean?"

Julian looked at it. "I have no idea."

Petra offered a snack bar to no one in particular. Ezra took it. She was on her fifth in two days, by her count, and she had stopped feeling self-conscious about this.

---

Noa had the signal spread across four notepads and two overlapping diagrams by 9 AM.

The echo was cleaner. She had known it would be — it had been getting cleaner every twelve hours, like a signal source moving steadily closer. She could hear its structure through her headphones, the yellow-green glow at her fingertips following the lines she traced.

"It's not just coordinates anymore," she said, to whoever was in the kitchen.

Ezra was in the kitchen. He had been eating a bowl of something grainy and thinking about the gold fragment.

"What is it now?" he asked.

"A sequence." She frowned at the diagram. "Like steps. Things in order. I don't know what the steps are yet, but there are seven of them, and they're not random."

Ezra looked at his chest. He pressed one hand there, habit by now.

The patient feeling was gone.

He had noticed it two days ago — the shift from patience to something like alertness. Not fear. Not dread. More like the feeling right before you walk into a room where you know something important is about to happen and you want your head clear.

The fragment had been ahead of him before. He'd learned to trust it.

He pressed harder.

*Pay attention*, it said. Without words. The way it always did.

"Seven steps," he repeated. He looked at the notepads. "Like the fragments."

Noa met his eyes. "Yeah," she said. "Like the fragments."

---

On the evening of Day 8, Declan pulled Ezra outside by the sleeve.

He did this with purpose — not a wandering toddler thing, but a going-somewhere-specific thing. Out the colony door, around the research building, to the flat red dust between the western structures where the sky opened up wide.

He pointed.

Ezra looked up.

The two paired stars Declan had been calling "the eyes" were visible in the darkening sky, low on the northwest horizon. They'd always been there — small, ordinary, easily missed. Ezra had been looking at them since Declan pointed them out a week ago and they'd looked like two stars.

Tonight they were brighter.

Not blinding. Not even alarming, exactly. Just — there. More there than before.

Declan kept his arm raised, pointing.

Ezra felt the gold fragment go warm at his chest, warmer than it had been in days. Alert. Leaning forward like a dog that hears something.

"They're getting closer," Ezra said.

Declan lowered his arm. He looked at Ezra like: *yes, and?*

"Fast," Ezra said.

Declan nodded.

The two stars held steady above the rust-and-violet horizon, brighter now than anything nearby.

Ezra went back inside and told Julian.

---

Sable said nothing at dinner. She was in her reading state — pattern-sense running, fragment bright, the look she got when she was working on something and did not want to be interrupted. Rafe had learned to recognize it early and had quietly made sure her water glass stayed full. He did not ask questions.

After dinner, she spoke.

"There's something in the colony grid," she said. "A pattern that wasn't there yesterday."

The table went quiet.

"The Builders?" Julian said.

"No. Wrong direction, wrong signature." She set down her cup. "The Builders are northwest. This is coming from the colony itself." She paused. "Someone has been moving through the settlement. Staying low. Small steps, scattered, not random."

"How long?" Petra asked.

"A day, maybe more." Sable looked at the table. "They're careful. But not careful enough."

"The Covenant Purge," Rafe said.

Nobody argued.

They had not seen the Purge since Rafe pulled Fragment 7 free of the white dwarf's orbit and the Purge's ship had broken off pursuit three months ago. Sable had been watching for them since. She had told the group, quietly, that their absence was not resolution. It was patience.

"What do they want?" Noa asked.

"What they've always wanted," Sable said. "The fragments."

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest under the table. Alert. Still alert. Not running — leaning.

"The Builders are three days out," Julian said. "So the Purge has three days to destroy the Covenant before its original builders get back." He looked at the whiteboard. "That's why they're here now."

Petra stood up and put on her jacket. "We sleep at the Cradle tonight," she said.

Nobody argued with that either.

---

They found them at midnight.

Two figures, standing at the rock shelf twenty meters from the Cradle entrance. Cloaked — the kind of gear that blurred the wearer's edges, made the eye want to skip past them. They had not come down to the door. They had not tried to enter.

They were waiting.

Sable stepped forward. The rest of the group fanned behind her — wide enough that Petra had clean sight lines, Rafe had room to work, Noa had distance to listen.

Declan was on Ezra's back, arms looped around his neck, watching.

The taller figure lowered its hood.

A woman. Dark eyes, close-cropped silver hair, a jaw that had been through something difficult. She looked at the group the way people look when they expected to see fewer children.

"We're not here to fight," she said.

"So you've been hiding in our colony for a day waiting for midnight?" Petra said.

The woman did not flinch. "We needed to see who you were first."

"And now?" Sable said.

"Now I'm warning you." She stepped forward once, hands open. "What's coming — the signal you sent, the builders who built the Covenant — they are not coming to help you."

The chamber behind them hummed. Seven fragments, steady and warm.

"The Covenant was not a gift," the woman continued. "It was a test. And the people who built it have been running that test for a very long time on a lot of different worlds. Not all of those worlds are still—" She stopped. She chose the word carefully. "—intact."

Silence.

Julian's jaw was tight. Ezra could see him going through everything, the way he always did, building the map.

"You've been trying to destroy the fragments," Rafe said. "For years. You killed people."

"We've been trying to prevent this," the woman said. "Badly, sometimes. Yes." She did not look away. "We were wrong about how. We were right about what."

---

Nobody moved for a long moment.

The colony was quiet. The two stars on the northwest horizon — Declan's eyes — blazed steady in the dark above them.

Then Declan unwrapped himself from Ezra's back and walked forward.

Ezra reached for him, then stopped.

Declan walked up to the woman and stood in front of her. He looked up at her face. She looked down at his.

He shook his head.

Slowly. Clearly. The deliberate no he used when he was certain.

Then he turned around, walked back past the group, down the slope, and into the Cradle.

Ezra followed. So did the rest of them.

In the chamber, the seven fragments blazed in their ring, warm and bright. Declan walked to the dais, stood in the full light of all seven of them, and held up one finger.

"One day?" Julian said.

Declan nodded.

"Not three."

Declan looked at the northwest wall. Back at Julian. He spread his hands wide — fast, closing distance.

"They're not hurrying anymore," Noa said quietly. She had her headphones on. Her eyes were wide. "They're *here.* The signal echo—" She turned. "It's not from space. It's from low orbit. Julian, they're already—"

The fragments blazed white.

All seven, one pulse — the same as when Rafe pulled Fragment 7 from the dead star, but closer, louder, real.

Above them, through the stone ceiling and the red-rock plain and the dark Monscollis sky, something crossed the horizon.

Declan looked up.

"Tomorrow," he said. He didn't look worried.

He sat back down on the floor in front of the dais, settled his hands in his lap, and waited.
