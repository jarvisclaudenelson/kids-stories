# Chapter 12: Yellow Marks

Julian had the star chart spread on the table before anyone else was awake.

This was not surprising. What was surprising was that he hadn't woken anyone up yet. He sat with his hands flat on either side of it. Just looking. The way he looked at a hard math problem before he wrote anything down. Like the looking was part of solving it.

Ezra came out of the bedroom and found him there. Ollie followed. Ollie checked the room, checked the window, then settled near the table. Nothing to report.

"You slept," Ezra said.

"Three hours," Julian said, not looking up.

"That's not sleeping. That's a nap."

"It was enough."

Ezra looked at the chart. Sable had left it the night before, rolled up on the table. Julian had clearly unrolled it the moment the cabin was quiet.

The third yellow mark was closer than the others. About a hand-width on the chart. That didn't mean much without knowing the scale.

"Four light-years," Ezra said.

"Three point nine," Julian said. "About."

"How do you know?"

Julian pointed to the edge of the chart. Sable had penciled in a tiny scale there, almost hidden. He'd been up for hours finding it.

"What star system?"

"No name. Just a number — HD 34729. Could be anything."

"Or it could be someone."

Julian looked up. He had the face he made when he was choosing between two things he didn't want to say.

"Could be both," he said.

---

Breakfast was eggs that one of the cooks had made very good. Apparently three weeks of bad food made you really want to do something interesting with actual eggs. The hall smelled great.

Rafe came in looking more like himself than yesterday. The borrowed clothes still didn't fit, but he'd folded the cuffs differently. He sat down and got coffee — real coffee, not the colony kind — and held it like someone who had earned it.

Declan arrived at the table from a direction nobody had been watching. That was normal for Declan. He looked at the eggs. He looked at the coffee pot.

"Hot," he announced.

"Yes," Dad said.

"Decker no hot."

"Also yes."

Declan nodded, satisfied that he had explained the situation clearly, and went for the eggs.

Sable came in a few minutes later, looked at the full table, and sat beside Rafe without any fuss. She just fit herself in. It had taken about a week for her to do that, which was fast for her.

Marcus came in last. He had the posture of someone with news.

"Okonkwo's been up since four," he said. "She wants to meet this morning. Fletcher gave her something last night — something about timing. She wouldn't say more over the radio."

---

Okonkwo arrived at nine.

She sat at the table the way she always did — all the way in, nothing held back. She put her notebook in front of her. She looked at everyone.

"Fletcher says the Shadow Hand is moving up their timeline," she said. "He doesn't know the exact date. But he knows the sign: when they have five fragments, they think they can force the shattering even without the other two. They won't wait for all seven."

Nobody spoke.

"They have two," Julian said. "We have three."

"Which leaves two we don't know about," Okonkwo said. "And the Shadow Hand knows where at least one of the missing ones is. Maybe both."

Sable's hand moved to her pendant. "How?"

"Fletcher doesn't know their whole network. But he said there's someone working with them — not a full agent, more like a helper — someone who was close to the Covenant a long time ago. Someone who knows how the power scatters and where it lands." She looked at Sable. "He used the words 'pattern reader.'"

Julian sat up straight.

Ezra felt the star in his chest give one short, clear pulse. Not danger-cold. Not warm exactly. Something that meant: *pay attention to this.*

"The Shadow Hand has someone who understands the fragment pattern," Julian said. He'd opened his notebook without even noticing he'd done it. "They're not just guessing. They know where to look."

"Or they knew," Sable said. "Past tense might matter."

"Why past tense?"

"If their helper was close to the Covenant, the Covenant would have noticed. The fragments might have shifted. The pull that brought me here didn't use logic — it went through the fragment itself. If the Shadow Hand's helper had that kind of access—"

"Then their map might be wrong," Rafe said.

Sable nodded. "Maybe. Or just old."

"So we need to get to HD 34729 before they figure that out," Julian said.

He looked at Okonkwo when he said it. Not asking. Just showing his math.

---

Okonkwo let the room sit quiet for a moment.

She did that — let hard things land before she touched them. Ezra had noticed. She never said something was wrong without giving people time to hear it first.

"The Ariadne can make three point nine light-years," she said. "It's done more. Wren's free until next week." She looked at Marcus.

Marcus made a sound that wasn't quite a word but meant *I already checked.*

"You'd have three hours at the location before the jump window closes," Okonkwo said. "That's not much time."

"It's enough," Sable said, "if the holder is there."

"And if they're not?"

"Then we come back and figure out something else."

Okonkwo looked at Ezra. Not asking him. More like checking something.

"Can you reach them?" she said. "The way you reached Rafe."

Ezra thought about the relay station. About how Bryn Dural had felt — ragged and cold and tired. Whatever was at HD 34729 would be different. He didn't know what shape it would take.

"I can try from the spire," he said. "If the fragment there is awake."

"Then try first. We plan based on what you find."

---

They went to the crystal spire at noon.

It stood at the north end of the colony, taller than any building. Pale and see-through in the midday light. It was always doing something at the edge of your hearing — a faint beat, a warmth. Most people had stopped noticing it. Ezra never had.

He stood close. The spire's pulse changed, the way it always did when he was near — matching his heartbeat, the way it had done since Chapter 8.

"Same as before," Sable said. She stayed back one step. Rafe stood beside her, watching.

Ezra closed his eyes. He thought about Declan eating eggs that morning with extreme personal focus. He thought about Rafe folding his cuffs. He let the star wake up gently.

Then he pushed it outward — slow and patient — in the direction Sable's chart said to look.

The spire made it bigger. He felt the signal go out the way sound goes into a big room. A little wider. A little deeper.

He waited.

Three seconds. Five.

And then something came back.

---

It was not like Rafe's signal — ragged and cold and closed up tight. This was sharper. Present. Careful. The way a person is careful when they've been watching a door for a very long time.

One beat. Pause. One beat. Pause. Then something like a question — too big for words, more like: *is that what I think it is?*

Ezra answered: *yes.*

The signal from HD 34729 went completely still.

Then it came back — and it felt, without any doubt, like relief.

Ezra opened his eyes. The clearing came back into focus. Sable and Rafe and Julian. Ollie was sitting right where he'd been. Not alerting. Just watching Ezra the way he watched when the fragment was doing something big.

"There's someone there," Ezra said. "They're not trapped. They're—" He looked for the word. "Waiting. Like they expected someone but didn't know when."

"Afraid?" Rafe asked.

"No. Careful. There's a difference."

Julian had the notebook out. "How strong was the signal compared to Bryn Dural?"

"Cleaner. Less tired. Like Rafe was, but—" He thought about it. "Like Rafe if the rockslide had happened two days later. Like someone who is okay, but knows that's not going to last."

Okonkwo had come with them. She was standing to one side, her own notebook in hand. When Ezra stopped talking, she looked at Marcus.

Marcus looked at Wren, who had appeared from the direction of the landing pad at some point nobody had noticed.

Wren said: "I'll have the Ariadne ready in four hours."

---

Packing didn't take long.

This was partly because they had gotten better at it. The first time they'd traveled somewhere fast there had been scrambling and forgotten things and Declan showing up with items no one had asked for.

This time, Julian had a list.

He read it without saying he'd made it: emergency kit, water, fragment chart, training notebook, Sable's pendant (she gave him a look — it was around her neck — he crossed it off without a word), extra clothes, Ollie's food.

"Ollie's food," Mom repeated.

"He's going," Julian said.

"Julian—"

"Ollie goes," Ezra said.

Dad made the face he made when he was choosing not to argue with something that was probably right.

Ollie went.

---

The part nobody talked about was Declan.

He figured it out, obviously. He was two years old and he noticed everything. He stood in the doorway while they were packing. He watched Sable roll up the star chart. He watched Rafe tuck his fragment under his collar. He watched Julian check the emergency kit three times.

Then he looked at Ezra.

"Boo going?" he said.

Ezra crouched down to his level.

"Yeah, buddy. We're going to find someone."

"Lone-ee?"

"Maybe. We'll fix it."

Declan was quiet for a moment. The kind of quiet that meant he was thinking harder than anyone gave him credit for.

Then he reached into his pocket and pulled out a small piece of toast. A little crumpled. It had clearly been there for a while.

He held it up to Ezra.

"For the lone-ee," he said.

Ezra took it. He looked at it — a small, crumpled, slightly-pocket-flavored piece of toast — and did not mention that it wouldn't survive the trip or that it had probably been in that pocket since breakfast. He just put it in his front chest pocket, right over where the star lived.

"I'll give it to them," he said.

Declan nodded. Serious. Satisfied. He patted Ezra's knee twice.

Behind them, Sable had gone very quiet in the way that meant she was pretending she hadn't heard something.

---

The Ariadne lifted off at four in the afternoon.

Monscollis went small below them. The colony, the clearing, the crystal spire catching the late sun and throwing it back in a slow pulse. The forest where they'd trained. All of it small, then smaller.

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest. The star was calm. Present. Ready.

Rafe looked out the window with the face of someone who had left places before and found it different every time. He'd traveled a lot. He knew what it looked like when a place got small.

"Does it get easier?" Ezra asked. "Leaving?"

Rafe thought about it for a real moment.

"You stop worrying about the leaving," he said. "You start paying attention to the going."

Ollie put his head on Ezra's knee and sighed.

Julian, from the seat behind them: "Four hours until we're in signal range. I'm going to sleep."

He was asleep in about ninety seconds.

Sable watched him. "Gifted," she said.

"It's skill," Rafe said seriously. "He told me. You have to commit."

"He told you that."

"First thing this morning. He said sleep is a discipline."

Sable looked at Julian's sleeping face. At his hand still loosely holding the closed notebook. At the completely peaceful look of someone who had decided this was a safe enough place to rest.

"He's not wrong," she said. And she closed her own eyes.

---

Three hours out, the amber fragment pulsed.

Rafe noticed first — he was awake, watching the stars. He put his hand to his chest.

Then Ezra's star answered.

The two pulses found each other, fitting together like they had in the clearing — amber and gold. And under it, from the direction they were heading, something else. A third note. Quiet. Not silver-blue like Sable's. Something new.

Ezra reached over and tapped Sable's knee.

She was awake right away — not startled, just awake, the way she always woke up.

"Hear that?" he said.

She wrapped her hand around her pendant. Went still.

"I feel it," she said. "I can't hear it the way you do. But I feel the change." She paused. "Something's joining the chord."

"The fragment at HD 34729."

"Still three hours away and already reaching. That's—" She stopped.

"Strong?" Rafe said.

"Eager," she said. "Whoever's holding it — they've been wanting to connect for a while."

Ezra looked at the window. Stars. The dark that looked empty but wasn't.

Somewhere out there, someone was awake.

Someone who had been careful and patient and waiting. And who had just, for the first time, gotten an answer.

He reached into his chest pocket and checked the piece of toast. Still there. A little more crumpled.

*For the lone-ee*, Declan had said.

He smiled. The star pulsed warm.

*We're coming.*
