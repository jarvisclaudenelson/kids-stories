# Chapter 12: Yellow Marks

Julian had the star chart spread on the table before anyone else was properly awake.

This was not surprising. What was surprising was that he hadn't woken anyone up to look at it yet. He was sitting with his hands flat on either side of it, just looking, the way he sometimes did with a math problem before he started writing anything down. Like the looking was part of the solving.

Ezra came out of the bedroom and found him there. Ollie followed, nose already going — checking the room, then the window, then settling near the table. Nothing to report.

"You slept," Ezra said.

"Three hours," Julian said, not looking up.

"That's not sleeping. That's a nap."

"It was enough."

Ezra looked at the chart. Sable had left it with them the night before, rolled and set on the table, and Julian had clearly unrolled it the moment the cabin was quiet.

The third yellow mark was closer than the others. Maybe a hand-width on the chart, which didn't mean much without scale.

"Four light-years," Ezra said.

"Three point nine," Julian said. "Approximately."

"How do you know?"

Julian pointed to the edge of the chart, where Sable had penciled in a scale — tiny, almost hidden. He'd been up for however many hours finding it.

"What star system?"

"Unnamed. Survey catalogued but not colonized. There's a designation—" He pointed to a string of letters and numbers beside the mark. "HD 34729. Could be anything."

"Or it could be someone."

Julian looked up. His eyes had the expression they got when he was weighing two things he equally didn't want to say.

"Could be both," he said.

---

Breakfast was eggs that one of the survey team cooks had improved significantly — apparently three weeks of habitat rations made you very motivated to do something interesting with actual eggs. The main hall smelled good for the first time in days.

Rafe came in looking more himself than yesterday. The borrowed clothes still didn't fit, but he'd folded the cuffs differently. He sat, got coffee — actual coffee, not colony concentrate — and held it like someone who had earned it.

Declan arrived at the table from a direction no one had been watching, which was typical. He assessed the eggs. He assessed the coffee pot.

"Hot," he announced.

"Yes," Dad said.

"Decker no hot."

"Also yes."

Declan nodded, satisfied to have established the situation, and went for the eggs instead.

Sable came in a few minutes later, looked at the full table, and sat beside Rafe without any particular announcement. She did that now — arrived and just fit herself in, no fanfare, no calculation. It had taken about a week, which was fast for her.

Marcus came in last, and he sat with the specific posture of someone who had news.

"Okonkwo's been up since four," he said. "She wants to meet this morning. Fletcher gave her something last night — something about timing. She wouldn't say more over the radio."

---

Okonkwo arrived at nine.

She sat at the table the same way she always did — all of it, no part held back. She set her notebook in front of her. She looked at everyone.

"Fletcher says the Shadow Hand is moving up their timeline," she said. "He doesn't know the specific date, but he knows the signal: when they have five fragments, they believe they can force the shattering even without the other two. They won't wait for all seven."

No one spoke.

"They have two," Julian said. "We have three."

"Which leaves two unaccounted for," Okonkwo said. "And the Shadow Hand knows about at least one of the unaccounted locations. Possibly both."

Sable's hand moved to her pendant. "How?"

"Fletcher doesn't know their full network. But he said there's someone inside — not an agent exactly, more like a source — someone who was close to the Covenant before. Before the first fragments were discovered, before any of this. Someone who knows how the power scatters and where it lands." She looked at Sable. "He used the phrase 'pattern reader.'"

Julian sat up straight.

Ezra felt the star in his chest give a short, clear pulse. Not threat-cold. Not warmth exactly. Something that meant: *pay attention to this.*

"The Shadow Hand has someone who understands the fragment pattern," Julian said. He'd opened his notebook without realizing he'd done it. "They're not hunting randomly. They know where to look."

"Or they knew," Sable said. "Past tense might matter."

"Why past tense?"

"If their source was close to the Covenant, the Covenant would have registered that. The fragments might have adjusted. Scattered differently. The pull that brought me here — it didn't go through logic, it went through the fragment itself. If the Shadow Hand's source had that kind of access—"

"Then their map might be wrong," Rafe said.

Sable nodded. "Possibly. Or just behind."

"So we need to get to HD 34729 before they recalculate," Julian said.

He looked at Okonkwo when he said it. Not asking permission. Just presenting the logic.

---

Okonkwo let the silence sit for a moment.

This was a thing she did — let the air settle around a difficult point before she touched it. Ezra had noticed it. She never said something was wrong without giving it time to be heard first.

"The Ariadne can make three point nine light-years," she said. "It's done more. Wren's not on colony assignment until next week, which means she's available." She looked at Marcus.

Marcus made a sound that wasn't quite a word, but meant *I already checked.*

"You'd have three hours at the destination site before the jump window closes," Okonkwo continued. "That's not much time."

"It's enough," Sable said, "if the holder is there."

"And if they're not?"

"Then we come back and figure out what else to try."

Okonkwo looked at Ezra. Not like she was asking him — more like she was checking something.

"Can you reach them?" she said. "The way you reached Rafe."

Ezra thought about the relay station. The quality of the signal from Bryn Dural — that ragged, cold, tired feeling. Whatever was at HD 34729 would be different. He didn't know what shape it would take.

"I can try from the spire," he said. "If the fragment there is awake."

"Then try first. We plan the mission based on what you find."

---

They went to the crystal spire at noon.

It stood at the north end of the colony clearing, taller than any building, pale and translucent in the midday light. It was always doing something at the edge of perception — a faint rhythm, a warmth. Most people had stopped noticing it. Ezra never had.

He stood close. The spire's pulse adjusted, as it always did when he was near — settling into sync with his heartbeat, the way it had done since Chapter 8.

"Same as before," Sable said. She stayed back a step. Rafe stood beside her, watching.

Ezra closed his eyes. He thought of Declan eating eggs this morning with extreme personal focus. He thought of Rafe adjusting his cuffs. He let the star wake up, gently, no reaching.

Then he pushed it outward — slow, patient, the searching-pulse — in the direction Sable's chart said to look.

The spire amplified it. He felt the signal go out the way you feel sound go into a large room: a slight expansion, a reverberation.

He waited.

Three seconds. Five.

And then something came back.

---

It was not like Rafe's signal — ragged and cold and enclosed. This was sharper. Present. Careful, the way a person is careful when they've been watching a doorway for a long time.

One beat. Pause. One beat. Pause. Then a kind of question, too complex for words, more like: *is that what I think it is?*

Ezra answered: *yes.*

The signal from HD 34729 went completely still.

Then it came back with something that felt, unmistakably, like relief.

Ezra opened his eyes. The clearing, the spire, Sable and Rafe and Julian — all of them came back into focus. Ollie was sitting exactly where he'd been, not alerting, just watching Ezra the way he watched Ezra when the fragment was doing something significant.

"There's someone there," Ezra said. "They're not trapped. They're—" He searched for the word. "Waiting. Like they've been expecting someone but didn't know when."

"Afraid?" Rafe asked.

"No. Careful. There's a difference."

Julian had the notebook out. "Signal strength compared to Bryn Dural?"

"Cleaner. Less tired. Like Rafe was, but—" He thought. "Like Rafe if the rockslide had happened two days later instead of two days earlier. Like someone who's been okay, but knows the situation isn't going to stay okay."

Okonkwo had come with them. She was standing to the side, notebook in her own hand, and when Ezra stopped talking, she looked at Marcus.

Marcus looked at Wren, who had appeared from the direction of the landing pad at some point that no one had specifically noticed.

Wren said: "I'll have the Ariadne ready in four hours."

---

Packing did not take long.

This was partly because they had gotten better at it. The first time any of them had traveled somewhere suddenly (the clearing north of the colony, then the relay station, then Bryn Dural) there had been scrambling, forgotten things, Declan appearing with items no one had asked him to retrieve.

This time, Julian had a list.

He read it off without announcing that he'd made it: emergency kit, water, fragment chart, training notebook, Sable's pendant (she gave him a look; it was around her neck; he crossed it off without comment), extra clothes, Ollie's food.

"Ollie's food," Mom repeated.

"He's going," Julian said.

"Julian—"

"Ollie goes," Ezra said.

Dad made the face he made when he was choosing not to argue with something that was probably correct.

Ollie went.

---

The part no one discussed out loud was Declan.

He figured it out, obviously. He was two years old and he noticed everything. He stood in the doorway while they were loading packs and watched Sable coil the star chart and Rafe tuck his fragment under his collar and Julian triple-check something in the emergency kit.

Then he looked at Ezra.

"Boo going?" he said.

Ezra crouched down to his level.

"Yeah, buddy. We're going to find someone."

"Lone-ee?"

"Maybe. We'll fix it."

Declan took this in. He was quiet for a moment — the particular quiet that meant he was running calculations that were more sophisticated than anyone gave him credit for.

Then he reached into his pocket and pulled out a small piece of toast. Slightly crumpled. It had clearly been there for a while.

He held it up to Ezra.

"For the lone-ee," he said.

Ezra took it. He looked at it — a small, crumpled piece of slightly-pocket-toast — and did not point out that it would not survive the journey or that it had probably been in that pocket since breakfast. He just put it in his front chest pocket, right over where the star lived.

"I'll give it to them," he said.

Declan nodded. Serious. Satisfied. He patted Ezra's knee twice.

Behind them, Sable had gone very quiet in the way that meant she was pretending not to have heard something.

---

The Ariadne lifted off at four in the afternoon.

Monscollis went small below them. The colony, the clearing, the crystal spire catching the late sun and throwing it back in a slow pulse. The forest where they'd trained. The relay station on the north ridge. All of it small, then smaller.

Ezra pressed his hand to his chest. The star was calm. Present. Ready.

Rafe was looking at the viewport with the expression of someone who had done this before and found it different every time. He'd traveled a lot, Ezra realized — colony-hopping since he was eight, survey team life, the trip to Bryn Dural and back. He knew what it looked like when a place went small.

"Does it get easier?" Ezra asked. "Leaving?"

Rafe thought about it for a real moment.

"You stop worrying about the leaving," he said. "You start paying attention to the going."

Ollie put his head on Ezra's knee and sighed.

Julian, from the seat behind them: "Four hours until we're in signal range. I'm going to sleep."

He was asleep in approximately ninety seconds.

Sable watched him. "Gifted," she said.

"It's skill," Rafe said seriously. "He told me. You have to commit."

"He told you that."

"First thing this morning. Apparently sleep is a discipline."

Sable looked at Julian's sleeping face. At his hand still loosely holding the closed notebook. At the small, completely uncomplicated peace of someone who had decided this was a safe enough place to rest.

"He's not wrong," she said. And she closed her own eyes.

---

Three hours out, the amber fragment pulsed.

Rafe noticed first — he was awake, watching the stars through the viewport. He put his hand to his chest.

Then Ezra's star answered.

The two pulses found each other, fit together like they had in the clearing — amber and gold, the two-note chord. And under it, from the direction they were traveling, something else. A third note, tentative. Not silver-blue (that was Sable's). Something new.

Ezra reached over and tapped Sable's knee.

She was awake instantly — not startled, just awake, the way she woke up.

"Hear that?" he said.

She wrapped her hand around her pendant. Held still.

"I feel it," she said. "I don't hear it the way you do. But I feel the change." She paused. "The chord. Something's coming into it."

"The fragment at HD 34729."

"Still three hours out and already reaching. That's—" She stopped.

"Strong?" Rafe said.

"Eager," she said. "Whoever's holding it — they've been wanting to connect for a while."

Ezra looked at the viewport. Stars. The particular dark of space that looked empty but wasn't.

Somewhere in that direction, someone was awake.

Someone who had been careful and patient and waiting, and who now — for the first time — had gotten an answer.

He reached into his chest pocket and checked the piece of toast. Still there. A little more crumpled.

*For the lone-ee,* Declan had said.

He smiled. The star pulsed warm.

*We're coming.*
