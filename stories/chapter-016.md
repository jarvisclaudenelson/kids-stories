**Page 1**

Meridian Point wasn't really a point. It was three metal cans bolted together in the middle of nowhere, floating around a star that was too tired to even glow bright. The kind of place people came to when they were between places, between plans, between lives. Wren brought the *Ariadne* in with the kind of docking maneuver that felt like a polite suggestion to the station, nudging the ship into the bay so gently that Declan didn't even wake up from his nap on Ollie's back. She cut the engines and turned around in her pilot's seat to face the group of children who had somehow become her most regular passengers. "Six hours," she said, holding up six fingers for emphasis. "Find your person. Don't touch anything that looks like it belongs to a pirate. And whatever you do, don't drink the coffee. It's been there since the colony was founded."

**Page 2**

Julian was already working. He had been up since the jump started, drawing lines between Monscollis and the transit hub in his notebook, cross-referencing passenger manifests Wren had pulled from the station's public registry. His pen moved in quick, precise strokes across a hand-drawn diagram of the station's three modules. "The signal is strongest in the East module," he said, tapping the page with the tip of his pen. "Seventy-two people are currently registered on the station. If the fragment holder is moving, they'll probably be in the transit lounge waiting for a ship." He looked up, hazel-blue eyes sharp behind a strand of sandy blonde hair. "That's where we start." Sable nodded from behind him, already checking the exits on her own mental map. Julian didn't have a fragment — he was the only one who didn't glow — but nobody in the group planned better, and right now planning was what they needed most.

**Page 3**

Ezra stood by the viewport with his hand flat against his chest. Beneath his orange hoodie, the gold star glowed with a soft, steady warmth that was different from the sharp panic-flares he had felt before finding Rafe or Petra. This wasn't alarm. This was a low, humming greeting, the way you might feel a friend's voice before you hear it across a crowded room. He pressed his palm harder against the warmth and closed his eyes, listening to something the others couldn't hear. The station turned slowly outside the glass, its old metal catching the dim orange starlight. "They're not waiting for a ship," Ezra said quietly, opening his brown eyes. Rafe looked up from turning his amber stone over in his hand. "Waiting for what?" he asked. Ezra turned from the viewport and looked at the group with the calm certainty of a five-year-old who had learned to trust the strange compass in his chest. "Us," he said.

**Page 4**

The station smelled like old oil and recycled air that had been through too many lungs. They walked through the corridors in a tight pack, boots clanging on metal grate floors that showed pipes and wiring underneath. Petra led the way, her violet-white light dimmed to a faint shimmer that made the shadows jump and dance along the curved walls. Sable walked at the back, her eyes scanning every face they passed — engineers with grease-stained hands, freighter pilots nursing cups of terrible coffee, a maintenance bot dragging itself along the ceiling on magnetic treads. She was looking for the Shadow Hand, the people who had already tried to take Rafe and Petra. Every stranger was a potential threat until proven otherwise. "Too many people," Julian whispered, counting faces and marking them against the registry in his notebook. "We need to narrow it down." Ezra didn't hesitate. "I have it," he said, and turned left down a corridor that none of the station signs pointed to, following the golden pull in his chest like a compass needle swinging north.

**Page 5**

The main transit lounge was a cavernous room with mismatched chairs, a food counter that served things that were mostly grey, and the kind of fluorescent lighting that made everyone look slightly unwell. Travelers slumped in clusters — sleeping, arguing, staring at tablets with the hollow patience of people who had been waiting too long. But in the far corner, tucked behind a pillar made of structural pipes, sat a girl. She was about twelve, with dark skin and hair cropped close to her head. She had a pair of massive headphones slung around her neck and a patched tan jacket that looked three sizes too big, the sleeves rolled up past her wrists. She wasn't looking at a map or a tablet or the departure board. She was looking straight at the group of children and the big grey dog walking toward her across the lounge. And on her left wrist, wrapped in a cord of woven wire, was a fragment. It glowed with a warm, yellow-green light that matched the pulse in Ezra's chest beat for beat.

**Page 6**

"Hi," Ezra said, stopping ten feet away the way you might approach a bird you didn't want to scare off. But Noa didn't look scared. She looked like someone who had been expecting a very long-delayed delivery and was frankly a little annoyed it had taken this long. "You took your time," she said. Her name was Noa Vasquez, and she had been at Meridian Point for three weeks, living off protein bars and the credits she earned fixing flight computers for passing pilots. Her mother's ship, the *Kessler*, had been caught in a gravity well four months ago. Noa was the only one who had made it to the escape pod. "The fragment found me in the pod," she said, showing them the wrist-stone. Her voice was steady, matter-of-fact, the voice of someone who had already cried all her tears and decided to be practical instead. "It kept the life support running when the batteries died. It boosted the signal so the rescue beacon could reach farther. It's been boosting things ever since."

**Page 7**

"Boosting?" Julian asked, his pen already poised over a fresh page. Noa looked at the station's main power conduit running along the ceiling, a thick pipe humming with the station's struggling electrical system. She touched her wrist-stone, and the yellow-green light flared like a match catching. Suddenly the dim, sickly fluorescent lights in the lounge turned bright and clear. The grinding hum of the air recyclers smoothed out until they purred like a contented cat. Across the room, a pilot's broken tablet flickered back to life and he nearly fell out of his chair. "I can make things work better," Noa said simply. "I hear the pattern of how machines *should* run, and I give them the push they need to get there." Sable murmured: "The Boost. Fragment Five." Julian wrote it down so fast his pen nearly tore through the paper, his hazel-blue eyes as wide as dinner plates. "That's incredible. We could use that for the spire. We could use that for the *Ariadne*!"

**Page 8**

Noa looked at the group assembled around her table — the tall girl with the scar and the silver pendant, the red-haired boy with the amber stone, the girl with the violet glow and the short-cropped red hair, the seven-year-old furiously scribbling in a notebook, and the five-year-old in the orange hoodie whose chest glowed gold like he had swallowed a small sun. Then her eyes settled on Declan, who had climbed down from Ollie's back and was sitting on the cold metal floor, trying to share a piece of very old hardtack with the dog. Ollie was politely pretending to consider it. "Is he a Guardian too?" Noa asked, pointing at the toddler. "He's the youngest," Ezra said. "And the bravest." Declan looked up, saw the new girl, and did his most important job — the job he had given himself without anyone asking. He reached into the pocket of his green jumpsuit and pulled out the smooth grey rock he had carried all the way from Monscollis. He walked over to Noa's table on his short, determined legs and placed the rock in front of her with both hands. "For you," he said. "Lone-ee no more."

**Page 9**

The return to the *Ariadne* wasn't as quiet. They were twenty yards from the docking bay when three men in dark, reinforced jumpsuits stepped out from behind a stack of cargo crates. They didn't have colony insignias. They had the mark of the Shadow Hand on their shoulders — a black palm printed over a grey sun. The lead man held a specialized containment unit, a sleek black cylinder designed to neutralize fragment energy, and he pointed it at Noa like a weapon. "The girl comes with us," he said. Petra stepped forward without hesitation, her violet light flaring bright enough to make the men shield their eyes. "I don't think so," she said. Rafe didn't say anything at all. He just looked at a heavy cargo pallet ten feet behind the agents, and the pallet groaned, lifted six inches off the ground, and began to drift slowly, inevitably, toward them. Sable's silver-blue pendant pulsed in time with the other fragments, and she stepped shoulder-to-shoulder with Petra. "We have six fragments now," she said. "You really want to do this on a station with bad coffee and no backup?" The men looked at the six glowing children. They looked at the hovering pallet. They looked at Ollie, who was baring every single one of his teeth. They stepped back.

**Page 10**

On the jump back to Monscollis, the *Ariadne* felt like a different ship. Noa sat in the secondary navigator's seat with her hand resting on the console, her wrist-stone glowing steady yellow-green, and the ship didn't just fly — it *hummed*. The vibration of the engines smoothed out until it felt like they were sliding on silk through the stars. The jump-lights didn't flicker. The hull didn't creak. "We're going twenty percent faster," Wren said from the pilot's seat, her eyes fixed on the gauges, and though she didn't look back, Ezra saw her hand tighten on the controls — the quiet tell of a pilot who was deeply impressed and trying not to show it. In the back of the cabin, the six Guardians sat together for the first time as six. Gold, silver-blue, amber, violet-white, yellow-green, and the soft orange glow from Declan's rectangle — the lights from their fragments mixed in the air between them like paint swirling in water, creating colors that didn't have names yet. Noa pulled the smooth grey rock from her jacket pocket and turned it over in her fingers. She looked at the boys and the dog and the older kids who had come all the way across the stars to find her. "I think I like being a Guardian," she said. Ezra smiled. "You're not alone anymore," he told her. And in the warm, humming cabin of the *Ariadne*, with six fragments glowing together and Declan already asleep on Ollie's back, it felt — for the first time — like the light was winning.
