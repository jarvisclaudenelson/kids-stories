# Chapter 16: Meridian Point

Meridian Point was exactly as advertised.

Three prefab modules connected by pressurized corridors, orbiting a star that looked like a star but offered nothing else — no planets worth noting, no interesting debris, nothing you'd come here for except the jump geometry. The kind of place you stopped because the universe had put it between two better places.

The docking port was small and damp with recycled air. There were six ships in the bays. One was enormous and battered, a freight hauler with hull patches the size of houses. One was smaller than the Ariadne. The rest were various sizes of *I've been here a while and I'm not sorry about it*.

"Cozy," Petra said, stepping out of the airlock.

"Functional," Sable said.

"Those aren't opposites," Rafe said.

Wren pulled the Ariadne's ramp up behind them and disappeared back into the cockpit to do things that were probably diagnostics. She had given them a one-line mission brief: *Find your person. Be back in six hours. Don't drink the coffee.*

She had said this last part looking directly at Rafe.

"I wasn't going to," Rafe said.

"You were thinking about it."

He had been.

---

The main module was about sixty meters wide and smelled like three hundred different meals that had been cooked in the same room for a decade without the ventilation catching up. There were tables bolted to the floor, mismatched chairs, a service counter along one wall with a hot beverage situation that validated everything Wren had said.

It was also, for a transit hub at eight in the morning local time, genuinely busy.

People in a dozen different colony jumpsuits. A family with three kids eating something from sealed pouches. Two engineers arguing at a whiteboard mounted on a bulkhead. A man asleep under a coat, his boots crossed on the chair across from him, entirely at peace with this decision.

"Seventy-three people," Julian said, having already completed a rapid count. "How do we find one?"

"The same way we always do," Ezra said.

He put his hand to his chest.

---

The signal was stronger here. Not overwhelming — not the bright certainty of standing next to Rafe or Petra — but present. Warm and directional, like the smell of something familiar in an unfamiliar building.

He turned slowly. The room blurred a little at the edges when he extended his star passively — it had started doing that a few chapters back, Sable said it was fine, he'd gotten used to it.

East. Or what passed for east in a pressurized module orbiting a dim star.

He let his eyes go to the right side of the room.

There was a girl sitting alone at a corner table.

She was maybe eleven or twelve. Dark skin, close-cropped natural hair, enormous headphones around her neck — the good kind, the kind that blocked out everything, currently not doing that job. She was wearing a patched colony jacket three sizes too large, bent over a battered tablet, and she was eating something that looked like a protein bar without making any particular face about it, which meant either she was used to bad food or she wasn't tasting it.

Next to her hand, barely visible under the edge of her jacket sleeve, was a warm yellow-green glow.

Not subtle. Not if you knew what to look for.

Ezra stopped walking.

"There," he said.

---

She noticed them before they reached her table. That was the first thing Ezra registered — she didn't startle, didn't hide the fragment, didn't do anything except look up and assess them with the kind of flat, rapid evaluation that said *I've been assessed by strangers first and I've made a habit of returning the favor.*

She looked at the five of them. She looked at Ollie, who was sitting at a respectful distance with the patient expression of a dog who understood that introductions were a process.

Then she looked at Ezra's chest.

"You're the signal," she said.

"You felt us coming?" he said.

"For three hours." She held up her wrist. The yellow-green stone was looped on a simple cord, warm and alive. "It started going insane when your ship jumped in. Before that it was just—" she made a vague gesture— "enthusiastic."

"Enthusiastic," Petra said.

"It does that thing where it pulses faster when other fragments are close." The girl looked at the group again. "You all have them? How many is that?"

"Four," Julian said. "Plus you."

She did the math. "Five. That's more than half."

"We know," Julian said. He had his notebook out. He had not been asked to produce his notebook; it had simply appeared. "You've had yours for how long?"

The girl looked at the notebook. She looked at Julian. She appeared to be deciding something.

"Who's the seven-year-old with the notebook?" she said.

"My brother," Ezra said. "He's — he tracks things."

"I track things," Julian confirmed.

"His name is Julian," Rafe said. "He grows on you."

Julian looked at Rafe. Rafe looked calmly back. This was a dynamic that had emerged approximately three weeks ago and Julian had not yet decided how he felt about it.

The girl at the table set her protein bar down. She pulled her headphones up and settled them back around her neck with the ease of someone who'd done it ten thousand times.

"Noa," she said. "My name."

"Ezra," said Ezra. "This is Sable, Rafe, Petra, and Julian."

Noa looked at Sable — really looked, the way you look at someone you might need to trust. "You're the oldest. Are you in charge?"

"Depends on the day," Sable said.

Noa seemed to find this acceptable. "Sit down," she said. "This is going to be a long conversation."

---

She'd been on Meridian Point for six weeks, which was not where she'd started.

She had grown up on her mother's cargo ship — the *Kessler*, medium-haul, ran the circuit between four colony systems on a two-year rotation. She'd seen more of the galaxy by the time she was eight than most people saw in a lifetime. She knew how to patch a hull seal, navigate by instrument and by eye, and calculate a jump window in her head because her mother had drilled it into her as a backup to the ship's computer.

She had found the fragment on a stopover at a survey depot fourteen months ago. She had been helping her mother take inventory of the depot's outgoing shipments, and one of the crates had been warm.

Not temperature warm. The other kind.

"I opened it," she said, without particular apology. "I knew I wasn't supposed to. But the warmth was—" she paused, looking for the word — "specific. Like it was warm at me. Not just warm."

"It chose you," Ezra said.

"It was in a sample container from a drill site three systems over. The survey team that packaged it had logged it as 'unusual mineral, warm to the touch, inert.' They had it scheduled for analysis at a central lab." She turned the stone on her wrist. The yellow-green glow shifted with it, steady as a heartbeat. "I put it in my pocket. I wrote 'collected for secondary analysis — E. Vasquez, survey authorization pending' on the manifest line and I never filled in the authorization." She looked up. "The survey team never followed up."

"You stole it," Julian said. His voice was not accusatory. It was interested.

"I rescued it," Noa said. "Someone would have put it in a lab and measured it and eventually given up on it when it didn't behave like regular minerals." She glanced at Ezra. "What would have happened to it?"

"I don't know," Ezra said honestly. "Nothing good, probably."

"That's what I figured."

---

Six months after she'd found the fragment, her mother's ship had taken a contract that meant an eighteen-month detour away from the colony circuits — deep survey run, communications-minimal, bad for a kid who had started hearing a faint pulse from the direction of Monscollis and didn't know what it meant.

She'd gotten off at Meridian Point.

This was stated simply, without drama. She'd said, *Mom, I need to stay*, and her mother had said, *For how long*, and she'd said, *I don't know, but I'll know when it's done*, and her mother had looked at her for a long time and then said, *I'll be back through in two months, you'll comm me every three days.*

She had been here six weeks.

"Your mom left you at a transit hub?" Julian said.

"I asked to stay," Noa said. "There's a difference."

"You're eleven."

"Twelve," Noa said. "And my mom's a cargo pilot. She's been leaving me places since I was eight. Supervised, obviously. I had an arrangement with the hub manager and two months of provisions." She looked untroubled by this. "I've been off-ship alone before."

Julian wrote something down. Ezra did not ask what.

"Your fragment—" Sable started.

"I know about the Shadow Hand," Noa said.

The table went quiet.

She looked around at them — she was good at reading rooms, Ezra could tell; she'd been reading rooms her whole life, moving ship to ship and station to station, the constant recalibration of new places. "They came through here four weeks ago. Two people. One of them asked me about my fragment directly."

"Directly?" Marcus said. He had been quiet in the corner — his default position when situations were still unfolding — but he was fully attentive now.

"They knew what it was," Noa said. "The first one asked if I'd found anything unusual in my cargo surveys. The second one found me alone at this table and told me that the fragment was dangerous if I didn't know how to use it, and that they could help." She looked at her wrist. "I said I didn't know what he was talking about. He didn't believe me." A pause. "My mother taught me to read people, too. He was lying about which part he wanted to help with."

"What did you do?" Petra asked.

Noa shrugged one shoulder. "I moved tables every day for the next two weeks. Transit hub — people come and go. They left after five days. But they'll be back." She said this last part without much fear in it, but with a weight that meant she'd been thinking about it since. "That's when the fragment really started pulling. Before that it was just — a signal. After they came through, it started pulling hard."

She looked at Ezra.

"I think it knew I couldn't stay here much longer," she said. "Not alone."

---

Declan's rock was in Ezra's pocket.

He took it out now and put it on the table between them, smooth and grey, warm from being carried two jumps.

"My brother sent this," he said.

Noa looked at the rock. "He's the two-year-old?"

"Yeah."

"Why did he send me a rock?"

"He sends things to people he thinks need something real," Ezra said. "He's done it before. Toast, usually, but apparently this time he thought a rock was better." He paused. "I don't always understand his logic, but I always think he's right afterward."

Noa picked up the rock. She turned it over in her palm.

"Declan," she said, quietly. Like she was testing the name.

"He'll want to hear you say it when we get back," Ezra said. "Fair warning."

For the first time, she smiled. It changed her whole face — younger, suddenly, less carefully composed.

"Okay," she said.

---

Ollie, who had waited patiently through this entire conversation at a distance, made his assessment at this point.

He walked to Noa's chair, sat down directly in front of her, and looked at her with the full, unhurried attention he gave to things he considered important.

She looked back.

He put one paw on her knee. Very briefly. Then he took it off and looked at Ezra with the expression that meant *handled*.

"Your dog," Noa said.

"He does that," Rafe said. "It means you passed."

"What was the test?"

"I don't know," Rafe said. "Nobody knows. But everyone he's done it to has turned out to be the right person."

She looked at Ollie again. He met her gaze with total dignity.

"I have never had a dog," she said.

"You have one now," Julian said.

Ollie's tail moved once in a slow sweep.

---

They were back at the Ariadne two hours before Wren's deadline.

Noa had packed, which had taken seventeen minutes because she had one bag and had clearly been ready to leave since approximately the second day. She left a comm message for her mother with the hub manager — *Going with people I trust. Monscollis. I'll message from there. I'm okay.* — and walked out without looking back at the table where she'd spent six weeks being careful and patient and alone.

Wren, when introduced, gave Noa the same twelve-word-or-fewer evaluation she gave everything, looked at the fragment on her wrist, and said, "Five of seven. Good."

Noa looked at Wren. "You're not surprised."

"I drove the ship," Wren said.

"Fair," said Noa.

Wren almost smiled. It was, for Wren, practically a celebration.

---

On the jump back to Monscollis, something happened that none of them had a word for yet.

Noa held her wrist next to Ezra's chest, and Rafe held his amber stone up, and Sable drew out the silver-blue pendant, and Petra let her violet-white fragment glow openly in the jump-blue light.

Five fragments in one room.

The sound wasn't actually a sound. It was more like the space between sounds — the pause before a chord resolves. Five different registers, five different colors, pulsing not in unison but in a pattern that felt like something being said in a language that didn't have words.

Noa's yellow-green fragment had been warm and scattered before. Now it was neither. It pulsed with the others in clean, steady rhythm, and the color of it deepened — not brighter, just more itself, the way some things become more clearly what they always were once they're in the right company.

"Is it always like this?" Noa asked, her voice quiet.

"Every time we add one," Sable said. "Each time it's different."

"What does it mean?"

Sable thought for a moment. "I think it means the Covenant is less afraid. Of itself. Of what it might become."

Noa was quiet for a while after that.

"The Shadow Hand has two," she said eventually.

"We know," Ezra said.

"Five and two. If your math is right—" she glanced at Julian — "and assuming your math is right—"

"It is," Julian said.

"—then one fragment is still out there." She turned the stone on her wrist. "And whoever has it is either one of us or one of them."

"One of us," Ezra said.

She looked at him.

"How do you know?"

He thought about it. He thought about the signal on the crystal spire, and the relay amplification, and the way Rafe's amber stone had pulsed toward each new person before they arrived — three hours, six hours, reliable as anything. He thought about Declan, who had said *lone-ee* before anyone else had framed it that way, and who had picked a rock out of forty because this one was right.

"Because it's warm," he said. "The way you were warm. The way Rafe was tired but not cold. The way Petra was careful but not afraid." He put his hand to his chest, where the gold star held its steady heartbeat. "The Shadow Hand's fragments are silent. Ours are — something else. Whatever's out there is something else."

She didn't argue. She turned the yellow-green stone over in her fingers and watched the five colors mix and shift in the jump light.

Outside the viewport, the stars compressed toward Monscollis.

---

Declan was on the dock when they landed.

He had, apparently, decided to be there, and no force known to the colony had successfully explained to him that docks were for authorized personnel. Marcus saw him standing at the end of the ramp before anyone else and made a face that was somewhere between resigned and impressed.

He was holding a piece of toast.

Ezra came down the ramp, and Declan performed the two-knee-pat with full solemnity. Then he looked past Ezra at Noa, who was carrying her bag and looking at the colony for the first time.

Declan studied her with the evaluative focus of a two-year-old who takes his responsibilities seriously.

Then he walked up to her and held out the toast.

She looked at it. She looked at him.

"Toast?" she said.

"Yeah," Declan said. "You Lone-ee."

"Was Lone-ee," Ezra said, from beside her.

Declan considered this correction. He appeared to find it acceptable. He pushed the toast at her more firmly.

She took it. She ate it. She did not make a face.

"Good," Declan said, deeply satisfied.

He looked up at the evening sky — the crystal spire in the distance, already faintly lit with what had become, lately, five colors — and then back at the group assembled at the base of the Ariadne's ramp.

"One more," he said, to no one in particular.

Then he turned and walked back toward the cabin, because dinner was presumably waiting and he had done what he came to do.

Noa watched him go.

"He's two?" she said.

"He's two," Ezra said.

She was quiet for a moment.

"I like him," she said.

The spire pulsed once in the distance, steady and warm, five colors shifting toward six.

*One more.*
